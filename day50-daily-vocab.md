# Day 50 Daily Vocabulary

| Word | Type | Definition | Example 1 | Example 2 | Example 3 |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Diatribe** | Noun | A forceful and bitter verbal attack against someone or something. | The politician launched a long **diatribe** against the government's new policies. | Her article was a scathing **diatribe** directed at the fashion industry. | He had to endure a twenty-minute **diatribe** from his angry supervisor. |
| **Effrontery** | Noun | Insolent or impertinent behavior. | I was shocked by his **effrontery** in asking for a raise after being late every day. | She had the **effrontery** to suggest that the mistake was entirely my fault. | The defendant's **effrontery** during the trial did not sit well with the jury. |
| **Fecund** | Adjective | Producing or capable of producing an abundance of offspring or new growth; fertile. | The lush, **fecund** valley was perfect for agriculture. | She has a **fecund** imagination that allows her to write a book every year. | The artist's most **fecund** period was during his stay in the countryside. |
| **Garrulous** | Adjective | Excessively talkative, especially on trivial matters. | The **garrulous** neighbor kept me at the gate for an hour talking about her cat. | He became quite **garrulous** after having a few drinks at the party. | Her **garrulous** nature made it difficult for others to get a word in. |
| **Harangue** | Noun/Verb | A lengthy and aggressive speech; (verb) to lecture someone at length in an aggressive manner. | The coach delivered a loud **harangue** to the team after their poor performance. | He was **harangued** by the protesters as he entered the building. | I didn't want to listen to another **harangue** about my spending habits. |
| **Iconoclast** | Noun | A person who attacks settled beliefs or institutions. | As an **iconoclast**, he challenged the traditional methods of the scientific community. | The filmmaker was known as an **iconoclast** who broke all the rules of cinema. | She has always been an **iconoclast**, never following the latest trends. |
| **Juxtapose** | Verb | To place or deal with close together for contrasting effect. | The exhibition **juxtaposes** modern art with classical sculptures. | If you **juxtapose** the two photographs, the differences become obvious. | The poet likes to **juxtapose** images of life and death in his work. |
| **Laconic** | Adjective | (Of a person, speech, or style of writing) using very few words. | His **laconic** reply suggested that he wasn't interested in the conversation. | The stoic hero of the movie was known for his **laconic** manner. | She sent a **laconic** text message saying only "No" to the invitation. |
| **Mendacious** | Adjective | Not telling the truth; lying. | The candidate's **mendacious** claims were quickly debunked by the media. | I found his account of the events to be entirely **mendacious**. | She was tired of dealing with her **mendacious** and unreliable business partner. |
| **Nadir** | Noun | The lowest point in the fortunes of a person or organization. | The company reached its **nadir** when its stock price fell to an all-time low. | Failing the exam was the **nadir** of his academic career. | After the scandal, his reputation hit its **nadir**. |
| **Obdurate** | Adjective | Stubbornly refusing to change one's opinion or course of action. | Despite the evidence, he remained **obdurate** in his belief that he was right. | The **obdurate** negotiator refused to make any concessions. | She found it impossible to argue with such an **obdurate** person. |
| **Paucity** | Noun | The presence of something only in small or insufficient quantities or amounts; scarcity. | There is a **paucity** of information regarding the long-term effects of the drug. | The **paucity** of evidence led to the dismissal of the case. | We were forced to cancel the event due to a **paucity** of volunteers. |
| **Quixotic** | Adjective | Exceedingly idealistic; unrealistic and impractical. | His **quixotic** quest to eliminate all poverty in a year was doomed to fail. | She has a **quixotic** notion that she can change the world single-handedly. | It was a **quixotic** project that wasted a lot of time and money. |
| **Recalcitrant** | Adjective | Having an obstinately uncooperative attitude toward authority or discipline. | The **recalcitrant** student was sent to the principal's office for the third time. | The government struggled to manage the **recalcitrant** provinces. | He is a **recalcitrant** employee who refuses to follow any company rules. |
| **Salient** | Adjective | Most noticeable or important. | The **salient** points of the report were summarized in the first two pages. | She pointed out the **salient** features of the new software. | The most **salient** fact is that we are running out of time. |
| **Taciturn** | Adjective | (Of a person) reserved or uncommunicative in speech; saying little. | My father was a **taciturn** man who rarely expressed his emotions. | The **taciturn** clerk merely nodded when I asked for assistance. | He became even more **taciturn** after the loss of his job. |
| **Umbrage** | Noun | Offense or annoyance. | She took **umbrage** at his remarks about her professional abilities. | He was quick to take **umbrage** whenever someone disagreed with him. | I meant no **umbrage** by my comment; it was just an observation. |
| **Vacillate** | Verb | To alternate or waver between different opinions or actions; be indecisive. | I **vacillate** between wanting to go out and wanting to stay home. | The government continued to **vacillate** on the issue of tax reform. | She **vacillated** for so long that the opportunity eventually passed her by. |
| **Winsome** | Adjective | Attractive or appealing in appearance or character. | She gave him a **winsome** smile that immediately melted his heart. | The actor's **winsome** personality made him a favorite among fans. | The cottage had a **winsome** charm that appealed to many buyers. |
| **Zealot** | Noun | A person who is fanatical and uncompromising in pursuit of their religious, political, or other ideals. | The religious **zealot** spent all his time trying to convert others. | He was a political **zealot** who refused to listen to any opposing views. | She is a fitness **zealot** who exercises for four hours every single day. |
