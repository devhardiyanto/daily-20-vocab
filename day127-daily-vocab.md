# Daily 20 Vocabulary - Day 127

| Word | Part of Speech | Meaning | Example 1 | Example 2 | Example 3 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Implacable | Adjective | Unable to be placated or appeased; relentless. | The implacable enemy continued their assault despite heavy losses. | She remained implacable in her demand for a full refund. | The implacable march of time affects us all. |
| Juxtapose | Verb | Place or deal with close together for contrasting effect. | The exhibition juxtaposes modern art with classical sculptures. | By juxtaposing the two characters, the author highlights their differences. | The architect juxtaposed glass and steel to create a striking building. |
| Kinetic | Adjective | Relating to or resulting from motion. | The kinetic energy of the falling object increased as it accelerated. | The dance performance was a kinetic display of athleticism. | The kinetic sculpture moved gracefully in the wind. |
| Languid | Adjective | Displaying or having a disinclination for physical exertion or effort; slow and relaxed. | They spent a languid afternoon lounging by the pool. | His languid movements suggested he was still half asleep. | The languid pace of the movie made it difficult to stay awake. |
| Magnanimous | Adjective | Generous or forgiving, especially toward a rival or less powerful person. | It was magnanimous of him to forgive her for the mistake. | The magnanimous victor praised the efforts of the defeated team. | She made a magnanimous donation to the charity. |
| Nefarious | Adjective | Wicked or criminal. | The nefarious plot to overthrow the government was foiled. | He was known for his nefarious activities in the underworld. | The villain's nefarious scheme involved stealing the city's gold. |
| Obfuscate | Verb | Render obscure, unclear, or unintelligible. | The politician tried to obfuscate the issue with confusing statistics. | The complex legal jargon only served to obfuscate the contract's meaning. | She obfuscated her true intentions behind a veil of politeness. |
| Palliate | Verb | Make (a disease or its symptoms) less severe or unpleasant without removing the cause. | The medication helped to palliate the pain, but it didn't cure the illness. | He tried to palliate his guilt by making a large donation. | The doctor's reassuring words palliated her anxiety. |
| Querulous | Adjective | Complaining in a petulant or whining manner. | The querulous child refused to eat his vegetables. | She became querulous when she didn't get her way. | His querulous tone annoyed everyone in the room. |
| Recalcitrant | Adjective | Having an obstinately uncooperative attitude toward authority or discipline. | The recalcitrant student refused to follow the teacher's instructions. | The recalcitrant horse wouldn't let anyone ride it. | The company faced a recalcitrant workforce demanding better pay. |
| Sagacious | Adjective | Having or showing keen mental discernment and good judgment; shrewd. | The sagacious old man offered wise advice to the young couple. | Her sagacious investments made her a wealthy woman. | The sagacious leader navigated the country through a difficult crisis. |
| Taciturn | Adjective | Reserved or uncommunicative in speech; saying little. | The taciturn cowboy rarely spoke unless spoken to. | His taciturn nature made it difficult to know what he was thinking. | The taciturn witness provided little information to the police. |
| Ubiquitous | Adjective | Present, appearing, or found everywhere. | Smartphones have become ubiquitous in modern society. | The ubiquitous advertising campaign ensured everyone knew about the product. | The ubiquitous presence of security cameras made her feel uneasy. |
| Vacillate | Verb | Alternate or waver between different opinions or actions; be indecisive. | He vacillated between going to college and starting a business. | The committee vacillated for months before making a decision. | She vacillated on whether to accept the job offer. |
| Wanton | Adjective | Deliberate and unprovoked (of a cruel or violent action). | The wanton destruction of property was condemned by the mayor. | He showed a wanton disregard for the safety of others. | The wanton cruelty of the dictator shocked the world. |
| Xenophobia | Noun | Dislike of or prejudice against people from other countries. | The rise of xenophobia in the country led to increased tensions. | The politician's speech was criticized for promoting xenophobia. | Education is key to combating xenophobia and promoting understanding. |
| Yearn | Verb | Have an intense feeling of longing for something, typically something that one has lost or been separated from. | She yearned for the days when she was young and carefree. | He yearned to return to his homeland after years of exile. | The lonely dog yearned for its owner's return. |
| Zealous | Adjective | Having or showing zeal; passionate. | The zealous supporters cheered loudly for their candidate. | She was a zealous advocate for animal rights. | His zealous pursuit of perfection often led to frustration. |
| Alacrity | Noun | Brisk and cheerful readiness. | She accepted the invitation with alacrity. | The team responded to the challenge with alacrity. | He completed the task with surprising alacrity. |
| Bellicose | Adjective | Demonstrating aggression and willingness to fight. | The bellicose nation threatened its neighbors with war. | His bellicose remarks escalated the conflict. | The bellicose tone of the letter alarmed the recipient. |
