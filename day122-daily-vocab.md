# Daily Vocabulary - Day 122

| No. | Word | Part of Speech | Definition | Example 1 | Example 2 | Example 3 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Ameliorate** | Verb | To make something bad or unsatisfactory better. | Efforts to ameliorate the living conditions in the slum were ongoing. | The new policies aim to ameliorate the effects of climate change. | A warm cup of tea can often ameliorate a sore throat. |
| 2 | **Capricious** | Adjective | Given to sudden and unaccountable changes of mood or behavior. | Her capricious nature made it difficult to predict her reactions. | The weather in the mountains is notoriously capricious, changing without warning. | Investors are wary of capricious market fluctuations. |
| 3 | **Delineate** | Verb | To describe or portray something precisely. | The architect delineated the boundaries of the new building on the blueprint. | In her speech, she clearly delineated the responsibilities of each team member. | The artist used sharp lines to delineate the figures in his drawing. |
| 4 | **Ebullient** | Adjective | Cheerful and full of energy. | The ebullient host kept the party lively and entertaining. | Her ebullient personality made her a favorite among her colleagues. | The team was ebullient after their unexpected victory. |
| 5 | **Fervent** | Adjective | Having or displaying a passionate intensity. | He was a fervent supporter of environmental protection. | Her fervent prayers for peace were heard by many. | The politician delivered a fervent speech that resonated with the crowd. |
| 6 | **Gregarious** | Adjective | Fond of company; sociable. | A gregarious person thrives in social gatherings and enjoys meeting new people. | Lions are gregarious animals, living in prides. | His gregarious nature made him an excellent salesperson. |
| 7 | **Hapless** | Adjective | Unfortunate. | The hapless traveler lost his passport and all his money. | The hapless victim of the scam lost all her savings. | Despite his hapless attempts, he never managed to win the lottery. |
| 8 | **Impecunious** | Adjective | Having little or no money. | The impecunious artist struggled to pay his rent. | He came from an impecunious background but worked hard to achieve success. | The charity provides assistance to impecunious families. |
| 9 | **Jubilant** | Adjective | Feeling or expressing great happiness and triumph. | The fans were jubilant after their team won the championship. | She was jubilant when she received the news of her promotion. | The jubilant crowd celebrated the national holiday with fireworks. |
| 10 | **Knell** | Noun | The sound of a bell, especially when rung solemnly for a death or funeral. | The knell of the church bell announced the passing of a beloved member of the community. | The company's bankruptcy sounded the knell for many small businesses. | The final whistle blew, sounding the knell of their hopes for victory. |
| 11 | **Languid** | Adjective | Displaying or having a disinclination for physical exertion or effort; slow and relaxed. | A languid afternoon spent reading in the garden was her idea of perfect relaxation. | The languid movements of the cat suggested it was about to fall asleep. | The hot, humid weather made everyone feel languid and sluggish. |
| 12 | **Mellifluous** | Adjective | (Of a voice or words) sweet or musical; pleasant to hear. | Her mellifluous voice captivated the audience. | The mellifluous tones of the cello filled the concert hall. | He spoke in a mellifluous voice, making even complex topics sound simple. |
| 13 | **Nefarious** | Adjective | (Typically of an action or activity) wicked or criminal. | The nefarious plot to overthrow the government was uncovered. | He was known for his nefarious dealings in the underworld. | The villain's nefarious schemes were always thwarted by the hero. |
| 14 | **Obfuscate** | Verb | To make obscure, unclear, or unintelligible. | The politician tried to obfuscate the truth with vague statements. | Technical jargon can often obfuscate the meaning of a document for a layperson. | He deliberately tried to obfuscate the issue to avoid taking responsibility. |
| 15 | **Pervasive** | Adjective | (Especially of an unwelcome influence or physical effect) spreading widely throughout an area or a group of people. | The pervasive smell of smoke lingered in the air after the fire. | Social media has a pervasive influence on modern culture. | The pervasive feeling of anxiety affected everyone during the crisis. |
| 16 | **Quell** | Verb | To put an end to (a rebellion or other disorder), typically by the use of force. | The police were called in to quell the riot. | She tried to quell her rising panic before giving the presentation. | The government took swift action to quell the unrest. |
| 17 | **Recalcitrant** | Adjective | Having an obstinately uncooperative attitude toward authority or discipline. | The recalcitrant student refused to follow the teacher's instructions. | The manager faced a challenge in dealing with the recalcitrant employees. | Despite repeated attempts, the recalcitrant donkey refused to move. |
| 18 | **Salient** | Adjective | Most noticeable or important. | The salient features of the new design were its simplicity and elegance. | He highlighted the salient points of the report during his presentation. | One of the salient differences between the two cultures is their approach to time. |
| 19 | **Tenebrous** | Adjective | Dark; shadowy or obscure. | The old castle had a tenebrous atmosphere, filled with secrets and history. | The tenebrous corners of the room seemed to hide untold mysteries. | His writing often explored the tenebrous aspects of human nature. |
| 20 | **Ubiquitous** | Adjective | Present, appearing, or found everywhere. | Mobile phones are now ubiquitous, found in nearly every pocket. | The brand's logo is ubiquitous, appearing on billboards and TV ads alike. | In the digital age, data is ubiquitous, generated by every click and interaction. |
