# Daily Vocabulary - Day 87

| Word | Definition | Example 1 | Example 2 | Example 3 |
| :----------- | :------------------------------------------- | :--------------------------------------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Unprecedented** | Never done or experienced before; without previous example. | The pandemic caused an **unprecedented** shift to online learning. | The company achieved **unprecedented** growth this quarter. | Her success at such a young age was **unprecedented**. |
| **Adept** | Highly skilled or proficient at something. | She’s **adept** at explaining complex grammar in a simple way. | He is **adept** at problem-solving. | The magician was **adept** at sleight of hand. |
| **Dwindle** | To gradually decrease or become smaller. | Attendance at the evening class **dwindled** after the holidays. | His hopes began to **dwindle** as time ran out. | The supplies began to **dwindle** rapidly. |
| **Mitigate** | To make something less severe, harmful, or painful. | Wearing a seatbelt can **mitigate** the risk of injury in an accident. | They are trying to **mitigate** the effects of climate change. | The new policies aim to **mitigate** poverty. |
| **Plethora** | A large or excessive amount of something. | Espresso English offers a **plethora** of lessons, courses, and e-books. | The internet provides a **plethora** of information. | There was a **plethora** of choices at the buffet. |
| **Ambiguous** | Unclear; open to multiple interpretations. | The instructions were **ambiguous**, so no one knew what to do. | His response was deliberately **ambiguous**. | The ending of the movie was **ambiguous**. |
| **Scrutinize** | To examine very carefully. | Investors will **scrutinize** the company’s financial statements. | The detective began to **scrutinize** the crime scene. | She **scrutinized** his face for any sign of deception. |
| **Convey** | To communicate or express. | It’s hard to **convey** sarcasm over text. | Words often fail to **convey** the depth of emotion. | The painting effectively **conveys** a sense of peace. |
| **Candid** | Honest and direct, even when uncomfortable. | She gave a **candid** answer about her experience with failure. | He offered a **candid** assessment of the situation. | To be **candid**, I don't think that's a good idea. |
| **Omit** | To leave out. | You can **omit** that paragraph — it’s not essential. | Please **omit** any sensitive information from the report. | He decided to **omit** the last chapter from his book. |
| **Caliber** | Level of quality or ability. | They’re hiring someone of the highest **caliber** for the role. | The orchestra attracted musicians of exceptional **caliber**. | Her work is always of a high **caliber**. |
| **Prevalent** | Widespread or commonly occurring. | Work-from-home policies have become more **prevalent**. | The belief in superstitions is still **prevalent** in some cultures. | This disease is more **prevalent** in tropical regions. |
| **Nuance** | A subtle or slight difference in meaning or tone. | His performance captured every **nuance** of the character. | She understood the subtle **nuances** of the language. | The artist paid attention to every **nuance** of color. |
| **Detrimental** | Harmful or damaging. | Smoking is clearly **detrimental** to your health. | Lack of exercise can be **detrimental** to your well-being. | The decision proved **detrimental** to the company's reputation. |
| **Discrepancy** | A difference between two things that should match. | There’s a **discrepancy** between the reports and the receipts. | The audit revealed a significant **discrepancy** in the accounts. | We need to investigate the **discrepancy** in the data. |
| **Pivotal** | Critically important or central. | That meeting was a **pivotal** moment in the negotiation. | The invention of the printing press was a **pivotal** event in history. | His role in the project was absolutely **pivotal**. |
| **Refute** | To prove something is false or incorrect. | She **refuted** the claim with solid evidence. | It is difficult to **refute** such strong arguments. | He tried to **refute** the accusations against him. |
| **Resilient** | Able to recover quickly from difficulties; strong and adaptable. | Despite many setbacks, she remained **resilient** and kept working toward her goal. | The community proved **resilient** in the face of disaster. | Children are often more **resilient** than adults. |
| **Unleash** | To release something powerful or intense. | The coach’s speech **unleashed** a wave of motivation in the team. | The storm **unleashed** its fury on the coastal town. | The new technology will **unleash** a wave of innovation. |
| **Pinnacle** | The highest or most successful point of something. | Winning the championship was the **pinnacle** of his career. | He reached the **pinnacle** of his profession. | The mountain peak was the **pinnacle** of their climb. |
