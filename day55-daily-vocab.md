| Word | Type | Definition | Example 1 | Example 2 | Example 3 |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Iconoclast** | Noun | A person who attacks cherished beliefs or established institutions. | As an **iconoclast**, he challenged the traditional methods of the industry. | The filmmaker was known as an **iconoclast** for his unconventional storytelling. | She has always been an **iconoclast**, never following the crowd. |
| **Juxtapose** | Verb | Place or deal with close together for contrasting effect. | The exhibition **juxtaposes** modern art with classical sculptures. | The poet **juxtaposes** images of war and peace to create a powerful message. | If you **juxtapose** the two photos, the differences become clear. |
| **Kindle** | Verb | Light or set on fire; arouse or inspire (an emotion or feeling). | The speaker's words helped to **kindle** a sense of hope in the audience. | He used small twigs to **kindle** the campfire. | Her passion for music was **kindled** at a very young age. |
| **Loquacious** | Adjective | Tending to talk a great deal; talkative. | The **loquacious** host kept the guests entertained all evening. | He became more **loquacious** after a few cups of coffee. | She is known for being **loquacious**, often sharing long stories. |
| **Magnanimous** | Adjective | Generous or forgiving, especially toward a rival or less powerful person. | He was **magnanimous** in victory, praising his opponent's efforts. | The company made a **magnanimous** donation to the local charity. | She showed a **magnanimous** spirit by forgiving her friend's mistake. |
| **Nefarious** | Adjective | (Typically of an action or activity) wicked, impious, or criminal. | The detective uncovered a **nefarious** plot to steal the crown jewels. | He was known for his **nefarious** activities in the underworld. | The villain's **nefarious** plan was eventually thwarted by the hero. |
| **Obsequious** | Adjective | Obedient or attentive to an excessive or servile degree. | The **obsequious** assistant was always ready to fulfill his boss's every whim. | He didn't like the **obsequious** behavior of the waiters at the fancy restaurant. | She found his **obsequious** compliments to be insincere. |
| **Pensive** | Adjective | Engaged in, involving, or reflecting deep or serious thought. | She sat by the window, looking **pensive** as she watched the rain. | He fell into a **pensive** mood after reading the letter. | The artist had a **pensive** expression while working on his masterpiece. |
| **Quintessential** | Adjective | Representing the most perfect or typical example of a quality or class. | It was the **quintessential** small town, with its main street and friendly neighbors. | He is the **quintessential** gentleman, always polite and helpful. | This dish is the **quintessential** example of Italian cuisine. |
| **Reticent** | Adjective | Not revealing one's thoughts or feelings readily. | He was **reticent** about his past, rarely speaking of his childhood. | She is a **reticent** person who prefers to listen rather than talk. | The witness remained **reticent** during the questioning. |
| **Sycophant** | Noun | A person who acts obsequiously toward someone important in order to gain advantage. | He was surrounded by **sycophants** who only told him what he wanted to hear. | The manager could see through the **sycophant's** false flattery. | She refused to be a **sycophant**, even if it meant losing her job. |
| **Taciturn** | Adjective | (Of a person) reserved or uncommunicative in speech; saying little. | My grandfather was a **taciturn** man who rarely spoke more than a few words. | He became **taciturn** and withdrawn after the accident. | The **taciturn** driver didn't say a word during the entire trip. |
| **Ubiquitous** | Adjective | Present, appearing, or found everywhere. | Smartphones have become **ubiquitous** in modern society. | The company's logo is **ubiquitous**, appearing on billboards and TV ads. | Coffee shops are **ubiquitous** in this part of the city. |
| **Venerate** | Verb | Regard with great respect; revere. | Many people **venerate** the saint for her life of service. | The culture **venerates** its elders for their wisdom and experience. | He is **venerated** as one of the greatest scientists of all time. |
| **Wary** | Adjective | Feeling or showing caution about possible dangers or problems. | He was **wary** of the stranger's intentions. | She gave him a **wary** look when he offered to help. | Be **wary** of deals that seem too good to be true. |
| **Xenophobia** | Noun | Dislike of or prejudice against people from other countries. | The government's policies were criticized for promoting **xenophobia**. | Education is key to combating **xenophobia** and intolerance. | He spoke out against the rising tide of **xenophobia** in the country. |

| **Zenith** | Noun | The time at which something is most powerful or successful. | The empire reached its **zenith** during the reign of the great emperor. | He was at the **zenith** of his career when he decided to retire. | The sun reached its **zenith** at noon, shining brightly overhead. |
| **Abstain** | Verb | Restrain oneself from doing or enjoying something. | He decided to **abstain** from alcohol for a month. | She chose to **abstain** from voting in the local election. | The doctor advised him to **abstain** from heavy exercise for a few days. |
| **Belligerent** | Adjective | Hostile and aggressive. | The **belligerent** customer started shouting at the store manager. | He has a **belligerent** attitude that often gets him into trouble. | The two countries have been in a **belligerent** state for years. |
