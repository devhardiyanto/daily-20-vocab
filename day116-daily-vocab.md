# Day 116: Daily English Vocabulary

| No. | Word | Part of Speech | Definition | Example 1 | Example 2 | Example 3 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Ubiquitous** | Adjective | Present, appearing, or found everywhere. | Smartphones have become ubiquitous in modern society. | The company aims to make its product ubiquitous in every household. | His ubiquitous presence at social events made him a well-known figure. |
| 2 | **Ephemeral** | Adjective | Lasting for a very short time. | The beauty of a sunset is ephemeral, fading quickly into twilight. | Fashion trends are often ephemeral, changing with each season. | Her happiness was ephemeral, quickly replaced by sadness. |
| 3 | **Cacophony** | Noun | A harsh, discordant mixture of sounds. | The cacophony of car horns and sirens filled the city streets. | The orchestra produced a cacophony of noise during their warm-up. | He couldn't concentrate with the cacophony of voices in the room. |
| 4 | **Benevolent** | Adjective | Well meaning and kindly. | The benevolent old woman always offered help to those in need. | His benevolent smile put everyone at ease. | The foundation was known for its benevolent acts of charity. |
| 5 | **Mellifluous** | Adjective | (Of a voice or words) sweet or musical; pleasant to hear. | Her mellifluous voice captivated the audience during the performance. | The mellifluous sound of the cello filled the concert hall. | He spoke in a mellifluous tone, making his words sound like music. |
| 6 | **Profligate** | Adjective | Recklessly extravagant or wasteful in the use of resources. | His profligate spending habits led him to bankruptcy. | The government was criticized for its profligate use of public funds. | She warned him against his profligate lifestyle. |
| 7 | **Sanguine** | Adjective | Optimistic or positive, especially in an apparently difficult situation. | Despite the challenges, he remained sanguine about the future of the project. | Her sanguine outlook on life was infectious, inspiring those around her. | He was sanguine about his chances of winning the competition. |
| 8 | **Querulous** | Adjective | Complaining in a petulant or whining manner. | The querulous child constantly whined about everything. | Her querulous tone made it difficult to have a calm conversation. | He grew tired of his neighbor's querulous complaints. |
| 9 | **Pernicious** | Adjective | Having a harmful effect, especially in a gradual or subtle way. | The pernicious effects of pollution on the environment are undeniable. | She warned him about the pernicious influence of bad company. | The pernicious rumor spread quickly, damaging his reputation. |
| 10 | **Obfuscate** | Verb | Render obscure, unclear, or unintelligible. | The politician tried to obfuscate the truth with vague statements. | His complex explanation only served to obfuscate the issue further. | They deliberately obfuscated the data to mislead investors. |
| 11 | **Luminous** | Adjective | Emitting or reflecting light; shining. | The full moon cast a luminous glow over the tranquil lake. | Her luminous eyes sparkled with joy and excitement. | The artist used luminous colors to create a vibrant painting. |
| 12 | **Kudos** | Noun | Praise and honor received for an achievement. | He received kudos from his colleagues for his outstanding presentation. | The team deserved kudos for their hard work and dedication. | She was showered with kudos after winning the prestigious award. |
| 13 | **Jubilant** | Adjective | Feeling or expressing great happiness and triumph. | The crowd was jubilant after their team won the championship. | She felt jubilant after receiving the news of her promotion. | The jubilant celebration lasted late into the night. |
| 14 | **Inchoate** | Adjective | Not yet organized or fully formed; rudimentary. | The inchoate idea for a novel slowly began to take shape in her mind. | The project was still in its inchoate stages, with many details yet to be decided. | His inchoate thoughts struggled to find expression in words. |
| 15 | **Hegemony** | Noun | Leadership or dominance, especially by one state or social group over others. | The country sought to establish its hegemony over the region. | The cultural hegemony of Hollywood films is evident worldwide. | The struggle for economic hegemony continues among global powers. |
| 16 | **Gregarious** | Adjective | (Of a person) fond of company; sociable. | She was a gregarious person, always surrounded by friends and acquaintances. | His gregarious nature made him a popular figure at parties. | The gregarious species of birds often flock together in large groups. |
| 17 | **Fatuous** | Adjective | Silly and pointless. | His fatuous remarks during the serious discussion were out of place. | The fatuous argument led nowhere and wasted everyone's time. | She found his fatuous attempts at humor rather annoying. |
| 18 | **Ebullient** | Adjective | Cheerful and full of energy. | Her ebullient personality lit up every room she entered. | The ebullient crowd cheered enthusiastically for their favorite band. | He was in an ebullient mood after receiving good news. |
| 19 | **Delineate** | Verb | Describe or portray (something) precisely. | The architect carefully delineated the boundaries of the new building. | The report clearly delineated the responsibilities of each team member. | She tried to delineate her thoughts and feelings in a coherent manner. |
| 20 | **Cajole** | Verb | Persuade someone to do something by sustained coaxing or flattery. | He tried to cajole her into agreeing to his plan with compliments and promises. | She managed to cajole her parents into letting her go to the concert. | The salesperson attempted to cajole the customer into buying the expensive car. |
