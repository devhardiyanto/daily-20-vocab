| **Soporific** | Adjective | Tending to induce drowsiness or sleep. | The professor's monotone voice had a **soporific** effect on the entire class. | Some herbal teas are known for their **soporific** properties, helping people fall asleep. | The long, rhythmic motion of the train was quite **soporific**. |
| **Ephemeral** | Adjective | Lasting for a very short time. | The beauty of the sunset was **ephemeral**, fading into darkness within minutes. | Social media trends are often **ephemeral**, being replaced by something new every week. | Fame can be **ephemeral**, so it is important to stay grounded. |
| **Laconic** | Adjective | (Of a person, speech, or style of writing) using very few words. | His **laconic** reply suggested that he wasn't interested in continuing the conversation. | The stoic cowboy was known for his **laconic** manner of speaking. | She sent a **laconic** text message simply saying "Yes" to the proposal. |
| **Mitigate** | Verb | Make less severe, serious, or painful. | The government implemented new policies to **mitigate** the effects of the economic crisis. | Planting more trees can help **mitigate** the impact of climate change in urban areas. | Taking aspirin can help **mitigate** the pain of a headache. |
| **Propriety** | Noun | The state or quality of conforming to conventionally accepted standards of behavior or morals. | She always behaved with the utmost **propriety**, even in difficult social situations. | There were questions about the **propriety** of the official's involvement in the deal. | He was careful to observe the **proprieties** of the formal dinner. |
| **Cacophony** | Noun | A harsh, discordant mixture of sounds. | The **cacophony** of car horns and sirens in the city made it hard to concentrate. | As the orchestra tuned their instruments, a brief **cacophony** filled the hall. | The classroom was a **cacophony** of shouting children and scraping chairs. |
| **Ingenuous** | Adjective | (Of a person or action) innocent and unsuspecting. | Her **ingenuous** nature made her believe everything people told her. | He gave an **ingenuous** explanation for why he was late, which everyone believed. | It was rather **ingenuous** of him to think that the problem would solve itself. |
| **Misanthrope** | Noun | A person who dislikes humankind and avoids human society. | The old hermit was a known **misanthrope** who lived alone in the mountains. | His cynical comments about society earned him a reputation as a **misanthrope**. | Scrooge is perhaps the most famous **misanthrope** in English literature. |

| **Venerate** | Verb | Regard with great respect; revere. | Many cultures **venerate** their ancestors through special rituals and ceremonies. | The scientist is **venerated** for his groundbreaking contributions to physics. | People from all over the world come to **venerate** the sacred site. |
| **Antipathy** | Noun | A deep-seated feeling of dislike; aversion. | There has always been a strong **antipathy** between the two rival sports teams. | She made no attempt to hide her **antipathy** toward the new management. | His **antipathy** to public speaking made him turn down the invitation. |
| **Deride** | Verb | Express contempt for; ridicule. | Critics were quick to **deride** the director's latest film as a complete failure. | It is unkind to **deride** someone for making a simple mistake. | He was **derided** by his peers for his unconventional ideas. |
| **Eulogy** | Noun | A speech or piece of writing that praises someone or something highly, typically someone who has just died. | His best friend delivered a moving **eulogy** at the funeral service. | The book is a beautiful **eulogy** to a way of life that is fast disappearing. | The retired general received a glowing **eulogy** for his years of service. |
| **Lethargic** | Adjective | Affected by lethargy; sluggish and apathetic. | The hot weather made everyone feel **lethargic** and unwilling to move. | After the heavy meal, I felt too **lethargic** to do any work. | The patient remained **lethargic** for several days after the surgery. |
| **Obsequious** | Adjective | Obedient or attentive to an excessive or servile degree. | The **obsequious** waiter hovered over the table, constantly refilling their glasses. | He was surrounded by **obsequious** assistants who agreed with everything he said. | Her **obsequious** behavior toward the boss was seen as insincere by her colleagues. |
| **Philanthropic** | Adjective | (Of a person or organization) seeking to promote the welfare of others, especially by donating money to good causes. | The billionaire is known for his **philanthropic** work in education and healthcare. | The foundation provides **philanthropic** support to various environmental projects. | She decided to dedicate her life to **philanthropic** endeavors. |

| **Bolster** | Verb | Support or strengthen; prop up. | The new evidence helped to **bolster** the prosecutor's case against the defendant. | He took a public speaking course to **bolster** his confidence. | The government introduced subsidies to **bolster** the local agricultural industry. |
| **Dissonance** | Noun | A lack of harmony among musical notes; a lack of agreement or consistency. | The **dissonance** in the modern music piece was intentional and striking. | There is a clear **dissonance** between what the politician says and what he does. | Cognitive **dissonance** occurs when a person holds two conflicting beliefs. |
