# Daily Vocabulary - Day 25

This list contains 20 advanced English vocabulary words, each accompanied by its definition and three contextual example sentences to aid in comprehension and usage.

| Word | Part of Speech | Definition | Contextual Examples |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Ephemeral** | Adjective | Lasting for a very short time. | 1. The beauty of the cherry blossoms is **ephemeral**, lasting only a few days. <br> 2. He realized that his fame was **ephemeral** and would soon fade away. <br> 3. The artist captured the **ephemeral** quality of the morning mist in his painting. |
| **Ubiquitous** | Adjective | Present, appearing, or found everywhere. | 1. In the modern world, smartphones have become truly **ubiquitous**. <br> 2. The company's logo is now **ubiquitous** across all forms of media. <br> 3. The smell of freshly baked bread was **ubiquitous** throughout the market. |
| **Mellifluous** | Adjective | (Of a voice or words) sweet or musical; pleasant to hear. | 1. The singer's **mellifluous** voice captivated the entire audience. <br> 2. She spoke in a **mellifluous** tone, which made her a natural storyteller. <br> 3. The sound of the harp was a **mellifluous** backdrop to the evening. |
| **Pernicious** | Adjective | Having a harmful effect, especially in a gradual or subtle way. | 1. The **pernicious** effects of the drug were not immediately apparent. <br> 2. He warned against the **pernicious** influence of gossip in the workplace. <br> 3. The slow, **pernicious** erosion of civil liberties is a serious concern. |
| **Cacophony** | Noun | A harsh, discordant mixture of sounds. | 1. The traffic jam created a **cacophony** of horns and shouting. <br> 2. The orchestra's warm-up session was a brief **cacophony** before the conductor arrived. <br> 3. He covered his ears to block out the **cacophony** from the construction site. |
| **Ebullient** | Adjective | Cheerful and full of energy. | 1. The winner was in an **ebullient** mood after receiving the award. <br> 2. Her **ebullient** personality makes her the life of every party. <br> 3. The team celebrated their victory with **ebullient** cheers and high-fives. |
| **Surreptitious** | Adjective | Kept secret, especially because it would not be approved of. | 1. They held a **surreptitious** meeting in the back room of the cafe. <br> 2. He cast a **surreptitious** glance at his watch during the long lecture. <br> 3. The spy made a **surreptitious** exchange of documents under the table. |
| **Capricious** | Adjective | Given to sudden and unaccountable changes of mood or behavior. | 1. The weather in the mountains is notoriously **capricious**. <br> 2. Her **capricious** spending habits often left her short of money. <br> 3. A **capricious** decision by the manager led to an unexpected change in policy. |
| **Ineffable** | Adjective | Too great or extreme to be expressed or described in words. | 1. The beauty of the Grand Canyon is almost **ineffable**. <br> 2. He experienced a moment of **ineffable** joy upon seeing his newborn child. <br> 3. The mystic spoke of an **ineffable** truth that transcended human language. |
| **Lugubrious** | Adjective | Looking or sounding sad and dismal. | 1. The dog gave a **lugubrious** howl when its owner left the house. <br> 2. He delivered the bad news in a **lugubrious** and mournful voice. <br> 3. The novel's main character was a **lugubrious** figure who rarely smiled. |
| **Fastidious** | Adjective | Very attentive to and concerned about accuracy and detail. | 1. She is a **fastidious** editor who catches every tiny grammatical error. <br> 2. The chef was **fastidious** about the presentation of every dish. <br> 3. Maintaining a **fastidious** record of all transactions is crucial for the business. |
| **Gregarious** | Adjective | (Of a person) fond of company; sociable. | 1. As a naturally **gregarious** person, he quickly made friends in the new city. <br> 2. Lions are not solitary animals; they are **gregarious** and live in prides. <br> 3. The convention was a great place for **gregarious** individuals to network. |
| **Querulous** | Adjective | Complaining in a petulant or whining manner. | 1. The child became **querulous** every time he was denied a snack. <br> 2. His constant, **querulous** remarks about the service annoyed the other diners. <br> 3. The old man's **querulous** voice echoed through the quiet hospital ward. |
| **Sycophant** | Noun | A person who acts obsequiously toward someone important in order to gain advantage. | 1. The manager was surrounded by **sycophants** who constantly praised his every decision. <br> 2. She was tired of the **sycophant** who tried to win favor by excessive flattery. <br> 3. No one respects a **sycophant**; genuine praise is always more valued. |
| **Vacillate** | Verb | Alternate or waver between different opinions or actions; be indecisive. | 1. He tends to **vacillate** between two career paths, unable to make a final choice. <br> 2. The committee continued to **vacillate** on the budget proposal for weeks. <br> 3. When faced with a difficult decision, it is natural to **vacillate** for a time. |
| **Parsimonious** | Adjective | Extremely unwilling to spend money or use resources; stingy or frugal. | 1. The company was so **parsimonious** that it refused to buy new office supplies. <br> 2. A **parsimonious** approach to budgeting can sometimes lead to long-term savings. <br> 3. Despite his wealth, he lived a surprisingly **parsimonious** life. |
| **Misanthrope** | Noun | A person who dislikes humankind and avoids human society. | 1. After years of disappointment, the old man became a bitter **misanthrope**. <br> 2. The protagonist of the novel is a wealthy **misanthrope** who lives in isolation. <br> 3. Despite his reputation as a **misanthrope**, he secretly donated to several charities. |
| **Alacrity** | Noun | Brisk and cheerful readiness. | 1. She accepted the new project with **alacrity** and enthusiasm. <br> 2. The waiter served the meal with impressive **alacrity**. <br> 3. The students responded to the call for volunteers with great **alacrity**. |
| **Obfuscate** | Verb | Render obscure, unclear, or unintelligible. | 1. The politician tried to **obfuscate** the issue with vague and confusing language. <br> 2. Technical jargon can often **obfuscate** the meaning for a general audience. <br> 3. The company's goal was to clarify, not to **obfuscate**, the financial reports. |
| **Salient** | Adjective | Most noticeable or important. | 1. The **salient** feature of the new design is its improved energy efficiency. <br> 2. He summarized the **salient** points of the long report in a brief memo. <br> 3. One of the most **salient** differences between the two cultures is their approach to time. |
