# Day 75: Daily English Vocabulary

| Word | Part of Speech | Definition | Example 1 | Example 2 | Example 3 |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Panache** | Noun | Flamboyant confidence of style or manner. | He entertained his guests with great **panache**, making everyone feel welcome. | The violinist performed the difficult concerto with remarkable **panache**. | She has a certain **panache** that makes her stand out in any crowd. |
| **Paradigm** | Noun | A typical example or pattern of something; a model. | The discovery led to a major **paradigm** shift in the field of physics. | Her successful business model became a **paradigm** for other startups. | The school's new curriculum represents a new **paradigm** in education. |
| **Paragon** | Noun | A person or thing viewed as a model of excellence. | He was considered a **paragon** of virtue and integrity by his peers. | The new library is a **paragon** of modern architectural design. | She is often cited as a **paragon** of professional dedication. |
| **Pariah** | Noun | An outcast. | After the scandal, he became a social **pariah** in his small hometown. | The company was treated as a **pariah** after its unethical practices were exposed. | No one deserves to be treated like a **pariah**, regardless of their past. |
| **Parsimony** | Noun | Extreme unwillingness to spend money or use resources. | His **parsimony** was well-known; he rarely treated himself to anything new. | The project failed due to the **parsimony** of the initial funding. | Despite her wealth, she lived a life of extreme **parsimony**. |
| **Paucity** | Noun | The presence of something only in small or insufficient quantities or amounts; scarcity. | There is a **paucity** of evidence to support the prosecutor's claims. | The researchers complained about the **paucity** of data available for the study. | A **paucity** of resources forced the team to scale back the project. |
| **Peccadillo** | Noun | A small, relatively unimportant offense or sin. | The politician's minor **peccadilloes** were overlooked in light of his achievements. | We all have our little **peccadilloes** that make us human. | It was a mere **peccadillo**, but he still felt guilty about it. |
| **Pedantic** | Adjective | Excessively concerned with minor details and rules or with displaying academic learning. | His **pedantic** approach to teaching often bored his students. | She can be quite **pedantic** when it comes to grammar and punctuation. | The meeting was delayed by a **pedantic** discussion over formatting. |
| **Pellucid** | Adjective | Translucently clear; easily understood. | The mountain stream was so **pellucid** that you could see every pebble on the bottom. | He is famous for his **pellucid** writing style that simplifies complex ideas. | The explanation was **pellucid**, leaving no room for confusion. |
| **Penchant** | Noun | A strong or habitual liking for something or tendency to do something. | He has a **penchant** for adopting stray animals and giving them a home. | Her **penchant** for high-risk investments often worried her financial advisor. | Most children have a **penchant** for sweets and sugary snacks. |
| **Penurious** | Adjective | Extremely poor; poverty-stricken; or parsimonious. | The family lived in **penurious** conditions after the factory closed down. | He was known for his **penurious** habits, even when he could afford more. | The **penurious** state of the school's facilities was a cause for concern. |
| **Perennial** | Adjective | Lasting or existing for a long or apparently infinite time; enduring or continually recurring. | The shortage of affordable housing is a **perennial** problem in the city. | She is a **perennial** favorite among the fans of the local theater. | These flowers are **perennials**, meaning they will bloom again next year. |
| **Perfidy** | Noun | Deceitfulness; untrustworthiness. | The king was shocked by the **perfidy** of his most trusted advisor. | Their friendship ended abruptly after an act of unexpected **perfidy**. | The history books are full of tales of political **perfidy** and betrayal. |
| **Perfunctory** | Adjective | (of an action or gesture) carried out with a minimum of effort or reflection. | He gave a **perfunctory** nod to his colleagues as he walked past. | The security check was **perfunctory**, barely glancing at the IDs. | She completed the task in a **perfunctory** manner, showing little interest. |
| **Peripatetic** | Adjective | Traveling from place to place, especially working or based in various places for relatively short periods. | He led a **peripatetic** life, moving from city to city for his research. | The **peripatetic** nature of her job allowed her to see much of the world. | Many ancient philosophers were **peripatetic**, teaching as they traveled. |
| **Pernicious** | Adjective | Having a harmful effect, especially in a gradual or subtle way. | The **pernicious** influence of social media on self-esteem is well-documented. | High inflation can have a **pernicious** effect on the economy over time. | Rumors can be **pernicious**, destroying reputations before they can be refuted. |
| **Perspicacious** | Adjective | Having a ready insight into and understanding of things. | The **perspicacious** investor recognized the potential of the startup early on. | Her **perspicacious** observations helped the team avoid several pitfalls. | He is known for his **perspicacious** analysis of current political events. |
| **Pervasive** | Adjective | (especially of an unwelcome influence or physical effect) spreading widely throughout an area or a group of people. | The smell of smoke was **pervasive** throughout the entire building. | Corruption was **pervasive** in the local government for many years. | Technology has become a **pervasive** part of our daily lives. |
| **Petulant** | Adjective | (of a person or their manner) childishly sulky or bad-tempered. | He threw a **petulant** fit when he didn't get his way during the meeting. | Her **petulant** behavior made it difficult for others to work with her. | The child gave a **petulant** shrug and refused to answer the question. |
| **Philanthropy** | Noun | The desire to promote the welfare of others, expressed by the generous donation of money to good causes. | His **philanthropy** has helped build several hospitals in impoverished areas. | The foundation is dedicated to **philanthropy** and environmental conservation. | She decided to devote her retirement to **philanthropy** and community service. |
