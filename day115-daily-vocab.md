# Day 115: Daily English Vocabulary

| No. | Word | Part of Speech | Definition | Example 1 | Example 2 | Example 3 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Alacrity** | Noun | Brisk and cheerful readiness. | She accepted the invitation with alacrity, excited to see her old friends. | The team responded with alacrity to the manager's request for help. | He completed the task with such alacrity that everyone was impressed. |
| 2 | **Belligerent** | Adjective | Hostile and aggressive. | His belligerent attitude towards his coworkers made it difficult to work with him. | The two nations were in a belligerent state, threatening each other with military action. | She tried to remain calm despite his belligerent remarks. |
| 3 | **Capricious** | Adjective | Given to sudden and unaccountable changes of mood or behavior. | The weather in this region is notoriously capricious, changing from sunny to rainy in minutes. | Her capricious nature made it hard for her friends to keep up with her plans. | The stock market can be capricious, with prices fluctuating wildly. |
| 4 | **Dichotomy** | Noun | A division or contrast between two things that are or are represented as being opposed or entirely different. | There is a clear dichotomy between the two political ideologies. | The film explores the dichotomy between good and evil. | He struggled with the dichotomy of his professional and personal life. |
| 5 | **Enervate** | Verb | Cause (someone) to feel drained of energy or vitality; weaken. | The heat and humidity of the tropical climate can quickly enervate even the most energetic person. | The long and stressful work week had enervated him completely. | She felt enervated after the grueling marathon. |
| 6 | **Furtive** | Adjective | Attempting to avoid notice or attention, typically because of guilt or a belief that discovery would lead to trouble; secretive. | He cast a furtive glance at his watch, hoping no one would notice he was late. | The two conspirators exchanged furtive whispers in the corner of the room. | She made a furtive attempt to hide the letter before her mother entered the room. |
| 7 | **Garrulous** | Adjective | Excessively talkative, especially on trivial matters. | The garrulous old man could talk for hours about his childhood memories. | She found it hard to get a word in edgewise with her garrulous neighbor. | His garrulous nature made him the life of the party, but also a bit exhausting. |
| 8 | **Harangue** | Noun / Verb | A lengthy and aggressive speech. | The boss delivered a long harangue to the employees about their poor performance. | He began to harangue the crowd about the importance of environmental conservation. | She had to endure a harangue from her parents after she failed her exam. |
| 9 | **Impetuous** | Adjective | Acting or done quickly and without thought or care. | His impetuous decision to quit his job without a backup plan surprised everyone. | She regretted her impetuous words as soon as they left her mouth. | The impetuous youth often got into trouble for his impulsive actions. |
| 10 | **Juxtapose** | Verb | Place or deal with close together for contrasting effect. | The artist juxtaposed bright colors with dark shadows to create a striking effect. | The documentary juxtaposes the lives of the rich and the poor in the city. | It's interesting to juxtapose the two different versions of the story. |
| 11 | **Kindle** | Verb | Light or set on fire; arouse or inspire (an emotion or feeling). | The campfire was kindled with dry leaves and twigs. | Her speech kindled a sense of hope and determination in the audience. | The old photos kindled memories of his childhood. |
| 12 | **Loquacious** | Adjective | Tending to talk a great deal; talkative. | The loquacious host kept the guests entertained with his stories and jokes. | She was known for being loquacious, always ready for a long conversation. | His loquacious nature sometimes made it difficult for others to speak. |
| 13 | **Meticulous** | Adjective | Showing great attention to detail; very careful and precise. | The architect was meticulous in his planning, ensuring every detail was perfect. | She took meticulous notes during the lecture, not wanting to miss anything. | He was meticulous about his appearance, always making sure his clothes were ironed. |
| 14 | **Nuance** | Noun | A subtle difference in or shade of meaning, expression, or sound. | The actor's performance was full of nuance, capturing the complexity of the character. | It's important to understand the nuances of the language to communicate effectively. | She appreciated the nuances of the music, noticing the subtle changes in tone. |
| 15 | **Opulent** | Adjective | Ostentatiously rich and luxurious or lavish. | The opulent palace was filled with gold ornaments and expensive furniture. | They lived an opulent lifestyle, traveling the world and staying in five-star hotels. | The opulent banquet featured a wide variety of exotic and delicious dishes. |
| 16 | **Pragmatic** | Adjective | Dealing with things sensibly and realistically in a way that is based on practical rather than theoretical considerations. | He took a pragmatic approach to the problem, focusing on finding a practical solution. | The company made a pragmatic decision to cut costs and increase efficiency. | She is a pragmatic person who doesn't get carried away by idealistic notions. |
| 17 | **Quixotic** | Adjective | Exceedingly idealistic; unrealistic and impractical. | His quixotic quest to change the world single-handedly was met with skepticism. | She had a quixotic dream of opening a bookstore in a small, remote village. | The project was criticized for being quixotic and lacking a clear plan. |
| 18 | **Reticent** | Adjective | Not revealing one's thoughts or feelings readily. | He was reticent about his personal life, rarely sharing details with his coworkers. | She remained reticent during the meeting, only speaking when directly asked. | His reticent nature made it difficult for others to get to know him. |
| 19 | **Sycophant** | Noun | A person who acts obsequiously toward someone important in order to gain advantage. | The manager was surrounded by sycophants who always agreed with his every word. | He was seen as a sycophant, constantly flattering the boss to get a promotion. | She disliked the sycophants who were always trying to win her favor. |
| 20 | **Taciturn** | Adjective | (Of a person) reserved or uncommunicative in speech; saying little. | The taciturn man preferred to listen rather than speak in social situations. | He was known for being taciturn, often giving one-word answers to questions. | She found it difficult to have a conversation with her taciturn colleague. |
