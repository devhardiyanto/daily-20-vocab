# Day 05 - Daily Vocabulary

Here are 20 new English vocabulary words with their definitions and three contextual examples for each.

| Word | Part of Speech | Definition | Example 1 | Example 2 | Example 3 |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Ephemeral** | Adjective | Lasting for a very short time. | The beauty of the cherry blossoms is **ephemeral**, lasting only a few weeks. | He realized that his fame was **ephemeral** and would soon fade. | The joy of the victory was **ephemeral**, quickly replaced by the stress of the next challenge. |
| **Ubiquitous** | Adjective | Present, appearing, or found everywhere. | In the modern world, smartphones have become **ubiquitous**. | The company's logo is now **ubiquitous** across all major cities. | A sense of fear was **ubiquitous** among the villagers during the storm. |
| **Mellifluous** | Adjective | (Of a voice or words) sweet or musical; pleasant to hear. | The singer's **mellifluous** voice captivated the entire audience. | He delivered the speech in a calm and **mellifluous** tone. | The sound of the stream was a **mellifluous** backdrop to their conversation. |
| **Perfunctory** | Adjective | (Of an action or gesture) carried out with a minimum of effort or reflection. | The guard gave a **perfunctory** nod as we entered the building. | She offered a **perfunctory** apology that didn't sound sincere. | His performance was **perfunctory**, showing a lack of real interest in the role. |
| **Capricious** | Adjective | Given to sudden and unaccountable changes of mood or behavior. | The weather in the mountains is notoriously **capricious**. | Her **capricious** spending habits often led to financial trouble. | The director's **capricious** decisions confused the entire cast and crew. |
| **Ineffable** | Adjective | Too great or extreme to be expressed or described in words. | The beauty of the sunset was **ineffable**. | He felt an **ineffable** sense of peace after meditating. | The artist tried to capture the **ineffable** quality of the human spirit. |
| **Languid** | Adjective | (Of a person, manner, or gesture) displaying or having a disinclination for physical exertion or effort; slow and relaxed. | They spent a **languid** afternoon by the pool, reading and napping. | The cat stretched in a **languid** manner before finally getting up. | Her **languid** movements suggested a deep sense of contentment. |
| **Pernicious** | Adjective | Having a harmful effect, especially in a gradual or subtle way. | The **pernicious** effects of social media on mental health are a growing concern. | He warned against the **pernicious** influence of gossip in the workplace. | The invasive species proved to be **pernicious** to the local ecosystem. |
| **Quixotic** | Adjective | Exceedingly idealistic; unrealistic and impractical. | His **quixotic** quest to save every stray animal was admirable but impossible. | The young inventor pursued a **quixotic** dream of building a flying car. | She was known for her **quixotic** attempts to reform the entire school system overnight. |
| **Serendipity** | Noun | The occurrence and development of events by chance in a happy or beneficial way. | Meeting her was a moment of pure **serendipity**. | The discovery of penicillin was a classic example of scientific **serendipity**. | They relied on **serendipity** to find the perfect location for their picnic. |
| **Taciturn** | Adjective | (Of a person) reserved or uncommunicative in speech; saying little. | The new student was **taciturn** and rarely spoke in class. | His naturally **taciturn** nature made him a good listener. | She found his **taciturn** demeanor to be mysterious and intriguing. |
| **Venerable** | Adjective | Accorded a great deal of respect, especially because of age, wisdom, or character. | The **venerable** professor had taught at the university for over fifty years. | They visited the **venerable** temple, which was centuries old. | The town's mayor was a **venerable** figure in the community. |
| **Zealous** | Adjective | Having or showing great energy or enthusiasm in pursuit of a cause or objective. | The new employee was **zealous** about learning all aspects of the job. | They were **zealous** supporters of the environmental protection movement. | His **zealous** dedication to fitness inspired everyone around him. |
| **Cacophony** | Noun | A harsh, discordant mixture of sounds. | The street market was a **cacophony** of shouting vendors and car horns. | The orchestra tuning up created a brief **cacophony** before the concert began. | He covered his ears to block out the **cacophony** of the construction site. |
| **Delineate** | Verb | Describe or portray (something) precisely. | The architect was asked to **delineate** the boundaries of the new park. | The report clearly **delineated** the responsibilities of each team member. | The artist used fine lines to **delineate** the figure in the portrait. |
| **Egregious** | Adjective | Outstandingly bad; shocking. | It was an **egregious** error that cost the company millions of dollars. | The judge called the defendant's actions an **egregious** abuse of power. | The newspaper published an **egregious** mistake in the headline. |
| **Fatuous** | Adjective | Silly and pointless. | He made a **fatuous** comment that everyone ignored. | The politician's speech was full of **fatuous** promises and empty rhetoric. | She gave a **fatuous** smile, clearly not understanding the gravity of the situation. |
| **Garrulous** | Adjective | Excessively talkative, especially on trivial matters. | The **garrulous** taxi driver told us his entire life story during the short ride. | She tried to avoid the **garrulous** neighbor who always stopped her for a chat. | The character in the play was known for his **garrulous** monologues. |
| **Hegemony** | Noun | Leadership or dominance, especially by one state or social group over others. | The company sought to establish **hegemony** in the global smartphone market. | The fall of the empire marked the end of its regional **hegemony**. | Cultural **hegemony** can be subtle, influencing people's beliefs and values. |
| **Iconoclast** | Noun | A person who attacks cherished beliefs or institutions. | The young artist was an **iconoclast**, challenging the traditional art establishment. | She was an intellectual **iconoclast**, always questioning accepted theories. | His reputation as an **iconoclast** made him a controversial but respected figure. |
