# Day 26: Daily 20 Vocabulary

| Word | Part of Speech | Definition | Example 1 | Example 2 | Example 3 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Ephemeral** | Adjective | Lasting for a very short time. | The beauty of the sunset was **ephemeral**, fading within minutes. | Fashion trends are often **ephemeral**, quickly replaced by new styles. | He captured the **ephemeral** moment with a quick photograph. |
| **Ubiquitous** | Adjective | Present, appearing, or found everywhere. | In the modern world, smartphones have become **ubiquitous**. | The company's logo is **ubiquitous** across all their products and advertising. | She found the same brand of coffee to be **ubiquitous** in every city she visited. |
| **Mellifluous** | Adjective | (Of a voice or words) sweet or musical; pleasant to hear. | The singer's **mellifluous** voice captivated the entire audience. | He delivered the speech in a calm and **mellifluous** tone. | The sound of the harp was wonderfully **mellifluous**. |
| **Capricious** | Adjective | Given to sudden and unaccountable changes of mood or behavior. | The weather in the mountains is notoriously **capricious**. | Her **capricious** spending habits led to financial trouble. | A **capricious** decision by the CEO caused widespread confusion. |
| **Misanthrope** | Noun | A person who dislikes humankind and avoids human society. | After years of betrayal, the old man became a bitter **misanthrope**. | The main character in the novel is a wealthy but lonely **misanthrope**. | He was not a true **misanthrope**, just extremely shy and socially awkward. |
| **Perfunctory** | Adjective | (Of an action or gesture) carried out with a minimum of effort or reflection. | The guard gave a **perfunctory** nod as we entered the building. | He made a **perfunctory** effort to clean his room before his parents arrived. | The doctor's examination was so **perfunctory** that I felt rushed. |
| **Gregarious** | Adjective | (Of a person) fond of company; sociable. | She is a **gregarious** person who loves hosting large parties. | Lions are not solitary animals; they are **gregarious** and live in prides. | His **gregarious** nature made him the perfect candidate for the sales job. |
| **Inadvertent** | Adjective | Not resulting from or achieved through deliberate planning. | The politician made an **inadvertent** admission of guilt during the interview. | I apologize for the **inadvertent** omission of your name from the list. | The error was **inadvertent**, caused by a simple copy-paste mistake. |
| **Exacerbate** | Verb | To make (a problem, bad situation, or negative feeling) worse. | The lack of rain will only **exacerbate** the already critical water shortage. | His constant complaining tends to **exacerbate** the tension in the office. | Stress can **exacerbate** the symptoms of certain medical conditions. |
| **Cacophony** | Noun | A harsh, discordant mixture of sounds. | The street market was a **cacophony** of shouting vendors and car horns. | The orchestra tuning up created a brief **cacophony** before the concert began. | He covered his ears to block out the **cacophony** of the construction site. |
| **Alacrity** | Noun | Brisk and cheerful readiness. | She accepted the new project with **alacrity** and enthusiasm. | The staff responded to the customer's request with impressive **alacrity**. | Despite the early hour, the team arrived at the meeting with great **alacrity**. |
| **Pervasive** | Adjective | (Especially of an unwelcome influence or physical effect) spreading widely throughout an area or group of people. | The scent of pine needles was **pervasive** throughout the forest. | The influence of social media is **pervasive** in modern culture. | A sense of pessimism became **pervasive** after the company announced layoffs. |
| **Mitigate** | Verb | To make less severe, serious, or painful. | Emergency funds were released to **mitigate** the effects of the flood. | They are looking for ways to **mitigate** the risk of cyber attacks. | Wearing a helmet can **mitigate** the severity of a head injury. |
| **Proclivity** | Noun | A tendency to choose or do something regularly; an inclination or predisposition. | He has a natural **proclivity** for mathematics and problem-solving. | The company has a **proclivity** to hire people with diverse backgrounds. | Her **proclivity** for late-night studying often left her tired in the morning. |
| **Specious** | Adjective | Superficially plausible, but actually wrong. | The argument he presented was **specious**, designed to mislead the jury. | He made a **specious** claim about the product's effectiveness. | The report was full of **specious** reasoning and unsubstantiated data. |
| **Vacillate** | Verb | To alternate or waver between different opinions or actions; be indecisive. | The committee continued to **vacillate** between the two proposed solutions. | She tends to **vacillate** when faced with a difficult choice. | His tendency to **vacillate** made him an unreliable leader. |
| **Reticent** | Adjective | Not revealing one's thoughts or feelings readily. | He was **reticent** about his past and preferred to talk about the future. | The witness remained **reticent** despite the lawyer's persistent questioning. | Her **reticent** nature made it difficult to get to know her well. |
| **Fortuitous** | Adjective | Happening by accident or chance rather than design. | It was a **fortuitous** coincidence that we were on the same flight. | The discovery of the old map was a **fortuitous** event for the treasure hunters. | He received a **fortuitous** promotion after his manager unexpectedly resigned. |
| **Obfuscate** | Verb | To make obscure, unclear, or unintelligible. | The politician tried to **obfuscate** the issue with vague language. | Complex jargon can often **obfuscate** the meaning of a simple text. | The company's goal was to **obfuscate** their financial losses from the public. |
| **Sanctimonious** | Adjective | Making a show of being morally superior to other people. | His **sanctimonious** comments about charity irritated everyone at the table. | The preacher delivered a **sanctimonious** sermon on the sins of the community. | She disliked his **sanctimonious** attitude toward people who made mistakes. |
