# Day 101 Daily Vocabulary

| Word | Definition | Example Sentences |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Ubiquitous** | Present, appearing, or found everywhere | 1. In today's digital age, smartphones are ubiquitous. 2. The company's logo has become ubiquitous in popular culture. 3. Coffee shops are ubiquitous in this city. |
| **Ephemeral** | Lasting for a very short time | 1. Fashion trends are often ephemeral. 2. The beauty of a sunset is ephemeral. 3. Childhood memories can feel ephemeral as we grow older. |
| **Cacophony** | A harsh, discordant mixture of sounds | 1. The city street was a cacophony of car horns and sirens. 2. The orchestra produced a cacophony of noise during practice. 3. I woke up to a cacophony of birds chirping outside my window. |
| **Meticulous** | Showing great attention to detail; very careful and precise | 1. She is meticulous in her work, ensuring every detail is perfect. 2. The artist's meticulous brushstrokes created a stunning portrait. 3. He planned his trip with meticulous care. |
| **Benevolent** | Well meaning and kindly | 1. The benevolent king was loved by his people. 2. She had a benevolent smile that put everyone at ease. 3. A benevolent donor contributed a large sum to the charity. |
| **Malevolent** | Having or showing a wish to do evil to others | 1. The villain had a malevolent grin. 2. He felt a malevolent presence in the old house. 3. The malevolent spirit haunted the ancient castle. |
| **Pernicious** | Having a harmful effect, especially in a gradual or subtle way | 1. The pernicious effects of social media on mental health are well-documented. 2. He warned against the pernicious influence of propaganda. 3. The pernicious weed quickly took over the garden. |
| **Gregarious** | Fond of company; sociable | 1. She is a gregarious person who loves to socialize. 2. Gregarious animals often live in herds or flocks. 3. His gregarious nature made him popular at parties. |
| **Ostentatious** | Characterized by vulgar or pretentious display; designed to impress or attract notice | 1. He drove an ostentatious sports car to impress his colleagues. 2. Her ostentatious jewelry drew a lot of attention. 3. The mansion was decorated in an ostentatious style. |
| **Surreptitious** | Kept secret, especially because it would not be approved of | 1. They had a surreptitious meeting in the dead of night. 2. He cast a surreptitious glance at his watch during the boring lecture. 3. She made a surreptitious attempt to sneak out of the house. |
| **Capricious** | Given to sudden and unaccountable changes of mood or behavior | 1. The weather in spring can be very capricious. 2. Her capricious decisions often led to unpredictable outcomes. 3. He was known for his capricious temper. |
| **Sycophant** | A person who acts obsequiously toward someone important in order to gain advantage | 1. The politician was surrounded by sycophants who praised his every move. 2. She despised sycophants who would say anything to get ahead. 3. He was accused of being a sycophant to his boss. |
| **Quixotic** | Extremely idealistic; unrealistic and impractical | 1. His quixotic quest to save the world was admirable but ultimately futile. 2. She had a quixotic dream of becoming a famous artist overnight. 3. The project's quixotic goals made it difficult to achieve. |
| **Belligerent** | Hostile and aggressive | 1. The belligerent drunk started a fight in the bar. 2. His belligerent attitude made it difficult to negotiate. 3. The country adopted a belligerent stance in the international dispute. |
| **Insidious** | Proceeding in a gradual, subtle way, but with harmful effects | 1. The insidious disease spread silently through the population. 2. The insidious rumors slowly eroded her reputation. 3. The insidious propaganda brainwashed many people. |
| **Perfunctory** | (Of an action or gesture) carried out with a minimum of effort or reflection | 1. He gave a perfunctory nod of greeting. 2. Her perfunctory apology did not seem sincere. 3. The security guard conducted a perfunctory search of the bags. |
| **Salient** | Most noticeable or important | 1. The salient feature of the new phone is its improved camera. 2. He highlighted the salient points of the report. 3. The most salient difference between the two theories is their approach to data. |
| **Vacillate** | Alternate or waver between different opinions or actions; be indecisive | 1. She vacillated between two career paths for months. 2. The committee continued to vacillate on the decision. 3. He tends to vacillate when faced with difficult choices. |
| **Parsimonious** | Unwilling to spend money or use resources; stingy or frugal | 1. The parsimonious old man refused to turn on the heating. 2. She was too parsimonious to buy a new dress for the party. 3. His parsimonious habits allowed him to save a significant amount of money. |
| **Zealous** | Having or showing great energy or enthusiasm in pursuit of a cause or objective | 1. He was a zealous supporter of environmental protection. 2. Her zealous efforts led to the success of the project. 3. The team's zealous fans cheered them on to victory. |
