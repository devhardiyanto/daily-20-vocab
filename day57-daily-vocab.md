# Day 57: Daily Vocabulary

| Word | Type | Definition | Example 1 | Example 2 | Example 3 |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Abstain** | Verb | Restrain oneself from doing or enjoying something. | He decided to **abstain** from chocolate for the entire month of Lent. | Most members of the committee chose to **abstain** from voting on the controversial issue. | To maintain his health, he had to **abstain** from drinking alcohol. |
| **Benevolent** | Adjective | Well meaning and kindly. | The **benevolent** old man donated a large portion of his wealth to the local orphanage. | She was known for her **benevolent** nature, always helping those in need. | The company's **benevolent** policies included generous maternity leave and health benefits. |
| **Candid** | Adjective | Truthful and straightforward; frank. | In a **candid** interview, the actress spoke openly about her struggles with fame. | I appreciate your **candid** feedback, even if it was difficult to hear. | The photographer captured a **candid** moment of the children playing in the park. |
| **Deference** | Noun | Humble submission and respect. | He addressed the judge with great **deference**, bowing slightly as he spoke. | Out of **deference** to his elders, he waited for them to be seated before eating. | The young soldier showed proper **deference** to his superior officers. |
| **Eloquent** | Adjective | Fluent or persuasive in speaking or writing. | The president gave an **eloquent** speech that inspired the entire nation. | Her writing is **eloquent** and deeply moving, capturing the essence of human emotion. | He is an **eloquent** advocate for human rights, often speaking at international conferences. |
| **Fabricate** | Verb | Invent or concoct (something), typically with deceitful intent. | He tried to **fabricate** an alibi to explain his whereabouts on the night of the crime. | The journalist was fired for attempting to **fabricate** quotes from famous sources. | It is easy to **fabricate** a story, but much harder to maintain the lie over time. |
| **Gratuitous** | Adjective | Uncalled for; lacking good reason; unwarranted. | The movie was criticized for its **gratuitous** violence and unnecessary gore. | He made a **gratuitous** remark about her appearance that left everyone feeling uncomfortable. | There was no need for such **gratuitous** insults during a professional meeting. |
| **Hypothetical** | Adjective | Based on or serving as a hypothesis; supposed but not necessarily true. | Let's consider a **hypothetical** situation where we have unlimited resources. | The scientist's theory was purely **hypothetical** until it could be proven by experiments. | She asked a **hypothetical** question to see how he would react in a difficult scenario. |
| **Impetuous** | Adjective | Acting or done quickly and without thought or care. | His **impetuous** decision to quit his job without a backup plan surprised everyone. | She is known for her **impetuous** nature, often jumping into new projects without planning. | The **impetuous** young man rushed into the burning building to save the cat. |
| **Jubilant** | Adjective | Feeling or expressing great happiness and triumph. | The crowd was **jubilant** after their team won the championship in the final seconds. | She felt **jubilant** when she received the news that she had been accepted into her dream college. | The city was filled with **jubilant** celebrations following the end of the war. |
| **Kindle** | Verb | Light or set on fire; arouse or inspire (an emotion or feeling). | He used small twigs to **kindle** the campfire as the sun began to set. | The teacher's passion for history helped to **kindle** a similar interest in her students. | Their shared love for music helped to **kindle** a deep and lasting friendship. |
| **Lucid** | Adjective | Expressed clearly; easy to understand; bright or luminous. | The professor gave a **lucid** explanation of the complex mathematical theory. | Despite his age, he remained **lucid** and could remember events from decades ago. | The water in the mountain stream was so **lucid** that you could see the pebbles at the bottom. |
| **Mundane** | Adjective | Lacking interest or excitement; dull; of this earthly world rather than a heavenly or spiritual one. | He found his daily routine of commuting and office work to be quite **mundane**. | Even the most **mundane** tasks can be made enjoyable with the right attitude. | The book focuses on the **mundane** details of everyday life in a small town. |
| **Nefarious** | Adjective | (Typically of an action or activity) wicked or criminal. | The detective uncovered a **nefarious** plot to overthrow the local government. | He was known for his **nefarious** activities, including smuggling and fraud. | The villain's **nefarious** schemes were eventually thwarted by the hero. |
| **Obscure** | Adjective | Not discovered or known about; uncertain; keep from being seen; conceal. | The origins of the ancient artifact remain **obscure** to this day. | He chose an **obscure** book from the library that few people had ever read. | The clouds began to **obscure** the moon, making the night even darker. |
| **Pensive** | Adjective | Engaged in, involving, or reflecting deep or serious thought. | She sat by the window in a **pensive** mood, staring out at the rain. | He gave a **pensive** look when asked about his plans for the future. | The artist's **pensive** expression suggested he was deep in thought about his next masterpiece. |
| **Quaint** | Adjective | Attractively unusual or old-fashioned. | They stayed in a **quaint** little cottage in the countryside for their vacation. | The town is known for its **quaint** streets and traditional architecture. | She has a **quaint** habit of collecting vintage postcards from every city she visits. |
| **Reverence** | Noun | Deep respect for someone or something. | The students showed great **reverence** for their teacher, listening intently to every word. | The ancient temple was treated with **reverence** by the local community. | He spoke of his grandfather with a sense of **reverence** and admiration. |
| **Superfluous** | Adjective | Unnecessary, especially through being more than enough. | The editor removed several **superfluous** paragraphs to make the article more concise. | He felt that the extra decorations were **superfluous** and cluttered the room. | Avoid using **superfluous** words when a simple explanation will suffice. |
| **Tacit** | Adjective | Understood or implied without being stated. | There was a **tacit** agreement between the two friends that they would always support each other. | Her silence was taken as **tacit** approval of the proposed plan. | They had a **tacit** understanding that the topic was not to be discussed in public. |
