# Daily Vocabulary - Day 123

| No. | Word | Part of Speech | Definition | Example 1 | Example 2 | Example 3 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Ephemeral** | Adjective | Lasting for a very short time. | The ephemeral beauty of a sunset is cherished by many. | Fashion trends are often ephemeral, changing with each season. | His fame was ephemeral, quickly fading after a few years. |
| 2 | **Serendipity** | Noun | The occurrence and development of events by chance in a happy or beneficial way. | Finding that rare book at a small, obscure shop was pure serendipity. | Serendipity played a role in their meeting, leading to a lifelong friendship. | The discovery of penicillin was a stroke of serendipity. |
| 3 | **Meticulous** | Adjective | Showing great attention to detail; very careful and precise. | The artist was meticulous in his brushstrokes, ensuring every detail was perfect. | She prepared the report with meticulous care, checking every fact. | A meticulous researcher leaves no stone unturned in their quest for knowledge. |
| 4 | **Cacophony** | Noun | A harsh, discordant mixture of sounds. | The cacophony of car horns and shouting filled the busy street. | The orchestra produced a cacophony of noise during their warm-up. | He couldn't concentrate with the cacophony coming from the construction site. |
| 5 | **Benevolent** | Adjective | Well meaning and kindly. | The benevolent king was loved by his people for his generosity. | She had a benevolent smile that instantly put everyone at ease. | A benevolent donor contributed a large sum to the charity. |
| 6 | **Pernicious** | Adjective | Having a harmful effect, especially in a gradual or subtle way. | The pernicious effects of social media on mental health are a growing concern. | He warned against the pernicious influence of propaganda. | The pernicious weed spread rapidly, choking out other plants. |
| 7 | **Querulous** | Adjective | Complaining in a petulant or whining manner. | The querulous child constantly whined about everything. | His querulous tone made it difficult to have a constructive conversation. | The old man was known for his querulous complaints about the weather. |
| 8 | **Sycophant** | Noun | A person who acts obsequiously toward someone important in order to gain advantage. | The politician was surrounded by sycophants who praised his every move. | She despised sycophants who would say anything to get ahead. | He was accused of being a sycophant, always flattering his boss. |
| 9 | **Tryst** | Noun | A private, romantic rendezvous between lovers. | They arranged a secret tryst in the secluded garden. | Their tryst was discovered, leading to a scandal. | The novel described a series of passionate trysts between the two protagonists. |
| 10 | **Vicarious** | Adjective | Experienced in the imagination through the feelings or actions of another person. | She lived vicariously through her favorite fictional characters. | Reading travel blogs allowed him to experience vicarious adventures. | Parents often feel vicarious pride in their children's achievements. |
| 11 | **Wane** | Verb | (Of the moon) have a progressively smaller part of its visible surface illuminated, so that it appears to decrease in size. (Of a state or feeling) decrease in vigor, power, or extent; become weaker. | The moon began to wane after the full moon. | Her enthusiasm for the project began to wane after several setbacks. | The empire's power waned over centuries, eventually leading to its collapse. |
| 12 | **Zephyr** | Noun | A soft, gentle breeze. | A gentle zephyr rustled the leaves in the trees. | The zephyr brought a welcome coolness on the hot summer day. | She enjoyed the light zephyr blowing through her hair. |
| 13 | **Abstruse** | Adjective | Difficult to understand; obscure. | The philosopher's theories were so abstruse that few could grasp them. | He found the mathematical concept to be utterly abstruse. | The ancient text was filled with abstruse symbols and meanings. |
| 14 | **Banal** | Adjective | So lacking in originality as to be obvious and boring. | The speaker's banal remarks failed to inspire the audience. | She found the plot of the movie to be utterly banal and predictable. | His writing was criticized for its banal observations. |
| 15 | **Cognizant** | Adjective | Having knowledge or being aware of. | He was cognizant of the risks involved in the venture. | It's important to be cognizant of cultural differences when traveling. | The committee was cognizant of the need for immediate action. |
| 16 | **Diatribe** | Noun | A forceful and bitter verbal attack against someone or something. | The politician launched into a diatribe against his opponents. | She delivered a lengthy diatribe about the injustices of the system. | His speech was a fiery diatribe against corruption. |
| 17 | **Eschew** | Verb | Deliberately avoid using; abstain from. | He decided to eschew unhealthy foods and adopt a healthier lifestyle. | The company chose to eschew traditional advertising methods. | Many modern artists eschew conventional forms and techniques. |
| 18 | **Fatuous** | Adjective | Silly and pointless. | His fatuous remarks were met with blank stares. | The fatuous plan was doomed to fail from the start. | She found his constant joking to be rather fatuous. |
| 19 | **Garrulous** | Adjective | Excessively talkative, especially on trivial matters. | The garrulous old man would talk for hours about his past. | She avoided him because of his garrulous nature. | The bartender was a garrulous fellow, always ready with a story. |
| 20 | **Hegemony** | Noun | Leadership or dominance, especially by one state or social group over others. | The cultural hegemony of Hollywood is evident worldwide. | The struggle for regional hegemony led to several conflicts. | The company sought to establish its hegemony in the market. |
