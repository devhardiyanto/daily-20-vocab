# Day 89: Daily Vocabulary

| Word | Definition | Example 1 | Example 2 | Example 3 |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Abstemious** | Not self-indulgent, especially when eating and drinking. | He was **abstemious** at the dinner party, choosing only light snacks. | Her **abstemious** lifestyle contributed to her long health. | The monk led an **abstemious** life in the monastery. |
| **Belligerent** | Hostile and aggressive. | The **belligerent** customer started shouting at the cashier. | His **belligerent** attitude made it difficult to resolve the conflict. | The two nations remained in a **belligerent** state for decades. |
| **Chicanery** | The use of trickery to achieve a political, financial, or legal purpose. | The politician was accused of using **chicanery** to win the election. | He resorted to financial **chicanery** to cover his losses. | The legal system should be free from **chicanery** and deceit. |
| **Deference** | Humble submission and respect. | He addressed the judge with great **deference**. | In many cultures, showing **deference** to elders is mandatory. | She acted in **deference** to her father's wishes. |
| **Enervate** | To cause someone to feel drained of energy or vitality; weaken. | The hot sun began to **enervate** the hikers. | Long hours of repetitive work can **enervate** even the most dedicated employee. | The illness had a tendency to **enervate** his spirit. |
| **Fastidious** | Very attentive to and concerned about accuracy and detail. | He is **fastidious** about his appearance, always wearing a suit. | The editor was **fastidious** in checking every citation. | She is so **fastidious** that she cleans her house every single day. |
| **Garrulous** | Excessively talkative, especially on trivial matters. | The **garrulous** neighbor kept me at the door for an hour. | He became more **garrulous** after a few drinks. | Her **garrulous** nature made her a popular storyteller. |
| **Harangue** | A lengthy and aggressive speech. | The boss delivered a long **harangue** about the team's performance. | He was tired of his father's constant **harangues** about money. | The activist began to **harangue** the crowd about social justice. |
| **Iconoclast** | A person who attacks cherished beliefs or institutions. | As an **iconoclast**, he challenged the traditional methods of the industry. | The artist was known as an **iconoclast** who broke all the rules. | She has always been an **iconoclast** in the world of fashion. |
| **Jovial** | Cheerful and friendly. | The **jovial** host made everyone feel welcome at the party. | He had a **jovial** personality that brightened the room. | Despite the rain, the mood at the festival remained **jovial**. |
| **Laconic** | (Of a person, speech, or style of writing) using very few words. | His **laconic** reply suggested he wasn't interested in talking. | The detective was known for his **laconic** and direct manner. | She gave a **laconic** "yes" to the proposal. |
| **Magnanimous** | Very generous or forgiving, especially toward a rival or someone less powerful than oneself. | He was **magnanimous** in victory, praising his opponent's effort. | The company made a **magnanimous** donation to the local hospital. | It was **magnanimous** of her to overlook his earlier rudeness. |
| **Nefarious** | (Typically of an action or activity) wicked or criminal. | The hackers had a **nefarious** plan to steal personal data. | He was convicted of **nefarious** activities involving money laundering. | The villain's **nefarious** schemes were finally thwarted. |
| **Obsequious** | Obedient or attentive to an excessive or servile degree. | The **obsequious** waiter bowed every time he approached the table. | He was surrounded by **obsequious** assistants who agreed with everything he said. | Her **obsequious** behavior towards the boss was quite transparent. |
| **Paradigm** | A typical example or pattern of something; a model. | The discovery led to a **paradigm** shift in scientific thinking. | This project serves as a **paradigm** for future developments. | He is considered a **paradigm** of integrity in his profession. |
| **Quixotic** | Exceedingly idealistic; unrealistic and impractical. | His **quixotic** quest to eliminate all poverty seemed noble but impossible. | They embarked on a **quixotic** journey to find the lost city. | It was a **quixotic** attempt to change the entire system overnight. |
| **Reticent** | Not revealing one's thoughts or feelings readily. | She was **reticent** about her personal life during the interview. | He is a **reticent** man who prefers to listen rather than speak. | The witness remained **reticent** despite the lawyer's questioning. |
| **Sycophant** | A person who acts obsequiously toward someone important in order to gain advantage. | The CEO was tired of being surrounded by **sycophants**. | He was a classic **sycophant**, always flattering the manager. | Don't be a **sycophant**; give me your honest opinion. |
| **Taciturn** | (Of a person) reserved or uncommunicative in speech; saying little. | The **taciturn** driver didn't say a word during the entire trip. | He was a **taciturn** man who kept his thoughts to himself. | Her **taciturn** nature was often mistaken for coldness. |
| **Ubiquity** | The fact of appearing everywhere or being very common. | The **ubiquity** of the internet has changed how we communicate. | The brand's **ubiquity** makes it a household name. | He was surprised by the **ubiquity** of coffee shops in the small town. |
