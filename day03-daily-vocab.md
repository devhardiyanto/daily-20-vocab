# Daily Vocabulary - Day 03

| Word | Part of Speech | Definition | Examples |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Ephemeral** | Adjective | Lasting for a very short time. | 1. The beauty of the sunset was **ephemeral**, fading within minutes. <br> 2. Fashion trends are often **ephemeral**, quickly replaced by new styles. <br> 3. He realized that his youthful energy was **ephemeral** and needed to be cherished. |
| **Ubiquitous** | Adjective | Present, appearing, or found everywhere. | 1. In the modern world, smartphones have become **ubiquitous**. <br> 2. The company's logo is **ubiquitous** across all forms of media. <br> 3. The smell of pine needles was **ubiquitous** in the mountain air. |
| **Mellifluous** | Adjective | (Of a voice or words) sweet or musical; pleasant to hear. | 1. The singer's **mellifluous** voice captivated the entire audience. <br> 2. He delivered the poem in a **mellifluous** tone that soothed everyone. <br> 3. The stream's **mellifluous** sound was the only noise in the quiet forest. |
| **Languid** | Adjective | Displaying or having a disinclination for physical exertion or effort; slow and relaxed. | 1. They spent a **languid** afternoon by the pool, sipping iced tea. <br> 2. The cat stretched in a **languid** manner before finally getting up. <br> 3. Her **languid** movements suggested a complete lack of urgency. |
| **Cacophony** | Noun | A harsh, discordant mixture of sounds. | 1. The city traffic created a deafening **cacophony** of horns and engines. <br> 2. The orchestra's warm-up was a brief **cacophony** before the conductor arrived. <br> 3. The children's excited shouting turned into a **cacophony** of noise. |
| **Pernicious** | Adjective | Having a harmful effect, especially in a gradual or subtle way. | 1. The **pernicious** effects of the drug were not immediately apparent. <br> 2. Spreading false rumors can be a **pernicious** form of social sabotage. <br> 3. He warned against the **pernicious** influence of negative thinking. |
| **Surreptitious** | Adjective | Kept secret, especially because it would not be approved of. | 1. They held a **surreptitious** meeting in the dead of night to discuss the plan. <br> 2. She cast a **surreptitious** glance at her watch during the boring lecture. <br> 3. The spy made a **surreptitious** exchange of documents in the crowded square. |
| **Resilience** | Noun | The capacity to recover quickly from difficulties; toughness. | 1. The community showed great **resilience** in rebuilding after the devastating storm. <br> 2. Developing emotional **resilience** is key to mental well-being. <br> 3. The plant's **resilience** allowed it to thrive even in the harsh desert climate. |
| **Equanimity** | Noun | Mental calmness, composure, and evenness of temper, especially in a difficult situation. | 1. The pilot maintained his **equanimity** despite the severe turbulence. <br> 2. She faced the criticism with remarkable **equanimity** and professionalism. <br> 3. Meditation helps to cultivate a sense of inner **equanimity**. |
| **Fastidious** | Adjective | Very attentive to and concerned about accuracy and detail. | 1. The chef was **fastidious** about the presentation of every dish. <br> 2. He is a **fastidious** dresser, always impeccably groomed. <br> 3. The editor's **fastidious** attention to grammar caught several subtle errors. |
| **Ineffable** | Adjective | Too great or extreme to be expressed or described in words. | 1. The beauty of the aurora borealis was an **ineffable** experience. <br> 2. He felt an **ineffable** joy upon holding his newborn child for the first time. <br> 3. The artist tried to capture the **ineffable** mystery of the universe in his work. |
| **Placid** | Adjective | (Of a person or animal) not easily upset or excited; (of a place or body of water) calm and peaceful. | 1. The **placid** lake reflected the surrounding mountains perfectly. <br> 2. Despite the chaos, the old man remained **placid** and unbothered. <br> 3. She found the **placid** rhythm of country life very soothing. |
| **Quixotic** | Adjective | Exceedingly idealistic; unrealistic and impractical. | 1. His **quixotic** quest to save the local bookstore from a chain was admirable but doomed. <br> 2. The young inventor pursued a **quixotic** dream of building a perpetual motion machine. <br> 3. She was known for her **quixotic** generosity, often giving away her last dollar. |
| **Sycophant** | Noun | A person who acts obsequiously toward someone important in order to gain advantage. | 1. The manager was surrounded by **sycophants** who constantly praised his every decision. <br> 2. No one respected the **sycophant** who only spoke to flatter the CEO. <br> 3. She despised the way her colleague acted like a **sycophant** to get a promotion. |
| **Tantamount** | Adjective | Equivalent in seriousness to; virtually the same as. | 1. His silence on the matter was **tantamount** to an admission of guilt. <br> 2. Ignoring the safety regulations is **tantamount** to risking the lives of the workers. <br> 3. For a professional athlete, a public apology is **tantamount** to a career-ending mistake. |
| **Vociferous** | Adjective | (Especially of a person or speech) vehement or clamorous. | 1. The protestors were **vociferous** in their demands for immediate change. <br> 2. A **vociferous** critic of the new policy, he wrote several angry letters to the editor. <br> 3. The crowd's **vociferous** cheering spurred the team on to victory. |
| **Wistful** | Adjective | Having or showing a feeling of vague or regretful longing. | 1. She gave a **wistful** sigh as she looked at the old photographs of her childhood home. <br> 2. The song evoked a **wistful** nostalgia for a time that could never return. <br> 3. He had a **wistful** look in his eyes when he spoke of his unfulfilled dreams. |
| **Zealous** | Adjective | Having or showing great energy or enthusiasm in pursuit of a cause or objective. | 1. The new employee was **zealous** in his efforts to impress the management. <br> 2. She is a **zealous** advocate for environmental protection and spends all her free time volunteering. <br> 3. The cult's **zealous** followers were willing to make any sacrifice for their leader. |
| **Incongruous** | Adjective | Not in harmony or keeping with the surroundings or other aspects of something. | 1. The bright pink flamingo lawn ornament looked utterly **incongruous** in the snowy landscape. <br> 2. His casual attire was **incongruous** with the formal setting of the black-tie gala. <br> 3. The detective noticed an **incongruous** detail in the witness's otherwise perfect story. |
| **Prevaricate** | Verb | Speak or act in an evasive way. | 1. When questioned about the missing funds, the accountant began to **prevaricate**. <br> 2. Rather than giving a straight answer, the politician chose to **prevaricate** about his true intentions. <br> 3. Stop trying to **prevaricate** and just tell me what happened. |
