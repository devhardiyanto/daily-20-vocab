# Daily Vocabulary - Day 120

| No. | Word | Part of Speech | Definition | Example 1 | Example 2 | Example 3 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Ameliorate** | Verb | To make (something bad or unsatisfactory) better. | The new policies were designed to ameliorate the living conditions of the poor. | Efforts to ameliorate the drought included water rationing and conservation campaigns. | A warm cup of tea can often ameliorate the discomfort of a cold. |
| 2 | **Cacophony** | Noun | A harsh, discordant mixture of sounds. | The cacophony of car horns and shouting voices filled the busy street. | The orchestra produced a cacophony of noise during their initial warm-up. | I couldn't concentrate with the cacophony of construction outside my window. |
| 3 | **Delineate** | Verb | To describe or portray (something) precisely. | The architect delineated the building's features in great detail on the blueprint. | In her speech, she clearly delineated the responsibilities of each team member. | The artist used sharp lines to delineate the figures in the painting. |
| 4 | **Ebullient** | Adjective | Cheerful and full of energy. | Her ebullient personality made her a joy to be around. | The ebullient crowd cheered as their team scored the winning goal. | He was in an ebullient mood after receiving the good news. |
| 5 | **Fervent** | Adjective | Having or displaying a passionate intensity. | He was a fervent supporter of environmental protection. | Her fervent prayers were for the safety of her family. | The speaker delivered a fervent plea for justice. |
| 6 | **Garrulous** | Adjective | Excessively talkative, especially on trivial matters. | The garrulous old man entertained us with stories from his youth. | I tried to avoid the garrulous passenger sitting next to me on the plane. | Her garrulous nature sometimes made it difficult to get a word in edgewise. |
| 7 | **Hapless** | Adjective | (Especially of a person) unfortunate. | The hapless victim of the scam lost all his savings. | The hapless traveler missed his flight due to a series of delays. | Despite their best efforts, the hapless team lost every game of the season. |
| 8 | **Impecunious** | Adjective | Having little or no money; penniless. | He was an impecunious artist struggling to make ends meet. | The family was left impecunious after the economic downturn. | Despite his impecunious state, he maintained a cheerful outlook. |
| 9 | **Jubilant** | Adjective | Feeling or expressing great happiness and triumph. | The fans were jubilant after their team won the championship. | She was jubilant when she received the acceptance letter from her dream university. | The entire nation was jubilant at the news of the peace treaty. |
| 10 | **Kudos** | Noun | Praise and honor received for an achievement. | Kudos to the team for their outstanding performance on the project. | She received kudos from her colleagues for her innovative ideas. | The chef earned kudos for his exquisite culinary creations. |
| 11 | **Languid** | Adjective | (Of a person, manner, or gesture) having a disinclination for physical exertion or effort; slow and relaxed. | A languid afternoon spent reading in the hammock was just what I needed. | The languid movements of the cat suggested it was ready for a nap. | She gave a languid wave as the train pulled away from the station. |
| 12 | **Malediction** | Noun | A magical word or phrase uttered with the intention of bringing about evil or destruction; a curse. | The witch uttered a malediction upon the unsuspecting villagers. | He feared the malediction would bring him bad luck for years to come. | The ancient text contained a powerful malediction against those who would disturb the tomb. |
| 13 | **Nefarious** | Adjective | (Typically of an action or activity) wicked or criminal. | The nefarious plot to overthrow the government was uncovered by the secret service. | He was known for his nefarious dealings in the underworld. | The villain's nefarious schemes were always thwarted by the hero. |
| 14 | **Obsequious** | Adjective | Obedient or attentive to an excessive or servile degree. | The obsequious waiter hovered around the wealthy diners, anticipating their every need. | Her obsequious flattery failed to impress her boss. | He was surrounded by obsequious assistants who catered to his every whim. |
| 15 | **Pernicious** | Adjective | Having a harmful effect, especially in a gradual or subtle way. | The pernicious effects of pollution on the environment are becoming increasingly evident. | Gossip can have a pernicious influence on a person's reputation. | The pernicious weed spread rapidly, choking out native plants. |
| 16 | **Querulous** | Adjective | Complaining in a petulant or whining manner. | The querulous child constantly whined about everything. | His querulous tone made it difficult to have a productive conversation. | The querulous old man complained about the slightest inconvenience. |
| 17 | **Rancor** | Noun | Bitterness or resentfulness, especially when long-standing. | The old feud was filled with deep-seated rancor between the two families. | Despite their disagreements, there was no rancor in their professional relationship. | He spoke with rancor about the injustices he had suffered. |
| 18 | **Sycophant** | Noun | A person who acts obsequiously toward someone important in order to gain advantage. | The politician was surrounded by sycophants who praised his every move. | She despised sycophants who would say anything to get ahead. | He was a sycophant, always flattering his superiors to earn favors. |
| 19 | **Tryst** | Noun | A private, romantic rendezvous between lovers. | They arranged a secret tryst in the secluded garden. | Their tryst was discovered, leading to a scandal. | The novel described a series of passionate trysts between the two protagonists. |
| 20 | **Ubiquitous** | Adjective | Present, appearing, or found everywhere. | Smartphones have become ubiquitous in modern society. | The ubiquitous presence of surveillance cameras raises privacy concerns. | Coffee shops are ubiquitous in urban areas. |
