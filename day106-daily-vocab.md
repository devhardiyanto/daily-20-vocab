# Day 106: Daily Vocabulary

| Word | Definition | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Abnegation** | The act of renouncing or rejecting something; self-denial | 1. Her abnegation of worldly pleasures allowed her to focus on spiritual growth. 2. The monk lived a life of abnegation, forsaking all material possessions. 3. His abnegation of responsibility led to the project's failure. |
| **Banal** | So lacking in originality as to be obvious and boring | 1. The movie's plot was so banal that I fell asleep. 2. He made a few banal remarks about the weather. 3. Her writing style was criticized for being banal and uninspired. |
| **Cacophony** | A harsh, discordant mixture of sounds | 1. A cacophony of car horns filled the street during rush hour. 2. The orchestra produced a cacophony of noise during their warm-up. 3. The classroom erupted in a cacophony of shouts and laughter. |
| **Delineate** | To describe or portray (something) precisely | 1. The architect delineated the plans for the new building. 2. The report clearly delineates the responsibilities of each team member. 3. She delineated the boundaries of the property on the map. |
| **Ebullient** | Cheerful and full of energy | 1. Her ebullient personality made her popular with everyone. 2. The ebullient crowd cheered for their team. 3. He was in an ebullient mood after receiving the good news. |
| **Fecund** | Producing or capable of producing an abundance of offspring or new growth; fertile | 1. The fecund soil yielded a bountiful harvest. 2. The artist's fecund imagination produced many masterpieces. 3. The rainforest is a fecund ecosystem, teeming with life. |
| **Gainsay** | Deny or contradict (a fact or statement) | 1. It is difficult to gainsay the evidence presented. 2. No one could gainsay her claim to the inheritance. 3. He was too polite to gainsay his host's opinion. |
| **Hapless** | (Especially of a person) unfortunate | 1. The hapless victim was robbed of all his belongings. 2. The hapless traveler lost his passport in a foreign country. 3. Despite their hapless attempts, they failed to win the game. |
| **Ignominious** | Deserving or causing public disgrace or shame | 1. The team suffered an ignominious defeat in the final. 2. He made an ignominious exit from the political arena. 3. The scandal led to his ignominious resignation. |
| **Juggernaut** | A huge, powerful, and overwhelming force or institution | 1. The company became a financial juggernaut in the industry. 2. The army was an unstoppable juggernaut, crushing all opposition. 3. The new technology was a juggernaut that transformed the market. |
| **Kudos** | Praise and honor received for an achievement | 1. Kudos to the team for their outstanding performance. 2. She received kudos from her colleagues for her innovative ideas. 3. He earned kudos for his bravery in saving the child. |
| **Languid** | (Of a person, manner, or gesture) having a disinclination for physical exertion or effort; slow and relaxed | 1. A languid afternoon spent by the pool. 2. Her languid movements suggested a lack of energy. 3. The hot weather made everyone feel languid. |
| **Mendacious** | Not telling the truth; lying | 1. The mendacious witness was caught in a lie. 2. He was known for his mendacious claims and exaggerations. 3. The newspaper published a mendacious report. |
| **Nadir** | The lowest point in the fortunes of a person or organization | 1. His career reached its nadir after the scandal. 2. The company hit its nadir during the economic recession. 3. She felt she had reached the nadir of her emotional state. |
| **Obsequious** | Obedient or attentive to an excessive or servile degree | 1. The obsequious waiter hovered around the wealthy diners. 2. She disliked his obsequious flattery. 3. The new employee was overly obsequious to the boss. |
| **Panacea** | A solution or remedy for all difficulties or diseases | 1. There is no panacea for all the world's problems. 2. Many people hoped the new drug would be a panacea for cancer. 3. Education is often seen as a panacea for social ills. |
| **Quiescent** | In a state or period of inactivity or dormancy | 1. The volcano has been quiescent for many years. 2. The disease remained quiescent for a long time before reappearing. 3. The political situation was quiescent after the protests. |
| **Recalcitrant** | Having an obstinately uncooperative attitude toward authority or discipline | 1. The recalcitrant student refused to follow the rules. 2. The government faced a recalcitrant opposition. 3. The donkey was recalcitrant and wouldn't move. |
| **Salient** | Most noticeable or important | 1. The salient features of the new design were its simplicity and elegance. 2. He pointed out the salient facts of the case. 3. One of the salient differences between the two cultures is their approach to time. |
| **Tenebrous** | Dark; shadowy or obscure | 1. The tenebrous alley was a perfect setting for a mystery novel. 2. His writing style was often described as tenebrous and melancholic. 3. The ancient castle had many tenebrous corridors. |
