# Day 78: Daily English Vocabulary

| Word | Part of Speech | Definition | Example 1 | Example 2 | Example 3 |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Quell** | Verb | Put an end to (a rebellion or other disorder), typically by the use of force. | The troops were sent in to **quell** the uprising in the capital city. | She took a deep breath to **quell** her rising panic before the presentation. | The government tried to **quell** public fears about the new economic policy. |
| **Querulous** | Adjective | Complaining in a petulant or whining manner. | The **querulous** customer was never satisfied, no matter how hard we tried. | He became increasingly **querulous** as he grew older and more frustrated. | Her **querulous** tone of voice made it difficult for others to enjoy the trip. |
| **Quiescent** | Adjective | In a state or period of inactivity or dormancy. | The volcano has been **quiescent** for several centuries, but it is not extinct. | During the winter months, the garden remains **quiescent** and peaceful. | The stock market was relatively **quiescent** today with very little trading. |
| **Quintessential** | Adjective | Representing the most perfect or typical example of a quality or class. | He is the **quintessential** English gentleman, always polite and well-dressed. | The small village is the **quintessential** example of rural charm and beauty. | This dish is the **quintessential** comfort food for many people in this region. |
| **Quixotic** | Adjective | Exceedingly idealistic; unrealistic and impractical. | His **quixotic** quest to eliminate all poverty in the world was admired but doubted. | She had a **quixotic** notion that she could change the world single-handedly. | The project was criticized for being a **quixotic** waste of time and resources. |
| **Quotidian** | Adjective | Of or occurring every day; daily; ordinary or everyday, especially when mundane. | The movie captures the **quotidian** struggles of a working-class family. | He found beauty in the **quotidian** details of life, like a sunrise or a meal. | Her journal was filled with accounts of her **quotidian** activities and thoughts. |
| **Rancor** | Noun | Bitterness or resentfulness, especially when long-standing. | Despite their divorce, there was no **rancor** between the two former spouses. | The political debate was filled with **rancor** and personal attacks. | He spoke without **rancor**, even though he had been treated very unfairly. |
| **Rapacious** | Adjective | Aggressively greedy or grasping. | The **rapacious** developers cleared the forest to build luxury apartments. | He had a **rapacious** appetite for power and would do anything to get it. | The company's **rapacious** pricing policies led to a boycott by consumers. |
| **Rapprochement** | Noun | (Especially in international relations) an establishment or resumption of harmonious relations. | The two countries are hoping for a **rapprochement** after years of conflict. | The meeting was seen as a significant step toward a **rapprochement**. | There are signs of a **rapprochement** between the rival factions in the party. |
| **Ratify** | Verb | Sign or give formal consent to (a treaty, contract, or agreement), making it officially valid. | The parliament voted to **ratify** the international trade agreement. | Both nations must **ratify** the treaty before it can come into effect. | The union members met to **ratify** the new contract with the company. |
| **Raucous** | Adjective | Making or constituting a disturbingly harsh and loud noise. | The party next door was so **raucous** that I couldn't get any sleep. | A **raucous** group of fans celebrated their team's victory in the streets. | The meeting ended in **raucous** laughter after someone told a funny joke. |
| **Ravenous** | Adjective | Extremely hungry; (of hunger or need) very great; voracious. | I was **ravenous** after the long hike and could have eaten anything. | The **ravenous** wolves searched for food in the deep snow of the forest. | He has a **ravenous** appetite for knowledge and reads several books a week. |
| **Raze** | Verb | Completely destroy (a building, town, or other settlement). | The old factory was **raze**d to the ground to make way for a new park. | The entire village was **raze**d during the war, leaving many homeless. | They plan to **raze** the dilapidated building and build a modern office block. |
| **Rebuff** | Verb | Reject (someone or something) in an abrupt or ungracious manner. | She **rebuff**ed his offer of help, saying she could manage on her own. | The company's takeover bid was firmly **rebuff**ed by the board of directors. | He felt hurt when his friendly advances were **rebuff**ed by his colleagues. |
| **Recalcitrant** | Adjective | Having an obstinately uncooperative attitude toward authority or discipline. | The **recalcitrant** student refused to follow the teacher's instructions. | The government struggled to deal with the **recalcitrant** rebel groups. | He remained **recalcitrant**, refusing to admit that he had made a mistake. |
| **Recant** | Verb | Say that one no longer holds a belief or opinion, especially one considered unorthodox. | The witness was pressured to **recant** her testimony by the defendant's lawyer. | He refused to **recant** his views, even when threatened with imprisonment. | She later **recant**ed her earlier statement, saying she had been mistaken. |
| **Recapitulate** | Verb | Summarize and state again the main points of. | At the end of the lecture, the professor will **recapitulate** the key concepts. | Let me **recapitulate** what we have discussed so far in this meeting. | The report concludes by **recapitulate**ing the main findings of the study. |
| **Reciprocal** | Adjective | Given, felt, or done in return; (of an agreement or obligation) binding on two or more parties. | They have a **reciprocal** arrangement to help each other with childcare. | The two countries signed a **reciprocal** trade agreement to reduce tariffs. | Respect is a **reciprocal** emotion; you must give it to receive it. |
| **Reclusive** | Adjective | Avoiding the company of other people; solitary. | The **reclusive** author rarely gave interviews or appeared in public. | He lived a **reclusive** life in a remote cabin deep in the mountains. | She became increasingly **reclusive** after the death of her husband. |
| **Recondite** | Adjective | (Of a subject or knowledge) little known; abstruse. | The book explores the **recondite** details of medieval philosophy. | His research focuses on a **recondite** area of theoretical physics. | The professor's lecture was so **recondite** that few students understood it. |
