# Day 23: Daily Vocabulary

| Word | Definition | Example 1 | Example 2 | Example 3 |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Ephemeral** | Lasting for a very short time. | The beauty of the cherry blossoms is **ephemeral**, lasting only a few weeks. | Fashion trends are often **ephemeral**, quickly replaced by new styles. | He realized that his fame was **ephemeral** and focused on more lasting achievements. |
| **Ubiquitous** | Present, appearing, or found everywhere. | In the modern world, smartphones have become truly **ubiquitous**. | The company's logo is **ubiquitous** across all forms of media. | The smell of pine needles was **ubiquitous** in the mountain air. |
| **Mellifluous** | (Of a voice or words) sweet or musical; pleasant to hear. | The singer's **mellifluous** voice captivated the entire audience. | She spoke in a **mellifluous** tone, making even complex instructions sound soothing. | The old violin produced a rich, **mellifluous** sound. |
| **Pernicious** | Having a harmful effect, especially in a gradual or subtle way. | The **pernicious** effects of social media on mental health are a growing concern. | He warned against the **pernicious** influence of gossip in the workplace. | The drought had a **pernicious** impact on the region's agriculture. |
| **Quixotic** | Exceedingly idealistic; unrealistic and impractical. | His **quixotic** quest to save the old factory was admirable but doomed to fail. | She has a **quixotic** belief in the inherent goodness of all people. | The plan was dismissed as too **quixotic** by the pragmatic board members. |
| **Serendipity** | The occurrence and development of events by chance in a happy or beneficial way. | Meeting my business partner was a moment of pure **serendipity**. | The discovery of penicillin was a famous example of scientific **serendipity**. | They found the perfect antique chair by sheer **serendipity** at a small market. |
| **Languid** | (Of a person, manner, or gesture) having a disinclination for physical exertion or effort; slow and relaxed. | A **languid** afternoon was spent reading a book by the pool. | The cat stretched in a **languid** manner, completely unbothered by the noise. | She gave a **languid** wave of her hand as the train pulled away. |
| **Capricious** | Given to sudden and unaccountable changes of mood or behavior. | The weather in the mountains is notoriously **capricious**. | Her **capricious** spending habits often led to financial trouble. | A good leader should be steady, not **capricious** in their decision-making. |
| **Egregious** | Outstandingly bad; shocking. | It was an **egregious** error that cost the company millions of dollars. | The judge described the defendant's actions as an **egregious** abuse of power. | The report highlighted several **egregious** failures in safety protocol. |
| **Inchoate** | Just begun and so not fully formed or developed; rudimentary. | The project is still in an **inchoate** stage, with many details yet to be decided. | He had an **inchoate** idea for a novel, but the plot was still hazy. | The artist's early sketches show an **inchoate** style that later matured. |
| **Recalcitrant** | Having an obstinately uncooperative attitude toward authority or discipline. | The **recalcitrant** student refused to follow the teacher's instructions. | The government faced a **recalcitrant** opposition determined to block the new law. | Despite the training, the donkey remained **recalcitrant** and refused to move. |
| **Disparate** | Essentially different in kind; not allowing comparison. | The committee was composed of people with widely **disparate** political views. | The novel weaves together several **disparate** storylines into a cohesive whole. | We need to find a way to reconcile the **disparate** data sources. |
| **Voracious** | Wanting or devouring great quantities of food; having a very eager approach to an activity. | He is a **voracious** reader, finishing several books a week. | The young athlete has a **voracious** appetite for competition and training. | A **voracious** fire quickly consumed the dry forest. |
| **Salient** | Most noticeable or important. | The **salient** feature of the new design is its energy efficiency. | She summarized the **salient** points of the long report for the executive team. | His height was the most **salient** characteristic that made him stand out. |
| **Paucity** | The presence of something only in small or insufficient quantities or amounts; scarcity. | There was a **paucity** of evidence to support the prosecution's case. | The remote village suffered from a **paucity** of medical supplies. | A **paucity** of fresh ideas led to a stagnation in the company's growth. |
| **Convivial** | (Of an atmosphere or event) friendly, lively, and enjoyable. | The wedding reception was a warm and **convivial** affair. | He was a **convivial** host, making sure every guest felt welcome. | They enjoyed a **convivial** dinner with good food and great conversation. |
| **Obfuscate** | Render obscure, unclear, or unintelligible. | The politician tried to **obfuscate** the issue with vague and confusing language. | His complex writing style tends to **obfuscate** the main message. | Do not try to **obfuscate** the facts; just tell us what happened. |
| **Profligate** | Recklessly extravagant or wasteful in the use of resources. | The company's **profligate** spending led to its eventual bankruptcy. | He was known for his **profligate** lifestyle, wasting his inheritance on parties. | The government was criticized for its **profligate** use of public funds. |
| **Alacrity** | Brisk and cheerful readiness. | She accepted the new assignment with **alacrity** and enthusiasm. | The staff responded to the customer's request with impressive **alacrity**. | He rose from his seat with **alacrity** to greet the unexpected visitor. |
| **Vacillate** | Alternate or waver between different opinions or actions; be indecisive. | The committee continued to **vacillate** between the two proposed solutions. | He tends to **vacillate** when faced with a difficult choice. | Her tendency to **vacillate** made it hard for her team to move forward. |
