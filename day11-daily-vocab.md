# Day 11: Daily 20 Vocabulary Words

| Word | Part of Speech | Definition | Contextual Examples |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Ephemeral** | Adjective | Lasting for a very short time. | 1. The beauty of the cherry blossoms is **ephemeral**, lasting only a few weeks. <br> 2. Fashion trends are often **ephemeral**, quickly replaced by new styles. <br> 3. He realized the joy he felt was **ephemeral**, fading as quickly as it came. |
| **Ubiquitous** | Adjective | Present, appearing, or found everywhere. | 1. In the modern world, smartphones have become truly **ubiquitous**. <br> 2. The company's logo is **ubiquitous** across all major cities. <br> 3. The scent of pine was **ubiquitous** in the mountain air. |
| **Mellifluous** | Adjective | (Of a voice or words) sweet or musical; pleasant to hear. | 1. The singer's **mellifluous** voice captivated the entire audience. <br> 2. She spoke in a **mellifluous** tone, making the complex topic easy to understand. <br> 3. The stream's **mellifluous** gurgle was the only sound in the forest. |
| **Cacophony** | Noun | A harsh, discordant mixture of sounds. | 1. The construction site was a **cacophony** of drills, hammers, and shouting. <br> 2. The orchestra's warm-up created a brief **cacophony** before the conductor arrived. <br> 3. The political debate descended into a **cacophony** of angry voices. |
| **Pernicious** | Adjective | Having a harmful effect, especially in a gradual or subtle way. | 1. The **pernicious** effects of misinformation can erode public trust. <br> 2. He warned against the **pernicious** influence of the toxic work environment. <br> 3. Smoking is a **pernicious** habit that slowly damages the lungs. |
| **Alacrity** | Noun | Brisk and cheerful readiness. | 1. She accepted the new project with **alacrity** and enthusiasm. <br> 2. The staff responded to the customer's request with surprising **alacrity**. <br> 3. Despite the early hour, the hikers started their journey with great **alacrity**. |
| **Voracious** | Adjective | Having a very eager approach to an activity; or, wanting or devouring great quantities of food. | 1. He is a **voracious** reader, finishing several books a week. <br> 2. The **voracious** appetite of the young puppy was astonishing. <br> 3. The company has a **voracious** need for new software engineers. |
| **Quixotic** | Adjective | Exceedingly idealistic; unrealistic and impractical. | 1. His plan to save the failing business with a single, massive investment was **quixotic**. <br> 2. She pursued her **quixotic** dream of building a self-sustaining farm in the desert. <br> 3. The young activist's **quixotic** fight against the entire system was admirable but futile. |
| **Insidious** | Adjective | Proceeding in a gradual, subtle way, but with harmful effects. | 1. The **insidious** disease showed no symptoms until it was too late. <br> 2. Propaganda can be an **insidious** force, slowly changing public opinion. <br> 3. The financial scam was **insidious**, targeting vulnerable elderly people over many months. |
| **Surreptitious** | Adjective | Kept secret, especially because it would not be approved of. | 1. They held a **surreptitious** meeting in the back room of the cafe. <br> 2. He cast a **surreptitious** glance at his watch during the long lecture. <br> 3. The spy made a **surreptitious** exchange of documents under the table. |
| **Capricious** | Adjective | Given to sudden and unaccountable changes of mood or behavior. | 1. The weather in the mountains is notoriously **capricious**, changing from sun to snow in minutes. <br> 2. Her **capricious** spending habits often left her short of money by the end of the month. <br> 3. The director's **capricious** decisions made it difficult for the team to plan ahead. |
| **Lugubrious** | Adjective | Looking or sounding sad and dismal. | 1. The funeral director's **lugubrious** expression matched the somber mood of the service. <br> 2. He delivered a long, **lugubrious** speech about the decline of the industry. <br> 3. The dog let out a **lugubrious** howl when its owner left the house. |
| **Obfuscate** | Verb | To make something obscure, unclear, or unintelligible. | 1. The politician tried to **obfuscate** the truth with vague and confusing statements. <br> 2. Complex jargon can often **obfuscate** the core message of a scientific paper. <br> 3. He deliberately used technical terms to **obfuscate** the simple problem. |
| **Salient** | Adjective | Most noticeable or important. | 1. The **salient** feature of the new phone is its improved battery life. <br> 2. She summarized the **salient** points of the long report for the executive team. <br> 3. His height was the most **salient** characteristic that made him stand out in the crowd. |
| **Mendacious** | Adjective | Not telling the truth; lying. | 1. The witness was proven to be **mendacious** after conflicting statements were presented. <br> 2. The article was full of **mendacious** claims designed to mislead the public. <br> 3. He was known for his **mendacious** nature, always exaggerating or inventing stories. |
| **Paucity** | Noun | The presence of something only in small or insufficient quantities or amounts; scarcity. | 1. There is a **paucity** of evidence to support the defendant's claims. <br> 2. The remote village suffered from a **paucity** of clean drinking water. <br> 3. A **paucity** of funding forced the research team to scale back their project. |
| **Recalcitrant** | Adjective | Having an obstinately uncooperative attitude toward authority or discipline. | 1. The **recalcitrant** student refused to follow the teacher's instructions. <br> 2. The government faced a **recalcitrant** opposition determined to block the new legislation. <br> 3. Training the **recalcitrant** donkey proved to be a difficult and frustrating task. |
| **Sycophant** | Noun | A person who acts obsequiously toward someone important in order to gain advantage; a flatterer. | 1. The manager was surrounded by **sycophants** who constantly praised his every idea. <br> 2. She was tired of dealing with the **sycophant** who only complimented her to get a promotion. <br> 3. The king dismissed the **sycophant** from his court, preferring honest counsel. |
| **Trenchant** | Adjective | Vigorous or incisive in expression or style. | 1. Her **trenchant** analysis of the economic situation was widely praised. <br> 2. The critic's **trenchant** wit made his reviews both feared and respected. <br> 3. He delivered a **trenchant** speech that cut through the political rhetoric. |
| **Venerable** | Adjective | Accorded a great deal of respect, especially because of age, wisdom, or character. | 1. The **venerable** professor had been teaching at the university for over fifty years. <br> 2. They visited the **venerable** temple, a site of pilgrimage for centuries. <br> 3. The company's **venerable** founder was celebrated on its anniversary. |
