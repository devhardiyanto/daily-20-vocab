# Day 20: Daily Vocabulary

| Word | Part of Speech | Definition | Example 1 | Example 2 | Example 3 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Ephemeral** | *Adjective* | Lasting for a very short time. | The **ephemeral** beauty of the cherry blossoms is a reminder that nothing lasts forever. | His fame was **ephemeral**, quickly fading after the initial media frenzy. | We must cherish these **ephemeral** moments of peace before the storm. |
| **Mellifluous** | *Adjective* | (Of a voice or words) sweet or musical; pleasant to hear. | The singer's **mellifluous** voice soothed the audience into a state of calm. | He delivered the poem in a **mellifluous** tone that captivated everyone. | The sound of the stream was a **mellifluous** backdrop to their conversation. |
| **Ubiquitous** | *Adjective* | Present, appearing, or found everywhere. | In the modern world, smartphones have become truly **ubiquitous**. | The company's logo is **ubiquitous** on every street corner. | The scent of pine needles was **ubiquitous** in the mountain air. |
| **Serendipity** | *Noun* | The occurrence and development of events by chance in a happy or beneficial way. | Meeting my old friend in a foreign city was a moment of pure **serendipity**. | The discovery of penicillin was a classic case of scientific **serendipity**. | She credits **serendipity** for leading her to the perfect job. |
| **Petrichor** | *Noun* | A pleasant, earthy smell after rain. | The heavy rain brought with it the distinct and refreshing scent of **petrichor**. | I love to open the windows and breathe in the **petrichor** after a summer shower. | The desert landscape was transformed by the sudden arrival of **petrichor**. |
| **Languid** | *Adjective* | Displaying or having a disinclination for physical exertion or effort; slow and relaxed. | A **languid** afternoon was spent reading a book by the pool. | The cat stretched in a **languid** manner, clearly enjoying the sun. | His **languid** movements suggested a lack of urgency about the task. |
| **Ineffable** | *Adjective* | Too great or extreme to be expressed or described in words. | The beauty of the sunset was an **ineffable** experience. | She felt an **ineffable** joy upon holding her newborn child. | The artist tried to capture the **ineffable** quality of the light in his painting. |
| **Quixotic** | *Adjective* | Exceedingly idealistic; unrealistic and impractical. | His **quixotic** quest to save every stray animal in the city was admirable but impossible. | The startup's business plan was dismissed as too **quixotic** by the investors. | She has a **quixotic** belief in the inherent goodness of all people. |
| **Susurrus** | *Noun* | A soft, low sound; a whisper or rustling. | The gentle **susurrus** of the leaves in the breeze was the only sound. | They spoke in a low **susurrus**, sharing secrets across the table. | The **susurrus** of the ocean waves lulled the vacationers to sleep. |
| **Halcyon** | *Adjective* | Denoting a period of time in the past that was idyllically happy and peaceful. | She often reminisced about the **halcyon** days of her childhood. | The company is trying to recapture the success of its **halcyon** years. | We enjoyed a **halcyon** week of perfect weather and relaxation at the beach. |
| **Ponderous** | *Adjective* | Slow and clumsy because of great weight; dull, laborious, or excessively solemn. | The speaker's voice was **ponderous** and monotonous, causing the audience to lose interest. | He lifted the **ponderous** suitcase with a grunt of effort. | The novel was a **ponderous** tome, full of long, unnecessary descriptions. |
| **Juxtaposition** | *Noun* | The fact of two things being seen or placed close together with contrasting effect. | The artist used the **juxtaposition** of bright and dark colors to create a dramatic effect. | The essay analyzed the striking **juxtaposition** of wealth and poverty in the city. | The photograph captured the strange **juxtaposition** of an ancient ruin and a modern skyscraper. |
| **Cacophony** | *Noun* | A harsh, discordant mixture of sounds. | The street market was a **cacophony** of shouting vendors and blaring horns. | The orchestra rehearsal was a **cacophony** before the conductor took charge. | I couldn't concentrate with the **cacophony** of construction noise outside. |
| **Ebullient** | *Adjective* | Cheerful and full of energy. | Her **ebullient** personality made her the life of every party. | The team was in an **ebullient** mood after winning the championship. | He gave an **ebullient** speech, thanking everyone for their support. |
| **Lugubrious** | *Adjective* | Looking or sounding sad and dismal. | The dog gave a **lugubrious** howl when its owner left the house. | He delivered the bad news in a **lugubrious** voice that matched the gravity of the situation. | The old mansion had a **lugubrious** atmosphere, filled with shadows and silence. |
| **Nefarious** | *Adjective* | (Typically of an action or activity) wicked or criminal. | The villain's **nefarious** plot to steal the city's water supply was foiled. | The company was involved in a **nefarious** scheme to defraud investors. | He was arrested for his part in the **nefarious** drug operation. |
| **Pernicious** | *Adjective* | Having a harmful effect, especially in a gradual or subtle way. | The **pernicious** effects of social media on mental health are becoming clear. | She warned against the **pernicious** influence of gossip in the workplace. | The drought had a **pernicious** impact on the region's agriculture. |
| **Ruminate** | *Verb* | To think deeply about something. | He needed time to **ruminate** on the difficult decision before giving an answer. | She would often **ruminate** on the meaning of life during her long walks. | The board members will **ruminate** over the proposal before the next meeting. |
| **Salient** | *Adjective* | Most noticeable or important. | The **salient** feature of the new phone is its improved battery life. | He summarized the **salient** points of the report for the executive team. | The most **salient** difference between the two policies is the cost. |
| **Vex** | *Verb* | To make (someone) feel annoyed, frustrated, or worried, especially with trivial matters. | The constant delays began to **vex** the travelers. | The complex instructions were designed to **vex** the user. | Don't let such a small problem **vex** you; it's easily fixed. |
