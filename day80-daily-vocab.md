# Day 80: Daily English Vocabulary

| Word | Part of Speech | Definition | Example 1 | Example 2 | Example 3 |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Resolute** | Adjective | Admirably purposeful, determined, and unwavering. | She remained **resolute** in her decision to pursue a career in medicine despite the challenges. | The team's **resolute** defense prevented the opposing side from scoring any goals. | He was **resolute** in his commitment to finishing the marathon, no matter how tired he felt. |
| **Resonant** | Adjective | (Of sound) deep, clear, and continuing to sound or ring. | The singer's **resonant** voice filled the entire concert hall with beautiful music. | The church bells had a **resonant** chime that could be heard throughout the village. | His speech was filled with **resonant** metaphors that left a lasting impression on the audience. |
| **Resplendent** | Adjective | Attractive and impressive through being richly colorful or shining. | The bride looked **resplendent** in her white lace gown and sparkling diamond tiara. | The garden was **resplendent** with vibrant flowers of every color and hue. | The sun set in a **resplendent** display of orange, pink, and purple across the sky. |
| **Restive** | Adjective | (Of a person) unable to keep still or silent and becoming difficult to control. | The crowd grew **restive** as they waited for the delayed concert to begin. | The children became **restive** during the long car ride and started to bicker. | The political situation in the country remained **restive** following the controversial election. |
| **Reticent** | Adjective | Not revealing one's thoughts or feelings readily. | He was **reticent** about his past and rarely spoke about his childhood. | The witness was **reticent** to provide any information to the police during the investigation. | She is a **reticent** person who prefers to listen rather than speak in social situations. |
| **Retort** | Verb | To say something in answer to a remark or accusation, typically in a sharp, angry, or wittily incisive manner. | "I don't need your help," she **retort**ed when he offered to carry her bags. | He was quick to **retort** with a clever comeback when his friend teased him. | The politician **retort**ed to the journalist's question with a firm denial of any wrongdoing. |
| **Retract** | Verb | To draw or pull (something) back or back in. | The cat **retract**ed its claws after it finished playing with the toy. | The company had to **retract** its statement after discovering that the information was incorrect. | He was forced to **retract** his allegations of fraud after failing to provide any evidence. |
| **Retrench** | Verb | (Of a company or government) to reduce costs or spending in response to economic difficulty. | The company decided to **retrench** its workforce in order to save money during the recession. | The government had to **retrench** its spending on public services to reduce the national debt. | Many families have had to **retrench** their household budgets due to the rising cost of living. |
| **Retribution** | Noun | Punishment inflicted on someone as vengeance for a wrong or criminal act. | The victim's family sought **retribution** for the senseless crime that had been committed. | He believed that his misfortune was a form of divine **retribution** for his past mistakes. | The legal system is designed to provide **retribution** for those who break the law. |
| **Revelry** | Noun | Lively and noisy festivities, especially when these involve drinking a large amount of alcohol. | The streets were filled with **revelry** as the city celebrated its annual festival. | The sounds of **revelry** could be heard from the neighbor's house late into the night. | After the team's victory, the fans engaged in a night of joyous **revelry** in the city center. |
| **Revere** | Verb | To feel deep respect or admiration for (something). | Many people **revere** the late civil rights leader for his courage and dedication. | The ancient civilization used to **revere** the sun as a powerful and life-giving deity. | He is a highly **revere**d figure in the world of science for his groundbreaking discoveries. |
| **Reverie** | Noun | A state of being pleasantly lost in one's thoughts; a daydream. | She was lost in a **reverie** about her upcoming vacation and didn't hear the phone ring. | He often falls into a **reverie** while staring out the window at the beautiful landscape. | The peaceful music induced a state of calm **reverie** in the listeners. |
| **Revile** | Verb | To criticize in an abusive or angrily insulting manner. | The politician was **revile**d by his opponents for his controversial policies. | He was **revile**d by the public after his involvement in the scandal was revealed. | It is never acceptable to **revile** someone based on their race, religion, or background. |
| **Rhapsodize** | Verb | To speak or write about someone or something with great enthusiasm and delight. | She would often **rhapsodize** about the beauty of the mountains in her hometown. | The food critic began to **rhapsodize** about the delicious flavors of the chef's signature dish. | He could **rhapsodize** for hours about his favorite classic movies and actors. |
| **Rhetoric** | Noun | The art of effective or persuasive speaking or writing, especially the use of figures of speech and other compositional techniques. | The politician's **rhetoric** was designed to appeal to the emotions of the voters. | The teacher emphasized the importance of using clear and concise **rhetoric** in academic writing. | His speech was filled with empty **rhetoric** and lacked any real substance or solutions. |
| **Ribald** | Adjective | Referring to sexual matters in an amusingly rude or irreverent way. | The comedian's **ribald** jokes were not suitable for a family audience. | He entertained his friends with a series of **ribald** stories and anecdotes. | The play was criticized for its **ribald** humor and suggestive language. |
| **Rife** | Adjective | (Especially of something undesirable or harmful) of common occurrence; widespread. | The city was **rife** with rumors about the upcoming election and potential candidates. | The company's management was **rife** with corruption and unethical practices. | The garden was **rife** with weeds that were choking the growth of the flowers. |
| **Rift** | Noun | A crack, split, or break in something. | The earthquake caused a large **rift** to open up in the middle of the road. | There was a growing **rift** between the two business partners over the company's future. | The disagreement led to a permanent **rift** in their long-standing friendship. |
| **Rigid** | Adjective | Unable to bend or be forced out of shape; not flexible. | The **rigid** plastic container was difficult to open without a pair of scissors. | The company's **rigid** hierarchy made it difficult for employees to share their ideas. | He had a **rigid** schedule that he followed every day without fail. |
| **Rigor** | Noun | The quality of being extremely thorough, exhaustive, or accurate. | The scientific study was conducted with great **rigor** to ensure the accuracy of the results. | The academic program is known for its intellectual **rigor** and demanding coursework. | He approached his work with a level of **rigor** that was admired by his colleagues. |
