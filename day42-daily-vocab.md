# Daily Vocabulary - Day 42

| Word | Part of Speech | Definition | Example 1 | Example 2 | Example 3 |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Pervasive** | Adjective | Spreading widely throughout an area or a group of people. | The **pervasive** smell of fresh coffee filled the entire house. | Corruption was **pervasive** within the local government for decades. | Social media has a **pervasive** influence on modern communication. |
| **Quiescent** | Adjective | In a state or period of inactivity or dormancy. | The volcano has been **quiescent** for over a hundred years. | During the winter, many animals remain **quiescent** to conserve energy. | The stock market was unusually **quiescent** during the holiday week. |
| **Rancor** | Noun | Bitterness or resentfulness, especially when long-standing. | The two brothers spoke to each other without any **rancor** despite their past. | He felt no **rancor** toward the person who had taken his job. | The political debate was filled with **rancor** and personal attacks. |
| **Sanguine** | Adjective | Optimistic or positive, especially in an apparently bad or difficult situation. | Despite the setbacks, the team remained **sanguine** about their chances of winning. | He has a **sanguine** disposition that helps him handle stress well. | Economists are not very **sanguine** about the prospects for a quick recovery. |
| **Taciturn** | Adjective | (Of a person) reserved or uncommunicative in speech; saying little. | My father was a **taciturn** man who rarely expressed his feelings. | The **taciturn** witness refused to provide any details to the police. | He became even more **taciturn** after the loss of his close friend. |
| **Ubiquitous** | Adjective | Present, appearing, or found everywhere. | Smartphones have become **ubiquitous** in today's society. | The company's logo is **ubiquitous**, appearing on billboards and TV ads. | Plastic waste is unfortunately **ubiquitous** in our oceans. |
| **Vacillate** | Verb | Alternate or waver between different opinions or actions; be indecisive. | I tended to **vacillate** between the two job offers for several weeks. | The politician was criticized for **vacillating** on key policy issues. | She continued to **vacillate** about whether to sell her house. |
| **Wary** | Adjective | Feeling or showing caution about possible dangers or problems. | Dogs that have been mistreated are often **wary** of strangers. | Investors are **wary** of putting money into such a volatile market. | She gave him a **wary** look when he offered to help her. |
| **Zealot** | Noun | A person who is fanatical and uncompromising in pursuit of their religious, political, or other ideals. | The religious **zealot** spent all his time trying to convert others. | He was described as a fitness **zealot** who never missed a workout. | The political **zealot** refused to listen to any opposing viewpoints. |
| **Abjure** | Verb | Solemnly renounce (a belief, cause, or claim). | He was forced to **abjure** his political beliefs to avoid imprisonment. | She decided to **abjure** all luxury items and live a simple life. | The convert was asked to **abjure** his former religion. |
| **Blandishment** | Noun | A flattering or pleasing statement or action used to persuade someone gently to do something. | Despite his **blandishments**, she refused to change her mind. | The salesman used every **blandishment** he knew to close the deal. | She was immune to the **blandishments** of the young suitor. |
| **Cajole** | Verb | Persuade (someone) to do something by sustained coaxing or flattery. | He managed to **cajole** his friend into lending him the car. | She **cajoled** her parents into letting her stay out late. | The manager tried to **cajole** the employees into working overtime. |
| **Disparate** | Adjective | Essentially different in kind; not allowing comparison. | The two cultures have **disparate** traditions and values. | The collection includes **disparate** items ranging from stamps to old coins. | It is difficult to reconcile such **disparate** points of view. |
| **Elicit** | Verb | Evoke or draw out (a response, answer, or fact) from someone in reaction to one's own actions or questions. | The comedian's jokes failed to **elicit** even a single laugh. | The police tried to **elicit** information from the suspect. | Her remarks were intended to **elicit** a reaction from the audience. |
| **Furtive** | Adjective | Attempting to avoid notice or attention, typically because of guilt or a belief that discovery would lead to trouble; secretive. | He cast a **furtive** glance at his watch during the boring meeting. | They had a **furtive** meeting in the park to discuss their secret plans. | The cat made a **furtive** approach toward the bird on the lawn. |
| **Gregarious** | Adjective | (Of a person) fond of company; sociable. | She is a **gregarious** person who loves hosting large parties. | Most dolphins are highly **gregarious** and live in social groups. | He was not particularly **gregarious**, preferring to spend time alone. |
| **Harangue** | Noun | A lengthy and aggressive speech. | The boss delivered a long **harangue** about the team's poor performance. | We had to listen to his **harangue** on the evils of modern technology. | The protestor's **harangue** could be heard from several blocks away. |
| **Inexorable** | Adjective | Impossible to stop or prevent. | The **inexorable** progress of science continues to change our lives. | We cannot escape the **inexorable** passage of time. | The company faced an **inexorable** decline in sales over the last year. |
| **Judicious** | Adjective | Having, showing, or done with good judgment or sense. | The curator made a **judicious** selection of paintings for the exhibition. | We should make **judicious** use of our limited natural resources. | Her **judicious** advice helped me avoid a major mistake. |
| **Kindle** | Verb | Light or set on fire; arouse or inspire (an emotion or feeling). | The teacher's passion for history helped **kindle** an interest in her students. | He used small twigs to **kindle** the campfire. | The romantic movie was enough to **kindle** old memories of their first date. |
