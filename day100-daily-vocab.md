# Day 100 Daily Vocabulary

| Word | Definition | Example Sentences |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Unprecedented** | Never done or experienced before; without previous example | 1. The pandemic caused an unprecedented shift to online learning. 2. The company experienced unprecedented growth in the last quarter. 3. Her success at such a young age was truly unprecedented. |
| **Adept** | Highly skilled or proficient at something | 1. She’s adept at explaining complex grammar in a simple way. 2. He is adept at problem-solving and can quickly find solutions. 3. The chef is adept at creating delicious and innovative dishes. |
| **Dwindle** | To gradually decrease or become smaller | 1. Attendance at the evening class dwindled after the holidays. 2. Their hopes of winning the game dwindled with each passing minute. 3. The population of the endangered species continues to dwindle. |
| **Mitigate** | To make something less severe, harmful, or painful | 1. Wearing a seatbelt can mitigate the risk of injury in an accident. 2. The government implemented policies to mitigate the effects of the economic crisis. 3. They tried to mitigate the damage caused by the storm. |
| **Plethora** | A large or excessive amount of something | 1. Espresso English offers a plethora of lessons, courses, and e-books. 2. The library has a plethora of books on various subjects. 3. There was a plethora of options to choose from at the buffet. |
| **Ambiguous** | Unclear; open to multiple interpretations | 1. The instructions were ambiguous, so no one knew what to do. 2. His response to the question was ambiguous, leaving us unsure of his stance. 3. The ending of the movie was ambiguous, allowing viewers to interpret it in different ways. |
| **Scrutinize** | To examine very carefully | 1. Investors will scrutinize the company’s financial statements. 2. The detective scrutinized every detail of the crime scene. 3. She scrutinized the document for any errors before submitting it. |
| **Convey** | To communicate or express | 1. It’s hard to convey sarcasm over text. 2. The painting conveys a sense of peace and tranquility. 3. He struggled to convey his feelings in words. |
| **Candid** | Honest and direct, even when uncomfortable | 1. She gave a candid answer about her experience with failure. 2. The politician was praised for his candid remarks during the interview. 3. I appreciate your candid feedback, even if it's difficult to hear. |
| **Omit** | To leave out | 1. You can omit that paragraph — it’s not essential. 2. Please omit any unnecessary details from your report. 3. The recipe instructed me to omit the sugar for a healthier version. |
| **Caliber** | Level of quality or ability | 1. They’re hiring someone of the highest caliber for the role. 2. The new recruits are of exceptional caliber. 3. The university attracts students of high academic caliber. |
| **Prevalent** | Widespread or commonly occurring | 1. Work-from-home policies have become more prevalent. 2. The use of smartphones is prevalent among teenagers. 3. This particular disease is more prevalent in certain regions. |
| **Nuance** | A subtle or slight difference in meaning or tone | 1. His performance captured every nuance of the character. 2. She understood the nuances of the negotiation. 3. The artist paid attention to every nuance of color in the painting. |
| **Detrimental** | Harmful or damaging | 1. Smoking is clearly detrimental to your health. 2. Lack of sleep can be detrimental to your academic performance. 3. The company's decision proved detrimental to its reputation. |
| **Discrepancy** | A difference between two things that should match | 1. There’s a discrepancy between the reports and the receipts. 2. The audit revealed a discrepancy in the financial records. 3. There was a noticeable discrepancy between his words and his actions. |
| **Pivotal** | Critically important or central | 1. That meeting was a pivotal moment in the negotiation. 2. The invention of the printing press was a pivotal event in history. 3. Her role in the project was pivotal to its success. |
| **Refute** | To prove something is false or incorrect | 1. She refuted the claim with solid evidence. 2. He tried to refute the accusations made against him. 3. The scientist presented data to refute the prevailing theory. |
| **Resilient** | Able to recover quickly from difficulties; strong and adaptable | 1. Despite many setbacks, she remained resilient and kept working toward her goal. 2. The community proved resilient in the face of the natural disaster. 3. The resilient plant managed to grow in harsh conditions. |
| **Unleash** | To release something powerful or intense | 1. The coach’s speech unleashed a wave of motivation in the team. 2. The storm unleashed its fury on the coastal town. 3. The new marketing campaign aims to unleash consumer demand. |
| **Pinnacle** | The highest or most successful point of something | 1. Winning the championship was the pinnacle of his career. 2. The mountain peak is the pinnacle of the climbing challenge. 3. Achieving that award was the pinnacle of her artistic achievements. |
