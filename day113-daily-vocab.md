# Day 113: Daily English Vocabulary

| No. | Word | Part of Speech | Definition | Example 1 | Example 2 | Example 3 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Aberration | Noun | A departure from what is normal, usual, or expected, typically an unwelcome one. | The sudden drop in temperature was an aberration for this time of year. | Her angry outburst was an aberration from her usually calm demeanor. | The flaw in the diamond was a minor aberration that didn't affect its value. |
| 2 | Alacrity | Noun | Brisk and cheerful readiness. | She accepted the invitation with alacrity. | The waiter responded to our request with surprising alacrity. | He tackled the difficult project with alacrity and enthusiasm. |
| 3 | Ameliorate | Verb | Make (something bad or unsatisfactory) better. | The new policies were designed to ameliorate the living conditions in the city. | A hot cup of tea can ameliorate the symptoms of a cold. | Steps have been taken to ameliorate the situation. |
| 4 | Anomaly | Noun | Something that deviates from what is standard, normal, or expected. | There are a number of anomalies in the present system. | The genetic anomaly caused the plant to grow twice as fast. | His behavior that day was an anomaly; he is usually very predictable. |
| 5 | Antipathy | Noun | A deep-seated feeling of aversion. | He felt a strong antipathy towards his rival. | There has always been a mutual antipathy between the two families. | Her antipathy for mathematics made her dread the class. |
| 6 | Arcane | Adjective | Understood by few; mysterious or secret. | The professor specialized in arcane legal history. | He was the only one who understood the arcane rules of the game. | The manuscript was filled with arcane symbols. |
| 7 | Assuage | Verb | Make (an unpleasant feeling) less intense. | The letter assuaged the fears of most members. | He tried to assuage his guilt by donating to charity. | A good massage can assuage muscle pain. |
| 8 | Audacious | Adjective | Showing a willingness to take surprisingly bold risks. | The company made an audacious decision to expand internationally. | His audacious remark shocked everyone in the room. | It was an audacious plan, but it just might work. |
| 9 | Banal | Adjective | So lacking in originality as to be obvious and boring. | The pop song's lyrics were completely banal. | He made some banal remarks about the weather. | The movie's plot was banal and predictable. |
| 10 | Belligerent | Adjective | Hostile and aggressive. | The belligerent customer demanded to see the manager. | He had a belligerent attitude towards authority. | The two nations have been belligerent for decades. |
| 11 | Cacophony | Noun | A harsh discordant mixture of sounds. | A cacophony of car horns woke her up. | The classroom was a cacophony of shouting children. | The orchestra produced a cacophony before tuning their instruments. |
| 12 | Capricious | Adjective | Given to sudden and unaccountable changes of mood or behavior. | The capricious weather ruined our picnic plans. | He is a capricious boss who changes his mind constantly. | Her capricious nature made it hard to rely on her. |
| 13 | Castigate | Verb | Reprimand (someone) severely. | The principal castigated the student for his poor behavior. | He was castigated by the media for his controversial comments. | She castigated herself for making such a foolish mistake. |
| 14 | Catalyst | Noun | A person or thing that precipitates an event. | The protests acted as a catalyst for political change. | His speech was the catalyst that sparked the revolution. | The new manager was a catalyst for improving team morale. |
| 15 | Cathartic | Adjective | Providing psychological relief through the open expression of strong emotions. | Crying can be a very cathartic experience. | Writing in her journal was a cathartic release for her anxiety. | The therapy session proved to be highly cathartic. |
| 16 | Caustic | Adjective | Sarcastic in a scathing and bitter way. | The critic's caustic review ruined the play's reputation. | She is known for her caustic wit. | His caustic comments hurt her feelings deeply. |
| 17 | Clandestine | Adjective | Kept secret or done secretively, especially because illicit. | The spies held a clandestine meeting in the park. | They had a clandestine affair that lasted for years. | The group's clandestine activities were eventually exposed. |
| 18 | Coerce | Verb | Persuade (an unwilling person) to do something by using force or threats. | They were coerced into signing the contract. | The dictator used fear to coerce the population. | He claimed he was coerced into making a false confession. |
| 19 | Cogent | Adjective | (of an argument or case) clear, logical, and convincing. | She presented a cogent argument for the new policy. | The lawyer's cogent defense won the case. | There is no cogent reason to delay the project any further. |
| 20 | Commensurate | Adjective | Corresponding in size or degree; in proportion. | Salary will be commensurate with experience. | The punishment should be commensurate with the crime. | His efforts were not commensurate with the results achieved. |
