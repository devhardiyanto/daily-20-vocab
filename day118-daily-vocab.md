# Day 118: Daily English Vocabulary

| No. | Word | Part of Speech | Definition | Example 1 | Example 2 | Example 3 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Abnegation** | Noun | The act of renouncing or rejecting something. | Her abnegation of worldly pleasures led her to a monastic life. | The abnegation of personal desires is often seen as a virtue in some philosophies. | He practiced abnegation of self-interest for the greater good of the community. |
| 2 | **Bifurcate** | Verb | To divide into two branches or forks. | The road bifurcated into two paths, one leading to the village and the other to the forest. | The river bifurcates around the island, creating two distinct channels. | The company decided to bifurcate its operations into two separate divisions. |
| 3 | **Cacophony** | Noun | A harsh, discordant mixture of sounds. | The cacophony of traffic outside made it difficult to concentrate. | The orchestra produced a cacophony of sounds during the chaotic rehearsal. | A cacophony of alarms blared through the building during the fire drill. |
| 4 | **Decimate** | Verb | To kill, destroy, or remove a large percentage or part of. | The disease decimated the population of the deer. | The budget cuts will decimate the public services in the city. | The invading army decimated the enemy forces. |
| 5 | **Ebullient** | Adjective | Cheerful and full of energy. | Her ebullient personality made her popular at parties. | The ebullient crowd cheered for their team with great enthusiasm. | He was in an ebullient mood after receiving the good news. |
| 6 | **Fiduciary** | Adjective | Involving trust, especially with regard to the relationship between a trustee and a beneficiary. | A lawyer has a fiduciary duty to act in the best interests of their client. | The financial advisor has a fiduciary responsibility to manage investments wisely. | The board members have a fiduciary obligation to the shareholders. |
| 7 | **Gregarious** | Adjective | (Of a person) fond of company; sociable. | She was a gregarious person who loved attending social events. | Lions are gregarious animals, living in prides. | His gregarious nature made him an excellent host. |
| 8 | **Hegemony** | Noun | Leadership or dominance, especially by one state or social group over others. | The cultural hegemony of Hollywood is evident in global cinema. | The struggle for regional hegemony often leads to conflicts. | The company sought to establish its hegemony in the technology market. |
| 9 | **Inchoate** | Adjective | Not yet fully formed or developed; rudimentary. | The inchoate idea for a novel slowly began to take shape. | His inchoate thoughts on the subject were still disorganized. | The project was still in an inchoate stage, lacking clear direction. |
| 10 | **Juxtapose** | Verb | To place or deal with close together for contrasting effect. | The artist juxtaposed bright colors with dark shades to create a dramatic effect. | The film juxtaposes scenes of poverty with scenes of extreme wealth. | She juxtaposed her childhood memories with her current experiences. |
| 11 | **Kudos** | Noun | Praise and honor received for an achievement. | Kudos to the team for their outstanding performance. | He received kudos from his colleagues for successfully completing the difficult project. | The chef earned kudos for his innovative culinary creations. |
| 12 | **Lachrymose** | Adjective | Tearful or given to weeping. | The lachrymose movie brought tears to everyone's eyes. | She was in a lachrymose mood after hearing the sad news. | His lachrymose farewell speech moved the audience. |
| 13 | **Mellifluous** | Adjective | (Of a voice or words) sweet or musical; pleasant to hear. | Her mellifluous voice captivated the audience. | The mellifluous sounds of the harp filled the concert hall. | He spoke in a mellifluous tone, making his words sound like music. |
| 14 | **Nadir** | Noun | The lowest point in the fortunes of a person or organization. | The company reached its nadir during the economic recession. | His career hit its nadir after a series of failures. | The nadir of her despair was when she lost all hope. |
| 15 | **Obfuscate** | Verb | To make obscure, unclear, or unintelligible. | The politician tried to obfuscate the truth with vague statements. | The complex jargon only served to obfuscate the main point of the argument. | He deliberately obfuscated the issue to avoid taking responsibility. |
| 16 | **Panacea** | Noun | A solution or remedy for all difficulties or diseases. | There is no single panacea for all the world's problems. | Many people hoped that the new law would be a panacea for social inequality. | He believed that education was the panacea for poverty. |
| 17 | **Quiescent** | Adjective | In a state or period of inactivity or dormancy. | The volcano has been quiescent for many years. | The political situation remained quiescent after the protests. | The disease can remain quiescent for a long time before symptoms appear. |
| 18 | **Recalcitrant** | Adjective | Having an obstinately uncooperative attitude toward authority or discipline. | The recalcitrant student refused to follow the teacher's instructions. | The government faced a recalcitrant opposition in parliament. | The wild horse was recalcitrant and difficult to train. |
| 19 | **Salient** | Adjective | Most noticeable or important. | The salient features of the report were highlighted in the summary. | One of the salient differences between the two cultures is their approach to time. | The most salient point of his argument was the need for immediate action. |
| 20 | **Tantamount** | Adjective | Equivalent in seriousness to; virtually the same as. | His silence was tantamount to an admission of guilt. | Ignoring the warning signs would be tantamount to negligence. | Her refusal to cooperate was tantamount to an act of rebellion. |
