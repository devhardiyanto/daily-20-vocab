# Day 19: Daily Vocabulary

| Word | Part of Speech | Definition | Contextual Examples |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Ephemeral** | Adjective | Lasting for a very short time. | 1. The beauty of the cherry blossoms is **ephemeral**, lasting only a few weeks each spring. <br> 2. He realized that fame in the digital age is often **ephemeral** and quickly forgotten. <br> 3. The artist specialized in creating **ephemeral** sculptures out of sand and ice. |
| **Ubiquitous** | Adjective | Present, appearing, or found everywhere. | 1. In the modern world, smartphones have become an **ubiquitous** part of daily life. <br> 2. The company's logo is now **ubiquitous**, visible on billboards, buses, and television screens. <br> 3. The **ubiquitous** presence of surveillance cameras raised concerns about privacy. |
| **Mellifluous** | Adjective | (Of a voice or words) sweet or musical; pleasant to hear. | 1. The singer's **mellifluous** voice soothed the audience and earned a standing ovation. <br> 2. He delivered the poem in a **mellifluous** tone that made every word sound beautiful. <br> 3. The sound of the stream flowing over the rocks was a **mellifluous** background to their conversation. |
| **Petrichor** | Noun | A pleasant, earthy smell after rain. | 1. After the long drought, the sudden downpour brought with it the distinct scent of **petrichor**. <br> 2. She loved the smell of the forest, especially the strong **petrichor** that followed a summer storm. <br> 3. The word **petrichor** is derived from the Greek *petra* (stone) and *ichor* (the fluid that flows in the veins of the gods). |
| **Languid** | Adjective | Displaying or having a disinclination for physical exertion or effort; slow and relaxed. | 1. They spent a **languid** afternoon by the pool, occasionally dipping their toes in the water. <br> 2. The cat stretched in a **languid** manner before finally getting up to eat. <br> 3. The oppressive heat made everyone feel **languid** and unwilling to work. |
| **Capricious** | Adjective | Given to sudden and unaccountable changes of mood or behavior. | 1. The weather in the mountains is notoriously **capricious**, changing from sun to snow in minutes. <br> 2. Her **capricious** spending habits often left her short of money by the end of the month. <br> 3. The CEO's **capricious** decision to change the project's direction caused a lot of confusion among the team. |
| **Serendipity** | Noun | The occurrence and development of events by chance in a happy or beneficial way. | 1. Meeting his future business partner on a delayed flight was a moment of pure **serendipity**. <br> 2. The discovery of penicillin was a classic example of scientific **serendipity**. <br> 3. She believes that **serendipity** plays a large role in her success, as she often stumbles upon great opportunities. |
| **Quixotic** | Adjective | Exceedingly idealistic; unrealistic and impractical. | 1. His **quixotic** quest to save every stray animal in the city was admirable but financially impossible. <br> 2. The young inventor pursued a **quixotic** dream of building a perpetual motion machine. <br> 3. She often laughed at his **quixotic** notions, but secretly admired his unwavering optimism. |
| **Pernicious** | Adjective | Having a harmful effect, especially in a gradual or subtle way. | 1. The doctor warned him about the **pernicious** effects of smoking on his lungs. <br> 2. The spread of misinformation can have a **pernicious** influence on public opinion. <br> 3. They fought against the **pernicious** rumors that were damaging the company's reputation. |
| **Ebullient** | Adjective | Cheerful and full of energy. | 1. The winner's **ebullient** mood was contagious, and soon the whole team was celebrating. <br> 2. She gave an **ebullient** performance that captivated the entire audience. <br> 3. Despite the setback, his **ebullient** spirit never wavered, and he quickly started planning his next move. |
| **Inchoate** | Adjective | Just begun and so not fully formed or developed; rudimentary. | 1. The project is still in an **inchoate** stage, and we have many details to work out. <br> 2. He had an **inchoate** idea for a novel, but the plot and characters were still vague. <br> 3. The philosopher struggled to articulate his **inchoate** thoughts into a coherent theory. |
| **Insidious** | Adjective | Proceeding in a gradual, subtle way, but with harmful effects. | 1. The disease is often hard to detect because its onset is so **insidious**. <br> 2. He was unaware of the **insidious** corruption that was slowly taking over the organization. <br> 3. The advertising campaign used an **insidious** method to make consumers feel inadequate without the product. |
| **Voracious** | Adjective | Having a very eager approach to an activity; or, wanting or devouring great quantities of food. | 1. He is a **voracious** reader, finishing several books a week. <br> 2. The teenage boy had a **voracious** appetite and could eat an entire pizza by himself. <br> 3. Her **voracious** curiosity led her to study a wide range of subjects in college. |
| **Cacophony** | Noun | A harsh, discordant mixture of sounds. | 1. The street market was a **cacophony** of shouting vendors, car horns, and loud music. <br> 2. The orchestra's warm-up session was a brief **cacophony** before the conductor raised his baton. <br> 3. The meeting descended into a **cacophony** of angry voices and conflicting opinions. |
| **Paucity** | Noun | The presence of something only in small or insufficient quantities or amounts; scarcity. | 1. The **paucity** of evidence made it difficult for the jury to reach a verdict. <br> 2. Due to the drought, there was a **paucity** of fresh water in the region. <br> 3. The company suffered from a **paucity** of new ideas, leading to stagnant growth. |
| **Salient** | Adjective | Most noticeable or important. | 1. The **salient** feature of the new phone is its incredibly long battery life. <br> 2. She summarized the **salient** points of the report for the executive board. <br> 3. His height was the most **salient** characteristic that made him stand out in the crowd. |
| **Plethora** | Noun | A large or excessive amount of something. | 1. The bookstore offered a **plethora** of titles on every conceivable subject. <br> 2. We received a **plethora** of applications for the single job opening. <br> 3. Despite the **plethora** of data, the analysts struggled to find a clear trend. |
| **Superfluous** | Adjective | Unnecessary, especially through being more than enough. | 1. The report contained a lot of **superfluous** details that could have been omitted. <br> 2. After the main course, the dessert felt entirely **superfluous**, but she ate it anyway. <br> 3. Removing the **superfluous** furniture made the small apartment feel much more spacious. |
| **Disparate** | Adjective | Essentially different in kind; not allowing comparison. | 1. The team was composed of people with **disparate** skills, from coding to graphic design. <br> 2. The museum displayed a collection of **disparate** artifacts from various cultures and time periods. <br> 3. They tried to reconcile the two **disparate** accounts of the accident, but the details simply didn't match. |
| **Sycophant** | Noun | A person who acts obsequiously toward someone important in order to gain advantage. | 1. The manager was surrounded by **sycophants** who constantly praised his every decision. <br> 2. She was tired of the **sycophant** in the office who was always trying to curry favor with the boss. <br> 3. The politician dismissed the criticism as the work of a few disgruntled former **sycophants**. |
