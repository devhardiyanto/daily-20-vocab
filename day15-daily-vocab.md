# Daily Vocabulary - Day 15

Here are 20 new English vocabulary words, complete with their definitions and three contextual example sentences for each.

| No. | Word | Definition | Example Sentences |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Ephemeral** | Lasting for a very short time. | 1. The beauty of the cherry blossoms is **ephemeral**, lasting only a few days.<br>2. His fame as a child actor proved to be **ephemeral**; he was soon forgotten.<br>3. We should cherish these **ephemeral** moments of peace before the storm. |
| 2 | **Mellifluous** | (Of a voice or words) sweet or musical; pleasant to hear. | 1. The singer's **mellifluous** voice soothed the audience.<br>2. He delivered the poem in a **mellifluous** tone that captivated everyone.<br>3. The sound of the harp was surprisingly **mellifluous** in the large, echoing hall. |
| 3 | **Ubiquitous** | Present, appearing, or found everywhere. | 1. In the modern world, smartphones have become truly **ubiquitous**.<br>2. The company's logo is **ubiquitous** at every major sporting event.<br>3. The problem of plastic waste is **ubiquitous** across all oceans. |
| 4 | **Serendipity** | The occurrence and development of events by chance in a happy or beneficial way. | 1. Meeting my old friend in a foreign country was a moment of pure **serendipity**.<br>2. The discovery of penicillin was a classic case of scientific **serendipity**.<br>3. She found the perfect vintage dress by sheer **serendipity** while browsing a small shop. |
| 5 | **Petrichor** | A pleasant, earthy smell after rain. | 1. After the long drought, the sudden rain brought a welcome scent of **petrichor**.<br>2. The smell of **petrichor** always reminds me of my childhood summers.<br>3. Scientists have studied the chemical compounds responsible for the distinct aroma of **petrichor**. |
| 6 | **Quixotic** | Extremely idealistic; unrealistic and impractical. | 1. His **quixotic** quest to save every stray animal in the city was admirable but impossible.<br>2. The startup's business plan was criticized as being too **quixotic** to succeed.<br>3. She had a **quixotic** belief that love could solve all the world's problems. |
| 7 | **Languid** | Displaying or having a disinclination for physical exertion or effort; slow and relaxed. | 1. They spent a **languid** afternoon by the pool, sipping iced tea.<br>2. The cat stretched in a **languid** manner, seemingly unconcerned with the world.<br>3. The patient's movements were **languid** after the long illness. |
| 8 | **Capricious** | Given to sudden and unaccountable changes of mood or behavior. | 1. The weather in the mountains is notoriously **capricious**.<br>2. Her **capricious** spending habits often left her short of money.<br>3. A **capricious** ruler can destabilize an entire nation with a single decree. |
| 9 | **Halcyon** | Denoting a period of time in the past that was idyllically happy and peaceful. | 1. She often reminisced about the **halcyon** days of her youth.<br>2. The company's **halcyon** years ended abruptly with the market crash.<br>3. We enjoyed a week of **halcyon** weather at the beach. |
| 10 | **Nefarious** | (Typically of an action or activity) wicked or criminal. | 1. The villain's **nefarious** plot was foiled by the quick-thinking detective.<br>2. The organization was involved in various **nefarious** activities, including smuggling.<br>3. He was arrested for his **nefarious** scheme to defraud elderly people. |
| 11 | **Ebullient** | Cheerful and full of energy. | 1. The winner was in an **ebullient** mood after receiving the award.<br>2. Her **ebullient** personality made her the life of every party.<br>3. The team's victory was celebrated with **ebullient** cheers from the fans. |
| 12 | **Pernicious** | Having a harmful effect, especially in a gradual or subtle way. | 1. The **pernicious** effects of social media on mental health are becoming clear.<br>2. He warned against the **pernicious** influence of propaganda.<br>3. The plant is known to have a **pernicious** toxin that affects the nervous system. |
| 13 | **Cacophony** | A harsh, discordant mixture of sounds. | 1. A **cacophony** of car horns and shouting filled the busy street.<br>2. The orchestra tuning up created a brief **cacophony** before the concert began.<br>3. The debate descended into a **cacophony** of angry voices. |
| 14 | **Inchoate** | Just begun and so not fully formed or developed; rudimentary. | 1. The project is still in an **inchoate** stage, with many details yet to be decided.<br>2. He had an **inchoate** idea for a novel, but the plot was still vague.<br>3. The government is struggling to deal with the **inchoate** demands of the protesters. |
| 15 | **Sycophant** | A person who acts obsequiously toward someone important in order to gain advantage. | 1. The manager was surrounded by **sycophants** who constantly praised his every decision.<br>2. She despised the **sycophant** who tried to flatter the professor for a better grade.<br>3. His rise to power was aided by a network of loyal **sycophants**. |
| 16 | **Insidious** | Proceeding in a gradual, subtle way, but with harmful effects. | 1. The **insidious** disease can progress for years without noticeable symptoms.<br>2. The company's marketing strategy was an **insidious** attempt to manipulate young consumers.<br>3. He was unaware of the **insidious** nature of the rumors being spread about him. |
| 17 | **Parsimonious** | Extremely unwilling to spend money or use resources; stingy or frugal. | 1. The company was too **parsimonious** to invest in necessary new equipment.<br>2. She is so **parsimonious** that she reuses tea bags.<br>3. A **parsimonious** approach to budgeting can sometimes lead to greater long-term costs. |
| 18 | **Malinger** | Exaggerate or feign illness in order to escape duty or work. | 1. The soldier was accused of **malingering** to avoid the difficult training exercise.<br>2. The doctor suspected the patient was **malingering** to get a prescription.<br>3. Constant complaints about minor ailments led his colleagues to believe he was **malingering**. |
| 19 | **Salient** | Most noticeable or important. | 1. The **salient** feature of the new phone is its improved camera.<br>2. He summarized the **salient** points of the report in a brief presentation.<br>3. The most **salient** difference between the two proposals is the cost. |
| 20 | **Paucity** | The presence of something only in small or insufficient quantities or amounts; scarcity. | 1. There is a **paucity** of evidence to support the claim.<br>2. The region suffers from a **paucity** of clean drinking water.<br>3. A **paucity** of qualified candidates made the hiring process difficult. |
