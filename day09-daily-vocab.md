# Day 09: Daily Vocabulary

| Word | Part of Speech | Definition | Contextual Examples |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Ephemeral** | Adjective | Lasting for a very short time. | 1. The beauty of the cherry blossoms is **ephemeral**, lasting only a few weeks. <br> 2. Fashion trends are often **ephemeral**, quickly replaced by new styles. <br> 3. He captured the **ephemeral** moment of the shooting star with his camera. |
| **Ubiquitous** | Adjective | Present, appearing, or found everywhere. | 1. In the modern world, smartphones have become truly **ubiquitous**. <br> 2. The company's logo is **ubiquitous**; you see it on every street corner. <br> 3. The fear of job loss is **ubiquitous** in the current economic climate. |
| **Mellifluous** | Adjective | (Of a voice or words) sweet or musical; pleasant to hear. | 1. The singer's **mellifluous** voice captivated the entire audience. <br> 2. She spoke in a **mellifluous** tone, making the complex subject easy to understand. <br> 3. The sound of the harp was **mellifluous** and calming. |
| **Pernicious** | Adjective | Having a harmful effect, especially in a gradual or subtle way. | 1. The **pernicious** effects of misinformation can erode public trust. <br> 2. He warned against the **pernicious** influence of negative peer pressure. <br> 3. Smoking is a **pernicious** habit that slowly damages the lungs. |
| **Serendipity** | Noun | The occurrence and development of events by chance in a happy or beneficial way. | 1. Meeting my old friend in a foreign city was a moment of pure **serendipity**. <br> 2. Scientific breakthroughs often rely on a combination of hard work and **serendipity**. <br> 3. The discovery of penicillin was a famous example of **serendipity**. |
| **Cacophony** | Noun | A harsh, discordant mixture of sounds. | 1. The construction site was a **cacophony** of drills, hammers, and shouting. <br> 2. The orchestra tuning up created a brief **cacophony** before the conductor arrived. <br> 3. The meeting descended into a **cacophony** of angry voices. |
| **Quixotic** | Adjective | Exceedingly idealistic; unrealistic and impractical. | 1. His **quixotic** quest to save the abandoned factory was admirable but doomed to fail. <br> 2. She has a **quixotic** belief in the perfectibility of human nature. <br> 3. The young inventor pursued a **quixotic** project with boundless enthusiasm. |
| **Languid** | Adjective | (Of a person, manner, or gesture) slow and relaxed; disinclined to exertion. | 1. The hot summer afternoon made everyone feel **languid** and sleepy. <br> 2. She gave a **languid** wave from the hammock. <br> 3. The cat stretched out in a **languid** manner on the sun-drenched windowsill. |
| **Fastidious** | Adjective | Very attentive to and concerned about accuracy and detail. | 1. He is a **fastidious** editor who catches every tiny grammatical error. <br> 2. The chef was **fastidious** about the presentation of the dish. <br> 3. Maintaining a **fastidious** record of expenses is crucial for a small business. |
| **Egregious** | Adjective | Outstandingly bad; shocking. | 1. The judge called the defendant's actions an **egregious** abuse of power. <br> 2. Failing to back up your data is an **egregious** mistake in this digital age. <br> 3. The company was fined for its **egregious** violation of environmental regulations. |
| **Garrulous** | Adjective | Excessively talkative, especially on trivial matters. | 1. The **garrulous** passenger kept talking throughout the entire train journey. <br> 2. He was known for his **garrulous** nature, often dominating every conversation. <br> 3. The usually quiet man became surprisingly **garrulous** after a few glasses of wine. |
| **Misanthrope** | Noun | A person who dislikes humankind and avoids human society. | 1. After years of betrayal, the old man became a bitter **misanthrope**. <br> 2. The character of Scrooge is a classic literary example of a **misanthrope**. <br> 3. Despite his reputation as a **misanthrope**, he secretly donated to several charities. |
| **Alacrity** | Noun | Brisk and cheerful readiness. | 1. She accepted the new project with **alacrity** and enthusiasm. <br> 2. The staff responded to the customer's request with surprising **alacrity**. <br> 3. He jumped out of bed with **alacrity** on the morning of his birthday. |
| **Platitude** | Noun | A remark or statement, especially one with a moral content, that has been used too often to be interesting or thoughtful. | 1. The politician's speech was full of empty **platitudes** about change and hope. <br> 2. I was tired of hearing the same old **platitudes** about hard work leading to success. <br> 3. She offered a **platitude** about "time healing all wounds," which did little to comfort him. |
| **Venerable** | Adjective | Accorded a great deal of respect, especially because of age, wisdom, or character. | 1. The **venerable** professor had been teaching at the university for over fifty years. <br> 2. They visited the **venerable** ruins of the ancient temple. <br> 3. The community looked up to the **venerable** elder for guidance. |
| **Incongruous** | Adjective | Not in harmony or keeping with the surroundings or other aspects of something. | 1. The sight of a tuxedo at a beach party was utterly **incongruous**. <br> 2. Her bright pink hair seemed **incongruous** with her conservative business suit. <br> 3. The modern sculpture looked **incongruous** in the medieval courtyard. |
| **Disparate** | Adjective | Essentially different in kind; not allowing comparison. | 1. The team was composed of people with **disparate** skills and backgrounds. <br> 2. We need to find a way to reconcile these two **disparate** viewpoints. <br> 3. The novel brings together several **disparate** storylines into a cohesive whole. |
| **Vacillate** | Verb | Alternate or waver between different opinions or actions; be indecisive. | 1. He tends to **vacillate** between two career paths, unable to make a final decision. <br> 2. The committee continued to **vacillate** on the proposed policy changes. <br> 3. Her mood would **vacillate** wildly from extreme joy to deep sadness. |
| **Mitigate** | Verb | Make less severe, serious, or painful. | 1. Emergency aid is being sent to **mitigate** the effects of the drought. <br> 2. They implemented new policies to **mitigate** the risk of data breaches. <br> 3. A good apology can sometimes **mitigate** the damage caused by a mistake. |
| **Ostracize** | Verb | Exclude (someone) from a society or group. | 1. The community decided to **ostraciize** the family after the scandal. <br> 2. He felt **ostracized** by his colleagues after voicing a dissenting opinion. <br> 3. Bullying often involves attempts to **ostraciize** a victim from their social circle. |
