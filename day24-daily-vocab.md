# Day 24: Daily Vocabulary

| Word | Definition | Contextual Examples |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Ephemeral** | Lasting for a very short time. | 1. The beauty of the cherry blossoms is **ephemeral**, lasting only a few weeks. <br> 2. Fashion trends are often **ephemeral**, quickly replaced by new styles. <br> 3. He captured the **ephemeral** moment of the shooting star with his camera. |
| **Ubiquitous** | Present, appearing, or found everywhere. | 1. In the modern world, smartphones have become truly **ubiquitous**. <br> 2. The company's logo is **ubiquitous** at every major sporting event. <br> 3. The smell of pine needles was **ubiquitous** in the forest. |
| **Mellifluous** | (Of a voice or words) sweet or musical; pleasant to hear. | 1. The singer's **mellifluous** voice soothed the audience. <br> 2. He delivered the poem in a deep, **mellifluous** tone. <br> 3. The sound of the harp was **mellifluous** and calming. |
| **Petrichor** | A pleasant, earthy smell that often accompanies the first rain after a long period of warm, dry weather. | 1. After the summer storm, the distinct scent of **petrichor** filled the air. <br> 2. She loved the smell of **petrichor** rising from the hot pavement. <br> 3. The word **petrichor** comes from the Greek words *petra* (stone) and *ichor* (the fluid that flows in the veins of the gods). |
| **Languid** | Displaying or having a disinclination for physical exertion or effort; slow and relaxed. | 1. A **languid** afternoon was spent reading a book by the pool. <br> 2. The cat stretched in a **languid** manner, clearly enjoying the sun. <br> 3. Her **languid** movements suggested a complete lack of urgency. |
| **Quixotic** | Exceedingly idealistic; unrealistic and impractical. | 1. His **quixotic** quest to save every stray animal in the city was admirable but costly. <br> 2. The politician's promises were dismissed as **quixotic** by the opposition. <br> 3. She had a **quixotic** belief that love could solve all the world's problems. |
| **Serendipity** | The occurrence and development of events by chance in a happy or beneficial way. | 1. Meeting my old friend in a foreign city was a moment of pure **serendipity**. <br> 2. Scientific breakthroughs often involve a degree of **serendipity**. <br> 3. The discovery of penicillin was a famous example of **serendipity**. |
| **Innuendo** | An allusive or oblique remark or hint, typically a suggestive or disparaging one. | 1. The article was full of malicious **innuendo** about the celebrity's private life. <br> 2. He communicated his disapproval through subtle **innuendo** rather than direct criticism. <br> 3. The comedian's routine relied heavily on clever **innuendo**. |
| **Resilience** | The capacity to recover quickly from difficulties; toughness. | 1. The community showed great **resilience** in rebuilding after the devastating hurricane. <br> 2. Children often demonstrate remarkable **resilience** in the face of change. <br> 3. The company's financial **resilience** allowed it to weather the economic downturn. |
| **Voracious** | Having a very eager approach to an activity; or, wanting or devouring great quantities of food. | 1. He is a **voracious** reader, finishing several books a week. <br> 2. The **voracious** appetite of the young athlete required a high-calorie diet. <br> 3. The startup had a **voracious** need for new talent and investment. |
| **Cacophony** | A harsh, discordant mixture of sounds. | 1. The city street was a **cacophony** of car horns and shouting vendors. <br> 2. The orchestra's warm-up created a brief **cacophony** before the conductor arrived. <br> 3. The meeting descended into a **cacophony** of conflicting opinions. |
| **Pensive** | Engaged in, involving, or reflecting deep or serious thought. | 1. He looked **pensive**, staring out the window at the falling rain. <br> 2. The artist's portrait captured her **pensive** mood perfectly. <br> 3. After the long discussion, she sat in **pensive** silence. |
| **Ethereal** | Extremely delicate and light in a way that seems too perfect for this world. | 1. The bride wore a dress of white lace that gave her an **ethereal** appearance. <br> 2. The northern lights created an **ethereal** glow across the night sky. <br> 3. Her music has an **ethereal** quality, almost like a dream. |
| **Idiosyncrasy** | A mode of behavior or way of thought peculiar to an individual. | 1. One of his charming **idiosyncrasies** is his habit of wearing mismatched socks. <br> 2. The car's braking system has a few minor **idiosyncrasies** that take getting used to. <br> 3. Every language has its own grammatical **idiosyncrasies**. |
| **Ineffable** | Too great or extreme to be expressed or described in words. | 1. The beauty of the Grand Canyon is almost **ineffable**. <br> 2. He felt an **ineffable** sense of peace after meditating. <br> 3. The joy of holding her newborn child was an **ineffable** experience. |
| **Juxtaposition** | The fact of two things being seen or placed close together with contrasting effect. | 1. The artist used the **juxtaposition** of bright colors and dark shadows to create a dramatic effect. <br> 2. The novel's power comes from the **juxtaposition** of wealth and poverty. <br> 3. The photograph showed a striking **juxtaposition** of an ancient ruin and a modern skyscraper. |
| **Ostracize** | Exclude (someone) from a society or group. | 1. The other children began to **ostracize** him after he betrayed their secret. <br> 2. She felt **ostracized** by her colleagues after she reported the misconduct. <br> 3. Historically, communities would **ostracize** those who broke social taboos. |
| **Perfunctory** | (Of an action or gesture) carried out with a minimum of effort or reflection. | 1. He gave a **perfunctory** nod of acknowledgment before returning to his work. <br> 2. The security guard conducted a **perfunctory** check of the bags. <br> 3. Her apology was **perfunctory** and lacked any real sincerity. |
| **Ruminate** | Think deeply about something. | 1. She spent the weekend **ruminating** on the difficult decision she had to make. <br> 2. The philosopher would often **ruminate** for hours in his study. <br> 3. It's important to **ruminate** on your mistakes to learn from them. |
| **Salient** | Most noticeable or important. | 1. The **salient** feature of the new phone is its improved battery life. <br> 2. He summarized the **salient** points of the report for the executive team. <br> 3. Her experience in crisis management was the most **salient** factor in her hiring. |
