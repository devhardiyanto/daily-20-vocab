# Day 108: Daily Vocabulary

| Word | Definition | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Apparent** | Something that is obvious, visible, or clear — but not necessarily true or factual. | 1. It was apparent to me that he was a trained dancer, but the others had no idea. 2. The solution to the puzzle became apparent after a few minutes of thought. 3. Her discomfort was apparent to everyone in the room. |
| **Aspect** | A part of something that you can consider or observe. | 1. The movie correctly portrayed the different aspects of living in a large city. 2. We need to consider every aspect of the problem before making a decision. 3. One aspect of his personality that I admire is his honesty. |
| **Derive** | Something derived comes from something else. | 1. Vegans do not eat products derived from animals. 2. Many English words derive from Latin. 3. She derived great pleasure from helping others. |
| **Facilitate** | To make a process easier. | 1. My lawyer facilitated the process of purchasing our home. 2. The new software will facilitate communication between team members. 3. A good leader should facilitate discussion, not dominate it. |
| **Monitor** | To check on its welfare, condition, or status over time. | 1. I called my mom to help monitor the children while we were painting our home. 2. Doctors constantly monitor the patient's vital signs. 3. The government monitors internet traffic for security reasons. |
| **Proportion** | The relationship or balance between parts of a whole. | 1. We tried to give away equal amounts of food to everyone, but our proportions were off. 2. The human body is drawn in perfect proportion. 3. A large proportion of the population lives in urban areas. |
| **Pursue** | To follow or proceed toward a goal with the intention of achieving it. | 1. Doug was set on pursuing his goal of being a world-renowned chef. 2. She decided to pursue a career in medicine. 3. The police pursued the suspect through the narrow streets. |
| **Retain** | To keep or hold onto something. | 1. The play ran for 20 years but always seemed to retain its novelty. 2. It's important to retain a sense of humor, even in difficult times. 3. The company struggled to retain its best employees. |
| **Theory** | A general idea, belief, or explanation about something. | 1. She said she had a theory that aliens from Mars visited the Earth centuries ago. 2. The scientist proposed a new theory about the origin of the universe. 3. His theory was based on years of research and experimentation. |
| **Underlying** | Describing something that is literally beneath something else, or refers to the root or fundamental cause of something. | 1. The little girl had a fever, but the tests found no underlying cause for her symptoms. 2. The underlying problem is a lack of communication. 3. There's an underlying tension in their relationship. |
| **Adapt** | To adjust to a new situation or environment. | 1. Soldiers have the ability to adapt to many different conditions and environments. 2. It's important to adapt to new technologies to stay competitive. 3. The chameleon can adapt its color to blend in with its surroundings. |
| **Adjacent** | Next to or very near something. | 1. Two houses side-by-side are adjacent. 2. The park is adjacent to the library. 3. The classroom is adjacent to the principal's office. |
| **Ambiguous** | Open to more than one interpretation; having a double meaning. | 1. The politician's statement was ambiguous, leaving everyone unsure of his true stance. 2. The ending of the movie was ambiguous, allowing viewers to draw their own conclusions. 3. Avoid using ambiguous language in legal documents. |
| **Coherent** | Logical and consistent; forming a united whole. | 1. The essay was well-written and presented a coherent argument. 2. After a good night's sleep, I was able to form a coherent thought. 3. The team worked together to develop a coherent strategy. |
| **Conjecture** | An opinion or conclusion formed on the basis of incomplete information. | 1. The detective's theory was pure conjecture, lacking any concrete evidence. 2. His remarks were dismissed as mere conjecture. 3. We can only conjecture about the future of technology. |
| **Diligent** | Having or showing care and conscientiousness in one's work or duties. | 1. She was a diligent student, always completing her assignments on time. 2. His diligent efforts led to the success of the project. 3. The police conducted a diligent investigation into the crime. |
| **Elaborate** | Developed or executed with care and in detail; intricate and rich in detail. | 1. The wedding cake was an elaborate creation with multiple tiers and intricate decorations. 2. He presented an elaborate plan for the new marketing campaign. 3. The artist created an elaborate tapestry. |
| **Inherent** | Existing in something as a permanent, essential, or characteristic attribute. | 1. The desire for freedom is inherent in human nature. 2. There are inherent risks in any investment. 3. The job has its own inherent difficulties. |
| **Plausible** | (Of an argument or statement) seeming reasonable or probable. | 1. Her excuse for being late was plausible, so I believed her. 2. The plot of the movie was not very plausible. 3. He offered a plausible explanation for his absence. |
| **Subtle** | (Especially of a change or distinction) so delicate or precise as to be difficult to analyze or describe. | 1. There was a subtle change in her expression that I almost missed. 2. The artist used subtle colors to create a sense of depth. 3. He made a subtle hint about wanting a raise. |
