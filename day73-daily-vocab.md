# Day 73: Daily English Vocabulary

| Word | Part of Speech | Definition | Example 1 | Example 2 | Example 3 |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Nonchalant** | Adjective | (of a person or manner) feeling or appearing casually calm and relaxed; not displaying anxiety, interest, or enthusiasm. | She gave a **nonchalant** shrug when asked about her exam results. | He tried to appear **nonchalant** despite the pressure of the interview. | The cat walked across the busy street in a surprisingly **nonchalant** manner. |
| **Nostalgia** | Noun | A sentimental longing or wistful affection for the period in the past. | Seeing the old playground filled him with a deep sense of **nostalgia**. | The movie relies heavily on 80s **nostalgia** to attract its audience. | We spent the evening in **nostalgia**, looking through old family photo albums. |
| **Notorious** | Adjective | Famous or well known, typically for some bad quality or deed. | The city is **notorious** for its heavy traffic during rush hour. | He was a **notorious** gambler who lost several fortunes at the casino. | The company became **notorious** for its poor treatment of employees. |
| **Novelty** | Noun | The quality of being new, original, or unusual. | The **novelty** of the new job soon wore off, and he settled into a routine. | Electric cars were once a **novelty**, but now they are becoming common. | The store sells various **novelties** and souvenirs for tourists. |
| **Nuance** | Noun | A subtle difference in or shade of meaning, expression, or sound. | The actor's performance captured every **nuance** of the character's complex emotions. | Without understanding the cultural **nuances**, it is easy to misinterpret the gesture. | A skilled translator must be able to convey the **nuances** of the original text. |
| **Nullify** | Verb | Make legally null and void; invalidate. | The court's decision effectively **nullified** the previous agreement. | The new evidence could **nullify** the prosecution's entire case. | Drinking alcohol can **nullify** the effects of certain medications. |
| **Obdurate** | Adjective | Stubbornly refusing to change one's opinion or course of action. | Despite the evidence against him, he remained **obdurate** in his denial. | The negotiator found the opposing side to be surprisingly **obdurate**. | No amount of persuasion could move the **obdurate** old man from his position. |
| **Obeisance** | Noun | Deferential respect. | They paid **obeisance** to the king as he entered the grand hall. | In many cultures, a bow is a traditional sign of **obeisance**. | The young monk showed deep **obeisance** to his spiritual teacher. |
| **Obfuscate** | Verb | Render obscure, unclear, or unintelligible. | The politician's vague answers seemed designed to **obfuscate** the truth. | Technical jargon can often **obfuscate** simple concepts for the general public. | Do not try to **obfuscate** the issue with irrelevant details. |
| **Objective** | Adjective | (of a person or their judgment) not influenced by personal feelings or opinions in considering and representing facts. | A journalist must strive to provide an **objective** account of the news. | It is difficult to remain **objective** when the situation involves your own family. | The committee's goal is to conduct an **objective** review of the project. |
| **Obliterate** | Verb | Destroy utterly; wipe out. | The heavy rainfall threatened to **obliterate** the ancient ruins. | The memory of the accident was so traumatic that his mind tried to **obliterate** it. | The new highway will **obliterate** several small villages in its path. |
| **Oblivion** | Noun | The state of being unaware or unconscious of what is happening. | He drank himself into **oblivion** to forget his mounting troubles. | Many once-famous brands have faded into **oblivion** over the years. | The old house sat in a state of **oblivion**, forgotten by the rest of the world. |
| **Obscure** | Adjective | Not discovered or known about; uncertain. | The origins of the ancient language remain **obscure** to modern scholars. | He lived an **obscure** life in a remote village, away from the public eye. | The meaning of the poem is somewhat **obscure** and open to interpretation. |
| **Obsequious** | Adjective | Obedient or attentive to an excessive or servile degree. | The waiter's **obsequious** behavior was more annoying than helpful. | He was surrounded by **obsequious** assistants who agreed with everything he said. | Her **obsequious** flattery did not fool the manager for a second. |
| **Obsolescence** | Noun | The process of becoming obsolete or outdated and no longer used. | Planned **obsolescence** is a strategy used by manufacturers to encourage frequent upgrades. | The rapid pace of technology leads to the quick **obsolescence** of electronic gadgets. | The factory was closed due to the **obsolescence** of its machinery. |
| **Obstinate** | Adjective | Stubbornly refusing to change one's opinion or chosen course of action, despite attempts to persuade one to do so. | The **obstinate** child refused to eat his vegetables no matter what. | Her **obstinate** refusal to compromise led to the breakdown of the negotiations. | He is so **obstinate** that he won't admit he's wrong even when proven so. |
| **Obtrude** | Verb | Become noticeable in an unwelcome or intrusive way. | I don't want to **obtrude** upon your private conversation. | The modern building seemed to **obtrude** upon the historic skyline. | He has a tendency to **obtrude** his opinions into every discussion. |
| **Obviate** | Verb | Remove (a need or difficulty). | The new medical treatment could **obviate** the need for surgery. | Careful planning can **obviate** many of the problems associated with travel. | The presence of a security guard helped to **obviate** the risk of theft. |
| **Occult** | Adjective | Supernatural, mystical, or magical beliefs, methods, or phenomena. | He spent years studying **occult** practices and ancient rituals. | The book explores the dark and **occult** history of the old mansion. | Many people are fascinated by the **occult** and the mysteries of the unknown. |
| **Odious** | Adjective | Extremely unpleasant; repulsive. | The dictator's **odious** crimes against humanity were finally exposed. | He found the task of cleaning the sewers to be utterly **odious**. | She couldn't stand his **odious** habit of interrupting everyone. |
