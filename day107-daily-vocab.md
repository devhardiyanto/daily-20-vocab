# Day 107: Daily Vocabulary

| Word | Definition | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Ephemeral** | Lasting for a very short time | 1. The ephemeral beauty of a sunset. 2. Fashion trends are often ephemeral. 3. His fame was ephemeral, lasting only a few months. |
| **Ubiquitous** | Present, appearing, or found everywhere | 1. Smartphones are ubiquitous in modern society. 2. The ubiquitous presence of advertising. 3. Coffee shops are becoming ubiquitous in urban areas. |
| **Serendipity** | The occurrence and development of events by chance in a happy or beneficial way | 1. It was pure serendipity that they met at the airport. 2. Discovering the old photograph was a moment of serendipity. 3. Serendipity played a role in the invention of penicillin. |
| **Mellifluous** | (Of a voice or words) sweet or musical; pleasant to hear | 1. Her mellifluous voice captivated the audience. 2. The mellifluous sound of the cello. 3. He spoke in a mellifluous tone, calming her fears. |
| **Luminous** | Emitting or reflecting light; shining | 1. The moon was a luminous orb in the night sky. 2. Her luminous eyes sparkled with joy. 3. The painting had a luminous quality. |
| **Benevolent** | Well meaning and kindly | 1. A benevolent smile graced her face. 2. The benevolent king ruled his people wisely. 3. He was known for his benevolent acts of charity. |
| **Capricious** | Given to sudden and unaccountable changes of mood or behavior | 1. Her capricious nature made her unpredictable. 2. The weather in the mountains can be very capricious. 3. He was a capricious leader, often changing his mind. |
| **Egalitarian** | Relating to or believing in the principle that all people are equal and deserve equal rights and opportunities | 1. An egalitarian society strives for equality for all. 2. The company had an egalitarian approach to management. 3. Her political views were strongly egalitarian. |
| **Fastidious** | Very attentive to and concerned about accuracy and detail | 1. He was fastidious about his appearance. 2. The fastidious chef meticulously prepared each dish. 3. She had a fastidious taste in art. |
| **Gregarious** | (Of a person) fond of company; sociable | 1. He was a gregarious host, always entertaining guests. 2. Gregarious animals live in herds. 3. Her gregarious personality made her many friends. |
| **Iconoclast** | A person who attacks cherished beliefs or institutions | 1. Steve Jobs was an iconoclast who revolutionized the tech industry. 2. The artist was an iconoclast, challenging traditional art forms. 3. He was an intellectual iconoclast, questioning established theories. |
| **Jubilant** | Feeling or expressing great happiness and triumph | 1. The team was jubilant after winning the championship. 2. She received the news with a jubilant shout. 3. The crowd was jubilant, celebrating the victory. |
| **Knell** | The sound of a bell, especially when rung solemnly for a death or funeral | 1. The church bells tolled a mournful knell. 2. The announcement sounded the knell for their hopes. 3. The defeat was the knell of his political career. |
| **Laconic** | (Of a person, speech, or style of writing) using very few words | 1. His laconic reply left her wondering. 2. The general was known for his laconic commands. 3. She had a laconic wit that was often misunderstood. |
| **Malediction** | A magical word or phrase uttered with the intention of bringing about evil or destruction; a curse | 1. The witch cast a malediction upon the prince. 2. He muttered a malediction under his breath. 3. The ancient tomb was said to carry a malediction. |
| **Nefarious** | (Typically of an action or activity) wicked or criminal | 1. The nefarious plot was uncovered by the police. 2. He was involved in nefarious activities. 3. The villain had a nefarious scheme to take over the world. |
| **Obfuscate** | Render obscure, unclear, or unintelligible | 1. Politicians often obfuscate the truth. 2. The complex jargon served to obfuscate the meaning. 3. He tried to obfuscate his involvement in the scandal. |
| **Pernicious** | Having a harmful effect, especially in a gradual or subtle way | 1. The pernicious effects of pollution on the environment. 2. She warned against the pernicious influence of social media. 3. The pernicious rumor spread quickly. |
| **Quixotic** | Extremely idealistic; unrealistic and impractical | 1. His quixotic quest to save the world. 2. She had a quixotic belief in the goodness of humanity. 3. The project was a quixotic endeavor with little chance of success. |
| **Rancor** | Bitterness or resentfulness, especially when long-standing | 1. He spoke with rancor about his former employer. 2. The dispute was marked by deep-seated rancor. 3. There was no rancor in her voice, only sadness. |
