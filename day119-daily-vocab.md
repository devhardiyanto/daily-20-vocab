# Day 119: Daily English Vocabulary

| No. | Word | Part of Speech | Definition | Example 1 | Example 2 | Example 3 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Ubiquitous** | Adjective | Present, appearing, or found everywhere. | Smartphones have become ubiquitous in modern society. | The ubiquitous presence of surveillance cameras raises privacy concerns. | Coffee shops are ubiquitous in urban areas. |
| 2 | **Ephemeral** | Adjective | Lasting for a very short time. | The beauty of a sunset is ephemeral, fading quickly into darkness. | Fashion trends are often ephemeral, changing with each season. | Childhood memories can be ephemeral, fading with age. |
| 3 | **Capricious** | Adjective | Given to sudden and unaccountable changes of mood or behavior. | Her capricious nature made it difficult to predict her reactions. | The weather in the mountains can be capricious, changing rapidly. | The stock market is often described as capricious due to its unpredictable fluctuations. |
| 4 | **Serendipity** | Noun | The occurrence and development of events by chance in a happy or beneficial way. | Finding a forgotten twenty-dollar bill in an old coat pocket was a moment of pure serendipity. | Their meeting was pure serendipity; they never would have crossed paths otherwise. | Serendipity played a role in the discovery of penicillin. |
| 5 | **Meticulous** | Adjective | Showing great attention to detail; very careful and precise. | The artist was meticulous in her brushstrokes, ensuring every detail was perfect. | He approached the task with meticulous care, double-checking every calculation. | A meticulous researcher leaves no stone unturned in their pursuit of knowledge. |
| 6 | **Perfunctory** | Adjective | (Of an action or gesture) carried out with a minimum of effort or reflection. | He gave a perfunctory nod of acknowledgment, clearly not interested. | The security guard conducted a perfunctory inspection of the bags. | Her perfunctory apology did not seem sincere. |
| 7 | **Gregarious** | Adjective | (Of a person) fond of company; sociable. | She was a gregarious person who loved attending social events. | Lions are gregarious animals, living in prides. | His gregarious nature made him an excellent host. |
| 8 | **Mellifluous** | Adjective | (Of a voice or words) sweet or musical; pleasant to hear. | Her mellifluous voice captivated the audience. | The mellifluous sounds of the harp filled the concert hall. | He spoke in a mellifluous tone, making his words sound like music. |
| 9 | **Obfuscate** | Verb | To make obscure, unclear, or unintelligible. | The politician tried to obfuscate the truth with vague statements. | The complex jargon only served to obfuscate the main point of the argument. | He deliberately obfuscated the issue to avoid taking responsibility. |
| 10 | **Panacea** | Noun | A solution or remedy for all difficulties or diseases. | There is no single panacea for all the world's problems. | Many people hoped that the new law would be a panacea for social inequality. | He believed that education was the panacea for poverty. |
| 11 | **Quiescent** | Adjective | In a state or period of inactivity or dormancy. | The volcano has been quiescent for many years. | The political situation remained quiescent after the protests. | The disease can remain quiescent for a long time before symptoms appear. |
| 12 | **Recalcitrant** | Adjective | Having an obstinately uncooperative attitude toward authority or discipline. | The recalcitrant student refused to follow the teacher's instructions. | The government faced a recalcitrant opposition in parliament. | The wild horse was recalcitrant and difficult to train. |
| 13 | **Salient** | Adjective | Most noticeable or important. | The salient features of the report were highlighted in the summary. | One of the salient differences between the two cultures is their approach to time. | The most salient point of his argument was the need for immediate action. |
| 14 | **Tantamount** | Adjective | Equivalent in seriousness to; virtually the same as. | His silence was tantamount to an admission of guilt. | Ignoring the warning signs would be tantamount to negligence. | Her refusal to cooperate was tantamount to an act of rebellion. |
| 15 | **Vacillate** | Verb | To waver between different opinions or actions; be indecisive. | He tends to vacillate when making important decisions. | The committee continued to vacillate on the proposed changes. | Her mood would vacillate between extreme happiness and deep sadness. |
| 16 | **Wane** | Verb | (Of the moon) have a progressively smaller part of its visible surface illuminated, so that it appears to decrease in size. | The moon began to wane after the full moon. | Her enthusiasm for the project began to wane after several setbacks. | The empire's power started to wane after centuries of dominance. |
| 17 | **Zephyr** | Noun | A soft, gentle breeze. | A gentle zephyr rustled through the leaves of the trees. | The zephyr carried the scent of blooming flowers across the meadow. | She enjoyed the cool zephyr on her face after a long, hot day. |
| 18 | **Abscond** | Verb | To leave hurriedly and secretly, typically to escape from custody or avoid arrest. | The suspect absconded with the stolen jewels. | He absconded from the country to avoid facing charges. | The cashier absconded with the day's takings. |
| 19 | **Banal** | Adjective | So lacking in originality as to be obvious and boring. | The movie's plot was so banal that I lost interest quickly. | His speech was filled with banal clichés and lacked any fresh ideas. | The conversation became banal after a while, with nothing new to discuss. |
| 20 | **Clandestine** | Adjective | Kept secret or done secretively, especially because illicit. | The couple had a clandestine meeting in a secluded cafe. | They conducted clandestine operations under the cover of darkness. | The government engaged in clandestine surveillance of its citizens. |
