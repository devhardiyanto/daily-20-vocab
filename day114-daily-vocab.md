# Day 114: Daily English Vocabulary

| No. | Word | Part of Speech | Definition | Example 1 | Example 2 | Example 3 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Ubiquitous | Adjective | Present, appearing, or found everywhere. | Smartphones are becoming increasingly ubiquitous in modern society. | The ubiquitous presence of surveillance cameras raises privacy concerns. | Coffee shops are ubiquitous in this city, you can find one on almost every corner. |
| 2 | Ephemeral | Adjective | Lasting for a very short time. | Fashion trends are often ephemeral, changing with each season. | The beauty of a sunset is ephemeral, lasting only a few moments. | His fame was ephemeral; he was a sensation for a year and then forgotten. |
| 3 | Serendipity | Noun | The occurrence and development of events by chance in a happy or beneficial way. | Finding that rare book at a small, obscure shop was pure serendipity. | Their meeting was a stroke of serendipity, leading to a lifelong friendship. | Serendipity played a role in the discovery of penicillin. |
| 4 | Quintessential | Adjective | Representing the most perfect or typical example of a quality or class. | A denim jacket is the quintessential casual wardrobe item. | His performance was the quintessential portrayal of the tragic hero. | The small, cozy cafe was the quintessential Parisian experience. |
| 5 | Mellifluous | Adjective | (Of a voice or words) sweet or musical; pleasant to hear. | The singer's mellifluous voice captivated the audience. | She spoke in a mellifluous tone that was very soothing. | The mellifluous sounds of the orchestra filled the concert hall. |
| 6 | Obfuscate | Verb | Render obscure, unclear, or unintelligible. | The politician tried to obfuscate the truth with vague statements. | Technical jargon can often obfuscate the meaning for a general audience. | He deliberately obfuscated his intentions to mislead his opponents. |
| 7 | Panacea | Noun | A solution or remedy for all difficulties or diseases. | Many people hope that technology will be a panacea for all of society's problems. | There is no single panacea for economic inequality. | The new drug was hailed as a panacea for various ailments. |
| 8 | Cacophony | Noun | A harsh, discordant mixture of sounds. | The construction site was a cacophony of drills and hammers. | A cacophony of barking dogs erupted in the neighborhood. | The orchestra produced a cacophony before tuning their instruments. |
| 9 | Luminous | Adjective | Emitting or reflecting light; shining. | The moon was a luminous orb in the night sky. | Her luminous smile brightened the entire room. | The deep-sea creatures had luminous organs that glowed in the dark. |
| 10 | Equanimity | Noun | Calmness and composure, especially in a difficult situation. | She faced the crisis with remarkable equanimity. | Maintaining equanimity during stressful times is a valuable skill. | His equanimity under pressure was admired by his colleagues. |
| 11 | Proliferate | Verb | Increase rapidly in numbers; multiply. | Rumors about the scandal began to proliferate. | Cell phones have proliferated in recent decades. | The weeds proliferated in the neglected garden. |
| 12 | Esoteric | Adjective | Intended for or likely to be understood by only a small number of people with a specialized knowledge or interest. | The book was filled with esoteric references that only scholars would understand. | His lectures were often too esoteric for the average student. | The ancient text contained esoteric symbols and rituals. |
| 13 | Fastidious | Adjective | Very attentive to and concerned about accuracy and detail. | He was fastidious about his appearance, always impeccably dressed. | The chef was fastidious about the quality of his ingredients. | She had a fastidious approach to her research, leaving no stone unturned. |
| 14 | Gregarious | Adjective | (Of a person) fond of company; sociable. | She was a gregarious person who loved hosting parties. | Gregarious animals tend to live in herds or flocks. | His gregarious nature made him popular among his classmates. |
| 15 | Idiosyncrasy | Noun | A peculiarity of constitution or temperament; an individualizing characteristic or quality. | One of his idiosyncrasies was his habit of always wearing mismatched socks. | The artist's work was full of unique idiosyncrasies. | Her refusal to eat vegetables was a strange idiosyncrasy. |
| 16 | Judicious | Adjective | Having, showing, or done with good judgment or sense. | It was a judicious decision to invest in renewable energy. | The committee made a judicious choice in selecting the new leader. | He exercised judicious restraint in his criticism. |
| 17 | Laconic | Adjective | (Of a person, speech, or style of writing) using very few words. | His laconic reply left us wondering what he really thought. | The detective was known for his laconic and observant nature. | She delivered a laconic but powerful speech. |
| 18 | Magnanimous | Adjective | Generous or forgiving, especially toward a rival or less powerful person. | The winning team was magnanimous in victory, praising their opponents. | Her magnanimous gesture of forgiveness surprised everyone. | He was a magnanimous leader who always put his people first. |
| 19 | Nefarious | Adjective | (Typically of an action or activity) wicked or criminal. | The nefarious plot to overthrow the government was uncovered. | He was involved in nefarious activities that led to his arrest. | The villain's nefarious schemes were always thwarted by the hero. |
| 20 | Obsequious | Adjective | Obedient or attentive to an excessive or servile degree. | The obsequious waiter hovered around the table, anticipating every need. | She disliked his obsequious flattery. | The new employee was overly obsequious to the boss. |
