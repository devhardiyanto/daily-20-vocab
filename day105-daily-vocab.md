# Day 105: Daily Vocabulary

| Word | Definition | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Ameliorate** | To make something bad or unsatisfactory better | 1. The new policies are designed to ameliorate the living conditions of the poor. 2. Efforts to ameliorate the situation were largely unsuccessful. 3. A hot cup of tea can often ameliorate a sore throat. |
| **Capitulate** | To surrender unconditionally or on stipulated terms | 1. The enemy was forced to capitulate after a long siege. 2. She refused to capitulate to their demands. 3. After hours of debate, he finally capitulated and agreed to their plan. |
| **Deleterious** | Causing harm or damage | 1. The deleterious effects of smoking are well-known. 2. Exposure to the sun can have deleterious consequences for your skin. 3. The chemical spill had a deleterious impact on the local ecosystem. |
| **Eschew** | To deliberately avoid using; abstain from | 1. He chose to eschew the limelight and live a quiet life. 2. Many religions eschew the consumption of certain foods. 3. To maintain a healthy lifestyle, one should eschew unhealthy habits. |
| **Fatuous** | Silly and pointless | 1. His fatuous remarks during the meeting annoyed everyone. 2. The fatuous argument failed to convince anyone. 3. She found his constant joking to be rather fatuous. |
| **Garrulous** | Excessively talkative, especially on trivial matters | 1. The garrulous old man told stories for hours. 2. Her garrulous nature made her a popular guest at parties. 3. He became garrulous after a few drinks. |
| **Hegemony** | Leadership or dominance, especially by one state or social group over others | 1. The cultural hegemony of Hollywood is undeniable. 2. The struggle for regional hegemony led to several conflicts. 3. The company sought to establish its hegemony in the market. |
| **Impecunious** | Having little or no money; penniless | 1. Despite his talent, the artist remained impecunious throughout his life. 2. The impecunious student struggled to pay for his tuition. 3. He came from an impecunious background but achieved great success. |
| **Jubilant** | Feeling or expressing great happiness and triumph | 1. The fans were jubilant after their team won the championship. 2. She received the news with a jubilant shout. 3. The jubilant crowd celebrated the victory late into the night. |
| **Knell** | The sound of a bell, especially when rung solemnly for a death or funeral; a signal of the end or failure of something | 1. The church bells tolled a mournful knell. 2. The announcement sounded the knell for their hopes of victory. 3. The company's bankruptcy was the knell of a once-great enterprise. |
| **Laconic** | (Of a person, speech, or style of writing) using very few words | 1. His laconic reply left her wondering what he really thought. 2. The detective was known for his laconic wit. 3. She preferred his laconic style of communication. |
| **Malediction** | A magical word or phrase uttered with the intention of bringing about evil or destruction; a curse | 1. The witch cast a malediction upon the prince. 2. He muttered a malediction under his breath. 3. The ancient tomb was said to carry a malediction. |
| **Nefarious** | (Typically of an action or activity) wicked or criminal | 1. The nefarious plot was uncovered by the police. 2. He was involved in nefarious activities. 3. The villain's nefarious schemes were always thwarted. |
| **Obfuscate** | To make obscure, unclear, or unintelligible | 1. Politicians often obfuscate the truth with vague statements. 2. His explanations only served to obfuscate the issue further. 3. The complex jargon was used to obfuscate the simple facts. |
| **Pernickety** | Placing too much emphasis on trivial details; fussy | 1. He was very pernickety about his clothes. 2. Her pernickety approach to grammar made her an excellent editor. 3. The chef was pernickety about the quality of his ingredients. |
| **Quixotic** | Extremely idealistic; unrealistic and impractical | 1. His quixotic quest to save the world was admirable but doomed. 2. She had a quixotic belief in the goodness of all people. 3. The project was a quixotic attempt to achieve the impossible. |
| **Recalcitrant** | Having an obstinately uncooperative attitude toward authority or discipline | 1. The recalcitrant student refused to follow the rules. 2. The government faced a recalcitrant opposition. 3. The donkey was recalcitrant and wouldn't move. |
| **Sycophant** | A person who acts obsequiously toward someone important in order to gain advantage | 1. The king was surrounded by sycophants who flattered him constantly. 2. She despised the sycophants who praised her boss excessively. 3. He was accused of being a sycophant to the powerful executive. |
| **Tirade** | A long, angry speech of criticism or accusation | 1. The manager launched into a tirade about the team's poor performance. 2. She endured his tirade in silence. 3. His speech quickly turned into a tirade against the government. |
| **Ubiquitous** | Present, appearing, or found everywhere | 1. Smartphones have become ubiquitous in modern society. 2. The ubiquitous presence of surveillance cameras is a concern for privacy advocates. 3. Coffee shops are ubiquitous in this city. |
