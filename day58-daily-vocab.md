# Day 58: Daily Vocabulary

| Word | Type | Definition | Example 1 | Example 2 | Example 3 |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Ubiquitous** | Adjective | Present, appearing, or found everywhere. | Smartphones have become **ubiquitous** in modern society, with almost everyone owning one. | The company's logo is **ubiquitous**, appearing on billboards, television, and social media. | In the digital age, data collection has become **ubiquitous** and often goes unnoticed. |
| **Venerable** | Adjective | Accorded a great deal of respect, especially because of age, wisdom, or character. | The **venerable** professor was honored for his fifty years of dedicated service to the university. | We visited a **venerable** institution that has been preserving local history for centuries. | The tradition is a **venerable** one, passed down through generations of the same family. |
| **Wary** | Adjective | Feeling or showing caution about possible dangers or problems. | Investors are increasingly **wary** of the volatile stock market and are seeking safer options. | She was **wary** of the stranger's intentions and kept a safe distance while speaking. | You should be **wary** of emails that ask for your personal information or passwords. |
| **Zealous** | Adjective | Having or showing zeal; great energy or enthusiasm in pursuit of a cause or an objective. | The **zealous** young activist spent all her free time campaigning for environmental protection. | He is a **zealous** supporter of the local football team, never missing a single match. | The detective was **zealous** in his pursuit of the truth, leaving no stone unturned. |
| **Alleviate** | Verb | Make (suffering, deficiency, or a problem) less severe. | The new medication helped to **alleviate** her chronic back pain within a few days. | The government implemented new policies to **alleviate** poverty in rural areas. | Adding more staff to the project will help to **alleviate** the workload on the current team. |
| **Belligerent** | Adjective | Hostile and aggressive. | His **belligerent** attitude during the meeting made it difficult to reach a peaceful resolution. | The two countries have been in a **belligerent** state for decades, with frequent border skirmishes. | She was shocked by the **belligerent** tone of the email she received from her former colleague. |
| **Complacent** | Adjective | Showing smug or uncritical satisfaction with oneself or one's achievements. | We cannot afford to become **complacent** about our success; we must continue to innovate. | The team's **complacent** attitude led to their unexpected defeat in the final game. | He grew **complacent** in his job and stopped looking for ways to improve his skills. |
| **Dormant** | Adjective | (Of an animal) having normal physical functions suspended or slowed down for a period of time; in or as if in a deep sleep. | The volcano has been **dormant** for hundreds of years, but scientists still monitor it closely. | Many seeds remain **dormant** in the soil until the conditions are right for them to sprout. | Her artistic talents lay **dormant** for years until she finally decided to take a painting class. |
| **Exacerbate** | Verb | Make (a problem, bad situation, or negative feeling) worse. | The humid weather only served to **exacerbate** his existing breathing difficulties. | Cutting the budget will only **exacerbate** the current crisis in the healthcare system. | His rude comments during the argument only served to **exacerbate** the tension between them. |
| **Frugal** | Adjective | Sparing or economical with regard to money or food. | She is very **frugal** with her expenses, always looking for the best deals and discounts. | He leads a **frugal** lifestyle, preferring to save his money rather than spend it on luxuries. | The company adopted a **frugal** approach to spending in order to survive the economic downturn. |
| **Gullible** | Adjective | Easily persuaded to believe something; credulous. | He is so **gullible** that he believed the story about the haunted house without question. | Scammers often target **gullible** individuals who are easily tricked by their elaborate schemes. | Don't be so **gullible**; you should always verify the information before you believe it. |
| **Hinder** | Verb | Create difficulties for (someone or something), resulting in delay or obstruction. | The heavy snowstorm began to **hinder** the progress of the rescue teams. | Lack of funding has continued to **hinder** the development of the new research facility. | Constant interruptions from his colleagues started to **hinder** his ability to finish the report. |
| **Innate** | Adjective | Inborn; natural. | She has an **innate** ability to connect with people and make them feel at ease. | Some people believe that musical talent is **innate**, while others think it can be learned. | His **innate** sense of direction helped him find his way back to the camp without a map. |
| **Jovial** | Adjective | Cheerful and friendly. | The host's **jovial** personality made everyone feel welcome at the dinner party. | We spent a **jovial** evening sharing stories and laughing with our old friends. | He is known for his **jovial** nature, always having a smile on his face and a joke to tell. |
| **Knack** | Noun | An acquired or natural skill at performing a task. | She has a **knack** for finding the perfect gift for every occasion. | He has a **knack** for explaining complex scientific concepts in a way that anyone can understand. | It takes a certain **knack** to get the old engine started on a cold morning. |
| **Lethargic** | Adjective | Affected by lethargy; sluggish and apathetic. | The hot and humid weather made everyone feel **lethargic** and unmotivated. | After a long day of hiking, he felt **lethargic** and just wanted to sleep. | The medication can cause you to feel **lethargic**, so avoid driving while taking it. |
| **Meticulous** | Adjective | Showing great attention to detail; very careful and precise. | The architect was **meticulous** in his planning, ensuring that every detail was perfect. | She is a **meticulous** researcher, always double-checking her sources and data. | The painting was executed with **meticulous** care, capturing every subtle nuance of light. |
| **Nurture** | Verb | Care for and encourage the growth or development of. | It is important to **nurture** a child's curiosity and encourage them to explore the world. | The mentor helped to **nurture** her talent and guide her through the early stages of her career. | We must **nurture** our relationships with friends and family to keep them strong. |
| **Ominous** | Adjective | Giving the impression that something bad or unpleasant is going to happen; threatening; inauspicious. | The dark, heavy clouds in the distance looked **ominous**, signaling an approaching storm. | There was an **ominous** silence in the room after the shocking news was announced. | The sudden drop in the company's stock price was seen as an **ominous** sign for its future. |
| **Pragmatic** | Adjective | Dealing with things sensibly and realistically in a way that is based on practical rather than theoretical considerations. | He took a **pragmatic** approach to the problem, focusing on what could be achieved immediately. | The government's decision was a **pragmatic** one, aimed at stabilizing the economy. | She is a **pragmatic** person who prefers to deal with facts rather than speculation. |
