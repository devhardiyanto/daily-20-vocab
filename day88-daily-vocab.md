# Day 88: Daily Vocabulary

| Word          | Definition                                     | Example 1                                                | Example 2                                                  | Example 3                                                    |
| :------------ | :--------------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------- | :--------------------------------------------------------- | :----------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Ubiquitous**    | Present, appearing, or found everywhere.       | Smartphones have become **ubiquitous** in modern society. | The company aims to make its product **ubiquitous**.       | Coffee shops are **ubiquitous** in this city.                |
| **Ephemeral**     | Lasting for a very short time.                 | Fashion trends are often **ephemeral**.                  | The beauty of a sunset is **ephemeral**.                   | Childhood memories can be surprisingly **ephemeral**.        |
| **Cacophony**     | A harsh, discordant mixture of sounds.         | The city traffic created a **cacophony** of horns.       | A **cacophony** of voices filled the crowded market.       | The orchestra produced a **cacophony** during rehearsal.     |
| **Benevolent**    | Well meaning and kindly.                       | The **benevolent** donor gave a large sum to charity.    | Her **benevolent** smile reassured the nervous child.      | He was known for his **benevolent** nature.                  |
| **Meticulous**    | Showing great attention to detail; very careful and precise. | The artist was **meticulous** in her brushstrokes.        | He prepared the report with **meticulous** accuracy.       | A **meticulous** planner, she left nothing to chance.        |
| **Pernicious**    | Having a harmful effect, especially in a gradual or subtle way. | The **pernicious** rumors damaged his reputation.         | Social media can have a **pernicious** influence on teens. | The drug had a **pernicious** effect on his health.          |
| **Gregarious**    | Fond of company; sociable.                     | She is a **gregarious** person who loves parties.         | Lions are **gregarious** animals, living in prides.         | His **gregarious** personality made him popular.             |
| **Ostentatious**  | Characterized by vulgar or pretentious display; designed to impress or attract notice. | He drove an **ostentatious** sports car.                 | Her **ostentatious** jewelry drew everyone's attention.    | The palace was decorated in an **ostentatious** style.       |
| **Capricious**    | Given to sudden and unaccountable changes of mood or behavior. | The weather in spring can be very **capricious**.          | Her **capricious** decisions often surprised her staff.     | He was known for his **capricious** temper.                  |
| **Sanctimonious** | Making a show of being morally superior to other people. | His **sanctimonious** pronouncements annoyed everyone.     | The politician's speech was full of **sanctimonious** platitudes. | She gave a **sanctimonious** lecture on morality.            |
| **Profligate**    | Recklessly extravagant or wasteful in the use of resources. | He was criticized for his **profligate** spending.        | The company's **profligate** use of funds led to its downfall. | A **profligate** lifestyle can lead to financial ruin.      |
| **Mellifluous**   | (Of a voice or words) sweet or musical; pleasant to hear. | Her **mellifluous** voice captivated the audience.         | The speaker had a **mellifluous** tone.                     | The song had a **mellifluous** melody.                       |
| **Obfuscate**     | Render obscure, unclear, or unintelligible.    | The lawyer tried to **obfuscate** the truth.              | His explanations only served to **obfuscate** the issue.    | Don't try to **obfuscate** the facts.                        |
| **Parsimonious**  | Unwilling to spend money or use resources; stingy or frugal. | The **parsimonious** landlord refused to make repairs.    | She was too **parsimonious** to buy new clothes.           | His **parsimonious** habits saved him a lot of money.        |
| **Equivocate**    | Use ambiguous language so as to conceal the truth or avoid committing oneself. | When asked about the scandal, the politician began to **equivocate**. | He tried to **equivocate** his way out of the difficult question. | Don't **equivocate**; just give me a straight answer.        |
| **Alacrity**      | Brisk and cheerful readiness.                  | She accepted the new challenge with **alacrity**.          | He responded to the request with **alacrity**.              | The team approached the project with great **alacrity**.     |
| **Paucity**       | The presence of something only in small or insufficient quantities or amounts; scarcity. | There was a **paucity** of evidence to support the claim. | The region suffered from a **paucity** of natural resources. | A **paucity** of jobs forced many to leave the area.         |
| **Recalcitrant**  | Having an obstinately uncooperative attitude toward authority or discipline. | The **recalcitrant** student refused to follow the rules. | The **recalcitrant** donkey would not move.                 | Managing **recalcitrant** employees can be challenging.      |
| **Surreptitious** | Kept secret, especially because it would not be approved of. | He made a **surreptitious** attempt to leave the room.    | She cast a **surreptitious** glance at her watch.          | The spy gathered information in a **surreptitious** manner.  |
| **Voracious**     | Having a very eager approach to an activity.   | He is a **voracious** reader, finishing several books a week. | A **voracious** appetite for knowledge.                    | The fire spread with **voracious** speed.                    |
