# Day 21: Daily 20 Vocabulary

| No | Word | Definition | Contextual Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Ephemeral | Lasting for a very short time. | 1. The beauty of the cherry blossoms is **ephemeral**, lasting only a few weeks. <br> 2. Childhood memories often feel **ephemeral** as we grow older. <br> 3. His fame as a one-hit wonder proved to be entirely **ephemeral**. |
| 2 | Ubiquitous | Present, appearing, or found everywhere. | 1. In the modern world, smartphones have become truly **ubiquitous**. <br> 2. The company's logo is **ubiquitous** across all major cities. <br> 3. Fear of job loss is a **ubiquitous** concern in the current economic climate. |
| 3 | Zenith | The time at which something is most powerful or successful. | 1. The Roman Empire reached its **zenith** in the second century AD. <br> 2. At the **zenith** of her career, she was the highest-paid actress in Hollywood. <br> 3. The sun was at its **zenith**, casting sharp shadows on the ground. |
| 4 | Nefarious | (Typically of an action or activity) wicked or criminal. | 1. The villain's **nefarious** plot to steal the crown was foiled by the hero. <br> 2. He was arrested for his involvement in a series of **nefarious** schemes. <br> 3. The hacker was known for his **nefarious** activities in the dark web. |
| 5 | Quixotic | Extremely idealistic; unrealistic and impractical. | 1. His **quixotic** quest to save every stray animal in the city was admirable but costly. <br> 2. She had a **quixotic** belief that she could change the entire corporate culture single-handedly. <br> 3. The project's goals were dismissed as **quixotic** by the more pragmatic board members. |
| 6 | Surreptitious | Kept secret, especially because it would not be approved of. | 1. They held a **surreptitious** meeting in the dead of night to discuss the merger. <br> 2. He cast a **surreptitious** glance at his watch during the long lecture. <br> 3. The spy made a **surreptitious** exchange of documents in the crowded market. |
| 7 | Pernicious | Having a harmful effect, especially in a gradual or subtle way. | 1. The **pernicious** effects of social media on mental health are a growing concern. <br> 2. She warned against the **pernicious** influence of gossip in the workplace. <br> 3. The invasive species proved to be **pernicious** to the native ecosystem. |
| 8 | Deleterious | Causing harm or damage. | 1. Smoking has many **deleterious** effects on the respiratory system. <br> 2. The drought had a **deleterious** impact on the region's agricultural output. <br> 3. They found that the chemical had a **deleterious** effect on water quality. |
| 9 | Fastidious | Very attentive to and concerned about accuracy and detail. | 1. Her **fastidious** attention to detail made her an excellent editor. <br> 2. He is **fastidious** about the cleanliness of his kitchen. <br> 3. The professor was known for his **fastidious** grading of research papers. |
| 10 | Vociferous | (Especially of a person or speech) vehement or clamorous. | 1. The crowd was **vociferous** in its demand for the manager's resignation. <br> 2. He was a **vociferous** critic of the new government policy. <br> 3. The debate was marked by **vociferous** arguments from both sides. |
| 11 | Assiduous | Showing great care and perseverance. | 1. The project required **assiduous** planning and execution. <br> 2. She was an **assiduous** student, often staying late in the library. <br> 3. His **assiduous** efforts to learn the language finally paid off. |
| 12 | Salient | Most noticeable or important. | 1. The **salient** feature of the new phone is its improved battery life. <br> 2. He summarized the **salient** points of the report for the executive team. <br> 3. Her bright red coat made her the most **salient** person in the photograph. |
| 13 | Capricious | Given to sudden and unaccountable changes of mood or behavior. | 1. The weather in the mountains is notoriously **capricious**. <br> 2. Her **capricious** spending habits often led to financial trouble. <br> 3. The director's decisions were often seen as **capricious** and unpredictable. |
| 14 | Esoteric | Intended for or likely to be understood by only a small number of people with a specialized knowledge or interest. | 1. The lecture was full of **esoteric** references that only experts could grasp. <br> 2. He had an **esoteric** collection of ancient maps. <br> 3. Quantum physics remains an **esoteric** subject for most laypeople. |
| 15 | Gregarious | (Of a person) fond of company; sociable. | 1. As a naturally **gregarious** person, she quickly made friends in her new city. <br> 2. Lions are not **gregarious** animals, preferring to hunt alone. <br> 3. His **gregarious** nature made him the perfect host for the party. |
| 16 | Innocuous | Not harmful or offensive. | 1. The comment seemed **innocuous** at the time, but it later caused a major misunderstanding. <br> 2. He suffered from an **innocuous** rash that cleared up within a day. <br> 3. The spy used an **innocuous** cover story as a traveling salesman. |
| 17 | Mitigate | Make less severe, serious, or painful. | 1. Emergency funds were released to **mitigate** the effects of the flood. <br> 2. They installed soundproofing to **mitigate** the noise from the nearby highway. <br> 3. The company took steps to **mitigate** the risk of a data breach. |
| 18 | Ostracize | Exclude (someone) from a society or group. | 1. The community decided to **ostracize** him after his betrayal. <br> 2. She felt **ostracized** by her colleagues after she reported the misconduct. <br> 3. In some cultures, speaking out against the elders can lead to being **ostracized**. |
| 19 | Profligate | Recklessly extravagant or wasteful in the use of resources. | 1. The company was criticized for its **profligate** spending on unnecessary luxuries. <br> 2. He was a **profligate** gambler who quickly lost his entire inheritance. <br> 3. The government's **profligate** use of public funds sparked a national protest. |
| 20 | Recalcitrant | Having an obstinately uncooperative attitude toward authority or discipline. | 1. The **recalcitrant** student refused to follow the teacher's instructions. <br> 2. The donkey was **recalcitrant** and would not move despite the driver's urging. <br> 3. Managing the **recalcitrant** factions within the party proved to be a difficult task. |
