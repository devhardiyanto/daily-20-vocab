# Day 74: Daily English Vocabulary

| Word | Part of Speech | Definition | Example 1 | Example 2 | Example 3 |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Officious** | Adjective | Assertive of authority in an annoyingly domineering way, especially with regard to petty or trivial matters. | The **officious** security guard insisted on checking everyone's bags twice. | He resented the **officious** interference of his neighbors in his personal life. | Her **officious** manner made it difficult for her colleagues to work with her. |
| **Ominous** | Adjective | Giving the impression that something bad or unpleasant is going to happen; threatening; inauspicious. | The dark, heavy clouds were an **ominous** sign of the approaching storm. | There was an **ominous** silence in the room after he made the announcement. | The doctor's grave expression felt **ominous** to the waiting family. |
| **Omnipotent** | Adjective | (of a deity) having unlimited power; able to do anything. | In many religions, God is described as an **omnipotent** and all-knowing being. | The dictator behaved as if he were **omnipotent**, ignoring the laws of the land. | No leader is truly **omnipotent**, as they all rely on the support of others. |
| **Omniscient** | Adjective | Knowing everything. | The novel's **omniscient** narrator provides insight into the thoughts of every character. | It is impossible for any human to be truly **omniscient** about world events. | She felt as if her mother was **omniscient**, always knowing when she was lying. |
| **Onerous** | Adjective | (of a task, duty, or responsibility) involving an amount of effort and difficulty that is oppressively burdensome. | The new regulations imposed **onerous** reporting requirements on small businesses. | Cleaning up after the festival proved to be an **onerous** task for the volunteers. | He found the **onerous** terms of the loan difficult to fulfill. |
| **Opaque** | Adjective | Not able to be seen through; not transparent. | The windows were covered with an **opaque** film to ensure privacy. | The technical manual was written in such **opaque** language that no one could understand it. | His reasons for leaving the company remained **opaque** to his coworkers. |
| **Opprobrium** | Noun | Harsh criticism or censure. | The politician faced public **opprobrium** after the scandal was revealed. | The company's unethical practices brought widespread **opprobrium** upon its board. | He resigned to avoid the **opprobrium** that would follow a public trial. |
| **Opulence** | Noun | Great wealth or luxuriousness. | The palace was a stunning display of **opulence** and architectural grandeur. | He was overwhelmed by the **opulence** of the five-star hotel suite. | Despite her **opulence**, she remained humble and dedicated to charity. |
| **Oracular** | Adjective | Relating to an oracle; hard to interpret; enigmatic. | The hermit's **oracular** warnings left the villagers feeling confused and uneasy. | His **oracular** style of speaking made it difficult to discern his true intentions. | The ancient inscriptions were written in an **oracular** and cryptic script. |
| **Orthodox** | Adjective | (of a person or their views, especially religious or political ones, or other beliefs or practices) conforming to what is generally or traditionally accepted as right or true; established and conventional. | He holds very **orthodox** views on education and traditional teaching methods. | The doctor suggested a more **orthodox** treatment before trying experimental medicine. | She grew up in an **orthodox** household where religious traditions were strictly followed. |
| **Oscillate** | Verb | Move or swing back and forth at a regular speed. | The pendulum began to **oscillate** slowly after being pushed. | His mood tends to **oscillate** between extreme optimism and deep despair. | The fan was set to **oscillate**, cooling the entire room evenly. |
| **Ostensible** | Adjective | Stated or appearing to be true, but not necessarily so. | Their **ostensible** reason for visiting was to see the garden, but they really wanted to talk business. | The **ostensible** goal of the project was to improve efficiency, but it mostly cut costs. | Despite his **ostensible** confidence, he was actually quite nervous. |
| **Ostentatious** | Adjective | Characterized by vulgar or pretentious display; designed to impress or attract notice. | He built an **ostentatious** mansion to show off his newfound wealth. | Her **ostentatious** jewelry seemed out of place at the casual dinner party. | The wedding was an **ostentatious** affair with hundreds of guests and lavish decorations. |
| **Oust** | Verb | Drive out or expel (someone) from a position or place. | The board of directors voted to **oust** the CEO after the poor quarterly results. | The rebels managed to **oust** the dictator from power after months of fighting. | He was **ousted** from the club for violating the code of conduct. |
| **Pacify** | Verb | Quell the anger, agitation, or excitement of. | The government tried to **pacify** the protesters by promising new reforms. | He spoke in a soft voice to **pacify** the crying child. | The negotiator's job was to **pacify** both sides and reach a peaceful agreement. |
| **Paean** | Noun | A song of praise or triumph. | The book is a moving **paean** to the beauty of the natural world. | The choir performed a joyful **paean** during the victory celebration. | His speech was a heartfelt **paean** to his mentors and teachers. |
| **Palatial** | Adjective | Resembling a palace in being spacious and splendid. | They stayed in a **palatial** villa overlooking the Mediterranean Sea. | The company's new headquarters is a **palatial** building with state-of-the-art facilities. | After living in a small apartment, the new house felt absolutely **palatial**. |
| **Palliate** | Verb | Make (a disease or its symptoms) less severe or unpleasant without removing the cause. | The medicine was prescribed to **palliate** the pain, not to cure the underlying infection. | The government's measures were intended to **palliate** the effects of the economic crisis. | He tried to **palliate** his guilt by making a large donation to charity. |
| **Palpable** | Adjective | (of a feeling or atmosphere) so intense as to seem almost tangible. | The tension in the courtroom was **palpable** as the verdict was being read. | There was a **palpable** sense of relief when the missing child was found safe. | The excitement among the crowd was **palpable** before the concert began. |
| **Panacea** | Noun | A solution or remedy for all difficulties or diseases. | Technology is often seen as a **panacea** for all of society's problems. | There is no single **panacea** for the complex issue of global poverty. | The new law was promoted as a **panacea** for the city's rising crime rate. |
