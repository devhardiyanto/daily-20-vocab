# Day 46 Daily Vocabulary

| Word | Part of Speech | Definition | Example 1 | Example 2 | Example 3 |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Abnegation** | Noun | The act of renouncing or rejecting something. | Her **abnegation** of worldly pleasures led her to a life of meditation. | The king's **abnegation** of the throne shocked the entire nation. | It requires great **abnegation** to put others' needs before your own. |
| **Beguile** | Verb | To charm or enchant someone, sometimes in a deceptive way. | He used his wit and charm to **beguile** the audience. | The salesman tried to **beguile** the customer into buying the expensive car. | She was **beguiled** by the beauty of the ancient city. |
| **Capricious** | Adjective | Given to sudden and unaccountable changes of mood or behavior. | The **capricious** weather made it difficult to plan the outdoor event. | He is known for his **capricious** nature, often changing his mind at the last minute. | The stock market can be **capricious**, with prices fluctuating wildly. |
| **Diatribe** | Noun | A forceful and bitter verbal attack against someone or something. | The politician launched a long **diatribe** against his opponent's policies. | Her article was a scathing **diatribe** against the fashion industry. | He had to listen to his father's **diatribe** about his poor grades. |
| **Ebullient** | Adjective | Cheerful and full of energy. | The **ebullient** children were excited about the upcoming field trip. | She was in an **ebullient** mood after receiving the good news. | His **ebullient** personality made him popular among his colleagues. |
| **Foment** | Verb | To instigate or stir up (an undesirable or violent sentiment or course of action). | They were accused of trying to **foment** a rebellion against the government. | The comments were intended to **foment** discord within the team. | He was arrested for attempting to **foment** unrest in the city. |
| **Gourmand** | Noun | A person who enjoys eating and often eats too much. | As a true **gourmand**, he traveled the world in search of the best cuisine. | The buffet was a paradise for any **gourmand**. | He is a bit of a **gourmand**, always looking for the next great meal. |
| **Hapless** | Adjective | (Especially of a person) unfortunate. | The **hapless** travelers were stranded at the airport for two days. | He felt like a **hapless** victim of circumstances beyond his control. | The **hapless** team lost every game of the season. |
| **Inchoate** | Adjective | Just begun and so not fully formed or developed; rudimentary. | Her ideas for the new project are still **inchoate** and need more work. | The organization was in an **inchoate** state, with no clear structure. | He had an **inchoate** feeling that something was wrong, but couldn't say what. |
| **Juxtapose** | Verb | To place or deal with close together for contrasting effect. | The artist **juxtaposed** bright colors with dark shadows to create a dramatic effect. | The documentary **juxtaposes** the lives of the rich and the poor. | It is interesting to **juxtapose** the two different styles of architecture. |
| **Knell** | Noun | The sound of a bell, especially when rung solemnly for a death or funeral. | The tolling of the bell was a **knell** for the passing of the old era. | The news of the factory closing was a death **knell** for the small town. | We heard the distant **knell** of the church bell. |
| **Languid** | Adjective | (Of a person, manner, or gesture) displaying or having a disinclination for physical exertion or effort; slow and relaxed. | They spent a **languid** afternoon lounging by the pool. | She made a **languid** gesture with her hand, indicating she was tired. | The hot weather made everyone feel **languid** and unmotivated. |
| **Maverick** | Noun | An unorthodox or independent-minded person. | He is a **maverick** in the world of finance, always taking risks. | The director is known as a **maverick** who refuses to follow Hollywood conventions. | She has always been a **maverick**, never following the crowd. |
| **Nefarious** | Adjective | (Typically of an action or activity) wicked or criminal. | The hackers were involved in a **nefarious** scheme to steal personal data. | He was known for his **nefarious** activities in the underworld. | The movie's villain had a **nefarious** plan to take over the world. |
| **Obdurate** | Adjective | Stubbornly refusing to change one's opinion or course of action. | Despite the evidence, he remained **obdurate** in his belief. | The negotiator found the other side to be **obdurate** and unwilling to compromise. | She was **obdurate** in her refusal to apologize. |
| **Palliate** | Verb | To make (a disease or its symptoms) less severe or unpleasant without removing the cause. | The medicine was used to **palliate** the pain, but it didn't cure the illness. | They tried to **palliate** the effects of the economic crisis with new subsidies. | His words were intended to **palliate** her fears, but they didn't help much. |
| **Quiescent** | Adjective | In a state or period of inactivity or dormancy. | The volcano has been **quiescent** for hundreds of years. | The disease can remain **quiescent** in the body for a long time. | During the winter, the garden is **quiescent**, waiting for spring. |
| **Redoubtable** | Adjective | (Of a person) formidable, especially as an opponent. | He is a **redoubtable** opponent on the tennis court. | The company faces a **redoubtable** competitor in the new market. | She is a **redoubtable** leader who is respected by everyone. |
| **Salient** | Adjective | Most noticeable or important. | The **salient** points of the report were summarized in the introduction. | He pointed out the **salient** features of the new product. | The most **salient** fact is that we are running out of time. |
| **Tantamount** | Adjective | Equivalent in seriousness to; virtually the same as. | His refusal to answer was **tantamount** to an admission of guilt. | The new law is **tantamount** to a tax increase for most people. | For many, losing their job is **tantamount** to losing their identity. |
