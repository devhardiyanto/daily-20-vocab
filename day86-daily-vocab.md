# Daily Vocabulary - Day 86

| Word | Definition | Example 1 | Example 2 | Example 3 |
| :----------- | :------------------------------------------- | :--------------------------------------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Ephemeral** | Lasting for a very short time. | The beauty of the cherry blossoms is **ephemeral**. | Fashion trends are often **ephemeral**. | His fame was **ephemeral**, quickly fading away. |
| **Equanimity** | Calmness and composure, especially in a difficult situation. | She faced the crisis with remarkable **equanimity**. | Maintaining **equanimity** under pressure is a valuable skill. | He accepted the news with **equanimity**. |
| **Eschew** | Deliberately avoid using; abstain from. | He chose to **eschew** processed foods for a healthier diet. | They were advised to **eschew** violence. | Many religions **eschew** certain types of food. |
| **Euphemism** | A mild or indirect word or expression substituted for one considered to be too harsh or blunt when referring to something unpleasant or embarrassing. | "Passed away" is a **euphemism** for "died." | Using a **euphemism** can soften the impact of bad news. | The report used several **euphemisms** to describe the company's financial difficulties. |
| **Exacerbate** | To make (a problem, bad situation, or negative feeling) worse. | The new policy will only **exacerbate** the existing social inequalities. | Lack of sleep can **exacerbate** stress. | His comments **exacerbated** the tension in the room. |
| **Exigent** | Pressing; demanding. | The **exigent** circumstances required immediate action. | She responded to the **exigent** demands of her job. | The project had **exigent** deadlines. |
| **Fastidious** | Very attentive to and concerned about accuracy and detail. | He was **fastidious** about his appearance. | The chef was **fastidious** in his preparation of the meal. | Her **fastidious** nature made her an excellent proofreader. |
| **Fervent** | Having or displaying a passionate intensity. | He was a **fervent** supporter of human rights. | She delivered a **fervent** speech. | The crowd gave a **fervent** applause. |
| **Fickle** | Changing frequently, especially as regards one's loyalties, interests, or affection. | The weather in spring can be very **fickle**. | Public opinion is notoriously **fickle**. | He complained about his girlfriend's **fickle** nature. |
| **Garrulous** | Excessively talkative, especially on trivial matters. | The **garrulous** old man told stories for hours. | She found his **garrulous** nature endearing. | The **garrulous** host kept the conversation flowing. |
| **Gregarious** | (Of a person) fond of company; sociable. | He was a **gregarious** and outgoing individual. | **Gregarious** animals tend to live in herds. | Her **gregarious** personality made her popular. |
| **Hapless** | (Especially of a person) unfortunate. | The **hapless** victim of the scam lost all his savings. | The **hapless** traveler missed his flight. | He was a **hapless** fellow, always getting into trouble. |
| **Harangue** | A lengthy and aggressive speech. | The politician delivered a fiery **harangue** against his opponents. | She had to endure a long **harangue** from her boss. | He launched into a **harangue** about the state of the economy. |
| **Ignominious** | Deserving or causing public disgrace or shame. | The team suffered an **ignominious** defeat. | He made an **ignominious** exit from the stage. | The scandal led to his **ignominious** downfall. |
| **Impecunious** | Having little or no money; penniless. | The **impecunious** artist struggled to make ends meet. | He came from an **impecunious** background. | The charity helps **impecunious** families. |
| **Imperious** | Assuming power or authority without justification; arrogant and domineering. | The **imperious** manager barked orders at his staff. | Her **imperious** tone offended many. | He had an **imperious** manner. |
| **Impetuous** | Acting or done quickly and without thought or care. | His **impetuous** decision led to unforeseen consequences. | She regretted her **impetuous** remarks. | The young man was known for his **impetuous** behavior. |
| **Impugn** | To dispute the truth, validity, or honesty of (a statement or motive); call into question. | His rivals tried to **impugn** his character. | She refused to **impugn** his motives. | The lawyer attempted to **impugn** the witness's testimony. |
| **Inchoate** | Just begun and so not fully formed or developed; rudimentary. | The project was still in an **inchoate** stage. | He had only an **inchoate** idea of what he wanted to do. | The **inchoate** thoughts slowly formed into a plan. |
| **Incongruous** | Not in harmony or keeping with the surroundings or other aspects of something. | The modern sculpture looked **incongruous** in the ancient temple. | His casual attire was **incongruous** with the formal event. | There was an **incongruous** mix of old and new furniture. |
