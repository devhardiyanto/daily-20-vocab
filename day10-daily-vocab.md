# Day 10: Daily Vocabulary

A curated list of 20 English vocabulary words to expand your lexicon.

| Word | Part of Speech | Definition | Contextual Examples |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Ephemeral** | Adjective | Lasting for a very short time. | 1. The beauty of the cherry blossoms is **ephemeral**, lasting only a few weeks. <br> 2. Fashion trends are often **ephemeral**, quickly replaced by new styles. <br> 3. He captured the **ephemeral** moment of the shooting star with his camera. |
| **Ubiquitous** | Adjective | Present, appearing, or found everywhere. | 1. In the modern world, smartphones have become truly **ubiquitous**. <br> 2. The company's logo is **ubiquitous**; you see it on every street corner. <br> 3. The scent of pine was **ubiquitous** throughout the forest. |
| **Mellifluous** | Adjective | (Of a voice or words) sweet or musical; pleasant to hear. | 1. The singer's **mellifluous** voice soothed the audience. <br> 2. He delivered the poem in a deep, **mellifluous** tone. <br> 3. The stream made a **mellifluous** sound as it flowed over the rocks. |
| **Pernicious** | Adjective | Having a harmful effect, especially in a gradual or subtle way. | 1. The **pernicious** effects of social media addiction are becoming clear. <br> 2. She warned her friend about the **pernicious** influence of the cult leader. <br> 3. The drought had a **pernicious** impact on the region's agriculture. |
| **Quixotic** | Adjective | Exceedingly idealistic; unrealistic and impractical. | 1. His **quixotic** quest to save the failing business single-handedly was admirable but doomed. <br> 2. The young inventor pursued a **quixotic** dream of building a perpetual motion machine. <br> 3. They laughed at his **quixotic** plans for a completely self-sufficient community. |
| **Surreptitious** | Adjective | Kept secret, especially because it would not be approved of. | 1. They held a **surreptitious** meeting in the dead of night. <br> 2. He cast a **surreptitious** glance at his watch during the boring lecture. <br> 3. The spy made a **surreptitious** exchange of documents in the crowded market. |
| **Tacit** | Adjective | Understood or implied without being stated. | 1. Their agreement to share the workload was **tacit**, never explicitly discussed. <br> 2. By remaining silent, she gave her **tacit** approval to the plan. <br> 3. There was a **tacit** understanding between the two rivals that they would not interfere with each other's territory. |
| **Wistful** | Adjective | Having or showing a feeling of vague or regretful longing. | 1. She gave a **wistful** sigh as she looked at the old photographs. <br> 2. The song evoked a **wistful** nostalgia for his childhood home. <br> 3. He had a **wistful** look in his eyes, dreaming of a life he never lived. |
| **Zealous** | Adjective | Having or showing great energy or enthusiasm in pursuit of a cause or objective. | 1. The new volunteer was **zealous** in his efforts to clean up the park. <br> 2. She is a **zealous** advocate for animal rights. <br> 3. His **zealous** devotion to the project impressed the entire team. |
| **Capricious** | Adjective | Given to sudden and unaccountable changes of mood or behavior. | 1. The weather in the mountains is notoriously **capricious**. <br> 2. Her **capricious** spending habits often led to financial trouble. <br> 3. A **capricious** decision by the CEO led to a sudden change in company policy. |
| **Dichotomy** | Noun | A division or contrast between two things that are or are represented as being opposed or entirely different. | 1. The film explores the **dichotomy** between good and evil. <br> 2. There is a clear **dichotomy** between the rich and the poor in the city. <br> 3. The philosophical debate centered on the **dichotomy** of mind and body. |
| **Egregious** | Adjective | Outstandingly bad; shocking. | 1. The judge called the defendant's actions an **egregious** abuse of power. <br> 2. Making such an **egregious** error in the final report cost him his job. <br> 3. The company was fined for its **egregious** disregard for environmental regulations. |
| **Fastidious** | Adjective | Very attentive to and concerned about accuracy and detail. | 1. He is **fastidious** about his appearance, always perfectly dressed. <br> 2. The scientist was **fastidious** in recording every detail of the experiment. <br> 3. Her **fastidious** nature made her an excellent proofreader. |
| **Gregarious** | Adjective | (Of a person) fond of company; sociable. | 1. As a naturally **gregarious** person, she quickly made friends in the new city. <br> 2. Lions are not **gregarious** animals, preferring to hunt alone. <br> 3. The party was filled with **gregarious** chatter and laughter. |
| **Hegemony** | Noun | Leadership or dominance, especially by one state or social group over others. | 1. The small nation struggled to resist the cultural **hegemony** of its powerful neighbor. <br> 2. The company achieved market **hegemony** after acquiring its main competitor. <br> 3. Critics argue that the media maintains a political **hegemony** over public opinion. |
| **Incongruous** | Adjective | Not in harmony or keeping with the surroundings or other aspects of something. | 1. The modern sculpture looked **incongruous** in the ancient temple courtyard. <br> 2. His bright red tie was **incongruous** with his somber black suit. <br> 3. The sound of a siren was **incongruous** with the peaceful rural setting. |
| **Languid** | Adjective | (Of a person, manner, or gesture) having a disinclination for physical exertion or effort; slow and relaxed. | 1. A **languid** afternoon was spent reading a book by the pool. <br> 2. She moved with a **languid** grace that suggested a lack of urgency. <br> 3. The heat made everyone feel **languid** and sleepy. |
| **Misanthrope** | Noun | A person who dislikes humankind and avoids human society. | 1. After years of betrayal, the old man became a bitter **misanthrope**. <br> 2. The main character in the novel is a wealthy but lonely **misanthrope**. <br> 3. He was not a true **misanthrope**, just very shy and reserved. |
| **Obfuscate** | Verb | To make obscure, unclear, or unintelligible. | 1. The politician tried to **obfuscate** the issue with vague language. <br> 2. His excessive use of jargon only served to **obfuscate** the simple concept. <br> 3. The company's complex financial reports were designed to **obfuscate** their true debt. |
| **Pithy** | Adjective | (Of language or style) concise and forcefully expressive. | 1. The speaker concluded with a **pithy** summary of the main points. <br> 2. She is known for her **pithy** remarks and sharp wit. <br> 3. The editorial offered a **pithy** critique of the government's new policy. |
