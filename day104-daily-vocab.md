# Day 104: Daily Vocabulary

| Word | Definition | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Ephemeral** | Lasting for a very short time | 1. The beauty of a sunset is often ephemeral. 2. Fashion trends are by nature ephemeral. 3. His fame was ephemeral, quickly fading after a few years. |
| **Ubiquitous** | Present, appearing, or found everywhere | 1. Smartphones have become ubiquitous in modern society. 2. The ubiquitous presence of surveillance cameras is a concern for privacy advocates. 3. Coffee shops are ubiquitous in this city. |
| **Cacophony** | A harsh, discordant mixture of sounds | 1. The city street was a cacophony of car horns and shouting. 2. The orchestra produced a cacophony of noise during their warm-up. 3. I woke up to a cacophony of birdsong outside my window. |
| **Mellifluous** | (Of a voice or words) sweet or musical; pleasant to hear | 1. Her mellifluous voice captivated the audience. 2. The speaker delivered his address in a mellifluous tone. 3. The mellifluous sound of the harp filled the concert hall. |
| **Serendipity** | The occurrence and development of events by chance in a happy or beneficial way | 1. Finding that old photograph was a moment of pure serendipity. 2. Serendipity played a role in their meeting at the airport. 3. The discovery of penicillin was a classic example of scientific serendipity. |
| **Luminous** | Emitting or reflecting light; shining | 1. The moon was a luminous orb in the night sky. 2. Her eyes were luminous with excitement. 3. The artist used luminous colors to make the painting glow. |
| **Benevolent** | Well meaning and kindly | 1. The benevolent king was loved by his people. 2. She has a benevolent smile that always puts people at ease. 3. A benevolent donor contributed a large sum to the charity. |
| **Malevolent** | Having or showing a wish to do evil to others | 1. The villain in the story had a malevolent grin. 2. He cast a malevolent glance at his rival. 3. The malevolent spirit haunted the old house. |
| **Equanimity** | Calmness and composure, especially in a difficult situation | 1. She faced the crisis with remarkable equanimity. 2. Maintaining equanimity during a stressful exam is crucial. 3. The doctor handled the emergency with professional equanimity. |
| **Pernicious** | Having a harmful effect, especially in a gradual or subtle way | 1. The pernicious effects of social media on mental health are well-documented. 2. He warned against the pernicious influence of propaganda. 3. The pernicious weed quickly took over the garden. |
| **Gregarious** | (Of a person) fond of company; sociable | 1. She is a gregarious person who loves to host parties. 2. Gregarious animals tend to live in herds or flocks. 3. His gregarious nature made him popular among his colleagues. |
| **Voracious** | Wanting or devouring great quantities of food; having a very eager approach to an activity | 1. He has a voracious appetite for knowledge. 2. The voracious reader finished the entire series in a week. 3. The young athlete had a voracious hunger for success. |
| **Insidious** | Proceeding in a gradual, subtle way, but with harmful effects | 1. The insidious disease spread silently through the population. 2. He was unaware of the insidious plot against him. 3. The insidious propaganda slowly changed public opinion. |
| **Resplendent** | Shining brilliantly; very attractive and impressive through being richly colorful or sumptuous | 1. The queen wore a resplendent gown adorned with jewels. 2. The garden was resplendent with colorful flowers. 3. The bride looked resplendent in her wedding dress. |
| **Capricious** | Given to sudden and unaccountable changes of mood or behavior | 1. The weather in spring can be very capricious. 2. Her capricious decisions often led to unpredictable outcomes. 3. He was known for his capricious temper. |
| **Ostentatious** | Characterized by vulgar or pretentious display; designed to impress or attract notice | 1. The millionaire lived in an ostentatious mansion. 2. She wore an ostentatious diamond necklace to the party. 3. His ostentatious display of wealth was off-putting. |
| **Salient** | Most noticeable or important | 1. The salient feature of the new phone is its improved camera. 2. He highlighted the salient points of the report. 3. One of the salient differences between the two cultures is their approach to time. |
| **Ebullient** | Cheerful and full of energy | 1. Her ebullient personality made her a joy to be around. 2. The team was ebullient after their victory. 3. He delivered an ebullient speech that energized the crowd. |
| **Paucity** | The presence of something only in small or insufficient quantities or amounts; scarcity | 1. There was a paucity of evidence to support his claim. 2. The report highlighted the paucity of resources in rural areas. 3. A paucity of rain led to a severe drought. |
| **Sanctimonious** | Making a show of being morally superior to other people | 1. His sanctimonious attitude annoyed everyone in the room. 2. She gave a sanctimonious lecture on the importance of honesty. 3. The politician's sanctimonious speech rang hollow to many voters. |
