# Daily Vocabulary - Day 07

## 20 English Vocabulary Words

| Word | Definition | Examples |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Ephemeral** | Lasting for a very short time. | 1. The beauty of the sunset was **ephemeral**, fading within minutes. <br> 2. Fashion trends are often **ephemeral**, quickly replaced by new styles. <br> 3. He realized the joy of his victory was **ephemeral** when he faced the next challenge. |
| **Ubiquitous** | Present, appearing, or found everywhere. | 1. In the modern world, smartphones have become **ubiquitous**. <br> 2. The company's logo is **ubiquitous** on every street corner. <br> 3. The scent of pine was **ubiquitous** throughout the forest. |
| **Mellifluous** | (Of a voice or words) sweet or musical; pleasant to hear. | 1. The singer's **mellifluous** voice captivated the audience. <br> 2. He delivered the speech in a **mellifluous** tone that was easy to listen to. <br> 3. The stream's **mellifluous** gurgle was the only sound in the quiet valley. |
| **Cacophony** | A harsh, discordant mixture of sounds. | 1. The city street was a **cacophony** of car horns and shouting vendors. <br> 2. The orchestra's warm-up was a brief **cacophony** before the conductor took the stage. <br> 3. The meeting descended into a **cacophony** of angry voices. |
| **Pernicious** | Having a harmful effect, especially in a gradual or subtle way. | 1. The **pernicious** effects of social media on mental health are becoming clear. <br> 2. He spread **pernicious** rumors that damaged her reputation. <br> 3. The drought had a **pernicious** influence on the region's crops. |
| **Alacrity** | Brisk and cheerful readiness. | 1. She accepted the new project with **alacrity**. <br> 2. The waiter served the meal with impressive **alacrity**. <br> 3. Despite the early hour, the team started the work with great **alacrity**. |
| **Surreptitious** | Kept secret, especially because it would not be approved of. | 1. They held a **surreptitious** meeting in the dead of night. <br> 2. He cast a **surreptitious** glance at his watch during the long lecture. <br> 3. The spy made a **surreptitious** exchange of documents in the crowded market. |
| **Gregarious** | (Of a person) fond of company; sociable. | 1. As a naturally **gregarious** person, she made friends easily. <br> 2. Lions are not solitary animals; they are **gregarious** and live in prides. <br> 3. His **gregarious** nature made him the perfect host for the party. |
| **Fastidious** | Very attentive to and concerned about accuracy and detail. | 1. He is **fastidious** about his appearance, always perfectly dressed. <br> 2. The editor was known for her **fastidious** attention to grammar. <br> 3. Only a **fastidious** researcher would notice such a small discrepancy in the data. |
| **Capricious** | Given to sudden and unaccountable changes of mood or behavior. | 1. The weather in the mountains is notoriously **capricious**. <br> 2. Her **capricious** spending habits often left her short of money. <br> 3. The director's **capricious** decisions confused the entire cast and crew. |
| **Equivocate** | Use ambiguous language so as to conceal the truth or avoid committing oneself. | 1. When questioned about the budget, the politician began to **equivocate**. <br> 2. Stop **equivocating** and give me a straight answer. <br> 3. He tried to **equivocate** his way out of the difficult situation. |
| **Ineffable** | Too great or extreme to be expressed or described in words. | 1. The beauty of the aurora borealis was **ineffable**. <br> 2. She felt an **ineffable** sense of peace after the meditation. <br> 3. The artist tried to capture the **ineffable** quality of light in the painting. |
| **Sanctimonious** | Making a show of being morally superior to other people. | 1. His **sanctimonious** pronouncements on charity rang hollow. <br> 2. She disliked his **sanctimonious** attitude toward her lifestyle choices. <br> 3. The preacher's **sanctimonious** tone alienated many members of the congregation. |
| **Recalcitrant** | Having an obstinately uncooperative attitude toward authority or discipline. | 1. The **recalcitrant** student refused to follow the teacher's instructions. <br> 2. The government faced a **recalcitrant** opposition to the new law. <br> 3. Despite the training, the puppy remained **recalcitrant** and chewed the furniture. |
| **Paucity** | The presence of something only in small or insufficient quantities or amounts; scarcity. | 1. There was a **paucity** of evidence to support the claim. <br> 2. The team suffered from a **paucity** of experienced players. <br> 3. A **paucity** of rain led to a severe drought in the region. |
| **Superfluous** | Unnecessary, especially through being more than enough. | 1. The extra paragraph in the report was **superfluous** and was removed. <br> 2. He made a **superfluous** gesture of apology, as no one was offended. <br> 3. After the main course, the dessert seemed entirely **superfluous**. |
| **Ameliorate** | Make (something bad or unsatisfactory) better. | 1. Efforts were made to **ameliorate** the living conditions of the refugees. <br> 2. Medicine can **ameliorate** the symptoms of the disease, but not cure it. <br> 3. The new policy is designed to **ameliorate** the problem of student debt. |
| **Juxtaposition** | The fact of two things being seen or placed close together with contrasting effect. | 1. The artist used the **juxtaposition** of light and shadow to create a dramatic effect. <br> 2. The novel's strength lies in the **juxtaposition** of the city's wealth and poverty. <br> 3. The museum displayed the ancient artifact in **juxtaposition** with a modern sculpture. |
| **Obfuscate** | Render obscure, unclear, or unintelligible. | 1. The lawyer tried to **obfuscate** the facts with confusing jargon. <br> 2. His tendency to **obfuscate** made it difficult to understand his true intentions. <br> 3. The complex code was designed to **obfuscate** its purpose from casual inspection. |
| **Vociferous** | (Especially of a person or speech) vehement or clamorous. | 1. The protestors were **vociferous** in their demands for change. <br> 2. He made a **vociferous** denial of the accusations against him. <br> 3. The crowd's **vociferous** cheering filled the stadium after the winning goal. |
