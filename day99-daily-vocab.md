# Day 99 Daily Vocabulary

| Word | Definition | Example Sentences |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Aberration** | a state or condition markedly different from the norm | 1. The single red leaf on the green plant was an aberration. 2. His angry outburst was an aberration from his normally calm demeanor. 3. The team's loss was an aberration, as they had been undefeated all season. |
| **Abhor** | feel hatred or disgust toward | 1. I abhor all forms of violence. 2. She abhors the taste of cilantro. 3. He abhors the way his boss treats his employees. |
| **Acquiesce** | agree or express agreement | 1. She will acquiesce to the new rules, but she doesn't like them. 2. He finally acquiesced to his wife's request for a new car. 3. The committee will acquiesce to the demands of the protesters. |
| **Alacrity** | liveliness and eagerness | 1. She accepted the invitation with alacrity. 2. He approached the task with alacrity and finished it in record time. 3. The children ran to the playground with alacrity. |
| **Amiable** | diffusing warmth and friendliness | 1. He was an amiable man, always ready with a smile and a kind word. 2. She has an amiable personality that makes her popular with everyone. 3. They had an amiable conversation over dinner. |
| **Appease** | make peace with | 1. He tried to appease his angry wife with a bouquet of flowers. 2. The government tried to appease the protesters by promising to reform the law. 3. She appeased the crying child with a cookie. |
| **Arcane** | requiring secret or mysterious knowledge | 1. The arcane rules of the game were difficult to understand. 2. He was an expert in the arcane world of ancient manuscripts. 3. She was fascinated by the arcane symbols on the old map. |
| **Avarice** | extreme greed for material wealth | 1. His avarice led him to a life of crime. 2. The company's avarice was evident in its exploitation of its workers. 3. She was consumed by avarice and would do anything for money. |
| **Brazen** | unrestrained by convention or propriety | 1. He made a brazen attempt to cheat on the exam. 2. She had a brazen disregard for the rules. 3. The politician's brazen lies were shocking to the public. |
| **Brusque** | marked by rude or peremptory shortness | 1. His brusque manner made him unpopular with his colleagues. 2. She gave a brusque reply to his question. 3. The doctor's brusque bedside manner upset the patient. |
| **Cajole** | influence or urge by gentle urging, caressing, or flattering | 1. He cajoled his friend into lending him the money. 2. She cajoled her parents into letting her go to the party. 3. The salesman cajoled the customer into buying the expensive car. |
| **Callous** | emotionally hardened | 1. He had a callous disregard for the feelings of others. 2. Her callous remarks hurt her friend's feelings. 3. The dictator's callous treatment of his people was condemned by the world. |
| **Candor** | the quality of being honest and straightforward | 1. I appreciate your candor in this matter. 2. She spoke with candor about her struggles with addiction. 3. The politician's candor was a refreshing change from the usual political spin. |
| **Chide** | scold or reprimand severely or angrily | 1. The mother chided her child for misbehaving. 2. The teacher chided the student for not doing his homework. 3. He chided himself for making such a stupid mistake. |
| **Circumspect** | careful to consider all circumstances and possible consequences | 1. He was circumspect in his business dealings. 2. She was circumspect about making new friends. 3. The general was circumspect in his military strategy. |
| **Clandestine** | conducted with or marked by hidden aims or methods | 1. The clandestine meeting was held in a secret location. 2. The spies were involved in a clandestine operation. 3. She had a clandestine affair with her boss. |
| **Coerce** | to cause to do through pressure or necessity, by physical, moral or intellectual means | 1. He was coerced into signing the confession. 2. She was coerced into marrying a man she didn't love. 3. The government coerced the people into submission. |
| **Coherent** | marked by an orderly, logical, and aesthetically consistent relation of parts | 1. He gave a coherent explanation of the events. 2. She had a coherent plan for the future. 3. The essay was well-written and coherent. |
| **Complacency** | the feeling you have when you are satisfied with yourself | 1. His complacency led to his downfall. 2. The team's complacency was evident in their sloppy play. 3. She was warned against complacency in her studies. |
| **Confidant** | someone to whom private matters are confided | 1. She was his closest confidant. 2. He shared his deepest secrets with his confidant. 3. The therapist was a trusted confidant for her patients. |
