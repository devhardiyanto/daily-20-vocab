# Day 76: Daily English Vocabulary

| Word | Part of Speech | Definition | Example 1 | Example 2 | Example 3 |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Phlegmatic** | Adjective | (of a person) having an unemotional and stolidly calm disposition. | Even in the middle of the crisis, the captain remained **phlegmatic** and composed. | His **phlegmatic** response to the news surprised everyone who expected him to be angry. | She is known for her **phlegmatic** approach to high-pressure situations. |
| **Pious** | Adjective | Devoutly religious; or making a hypocritical display of virtue. | The family was known for their **pious** lifestyle and regular attendance at church. | His speech was filled with **pious** platitudes that failed to impress the audience. | She lived a quiet, **pious** life dedicated to helping the less fortunate. |
| **Pique** | Noun / Verb | A feeling of irritation or resentment; or to stimulate interest or curiosity. | He left the party in a fit of **pique** after being ignored by the host. | The mysterious headline was designed to **pique** the readers' curiosity. | She felt a sense of **pique** when her suggestions were dismissed without consideration. |
| **Placate** | Verb | Make (someone) less angry or hostile. | The manager tried to **placate** the angry customer by offering a full refund. | They attempted to **placate** the protesters with promises of reform. | A simple apology was enough to **placate** his ruffled feelings. |
| **Placid** | Adjective | (of a person or animal) not easily upset or excited; or (of a place) calm and peaceful. | The lake was **placid** and still in the early morning light. | Despite the chaos around him, he maintained a **placid** expression. | She has a **placid** nature that makes her a great mediator in conflicts. |
| **Platitude** | Noun | A remark or statement, especially one with a moral content, that has been used too often to be interesting or thoughtful. | The commencement speaker filled his address with uninspiring **platitudes**. | We need real solutions, not just empty **platitudes** from politicians. | His attempt at comfort consisted of nothing more than a tired **platitude**. |
| **Plethora** | Noun | A large or excessive amount of (something). | The report provided a **plethora** of data to support its conclusions. | There is a **plethora** of choices available in the modern digital market. | After the harvest, there was a **plethora** of fresh vegetables in the kitchen. |
| **Pliant** | Adjective | Pliable; easily bent; or easily influenced. | The **pliant** branches of the willow tree swayed gracefully in the breeze. | He was looking for a **pliant** assistant who would follow his orders without question. | The leather becomes more **pliant** and comfortable with regular use. |
| **Polemic** | Noun | A strong verbal or written attack on someone or something. | The author's latest book is a fierce **polemic** against modern consumerism. | His speech was more of a **polemic** than a balanced discussion of the issues. | She published a powerful **polemic** defending the rights of the marginalized. |
| **Pragmatic** | Adjective | Dealing with things sensibly and realistically in a way that is based on practical rather than theoretical considerations. | We need a **pragmatic** approach to solving the budget deficit. | She is a **pragmatic** leader who focuses on results rather than ideology. | Taking a **pragmatic** view of the situation helped them make a quick decision. |
| **Precarious** | Adjective | Not securely held or in position; dangerously likely to fall or collapse; or uncertain. | The ladder was in a **precarious** position against the uneven wall. | Many families are in a **precarious** financial situation due to the rising costs. | The peace agreement remains **precarious** and could fail at any moment. |
| **Precipitous** | Adjective | Dangerously high or steep; or (of a change to a worse condition) sudden and dramatic. | The hikers were careful as they navigated the **precipitous** mountain trails. | The company saw a **precipitous** decline in sales over the last quarter. | Her **precipitous** departure from the firm left everyone in shock. |
| **Precocious** | Adjective | (of a child) having developed certain abilities or proclivities at an earlier age than usual. | The **precocious** young pianist performed a complex concerto at age seven. | Her **precocious** interest in science led her to win the national fair. | He was a **precocious** child who started reading at the age of three. |
| **Predilection** | Noun | A preference or special liking for something; a bias in favor of something. | He has a **predilection** for spicy food and exotic flavors. | Her **predilection** for historical novels was evident from her large library. | The judge was accused of having a **predilection** toward the prosecution. |
| **Presumptuous** | Adjective | (of a person or their behavior) failing to observe the limits of what is permitted or appropriate. | It would be **presumptuous** of me to tell you how to do your job. | He made a **presumptuous** assumption that he was invited to the private meeting. | Her **presumptuous** attitude often rubbed her colleagues the wrong way. |
| **Prevaricate** | Verb | Speak or act in an evasive way. | When questioned about the missing funds, he began to **prevaricate**. | The witness was warned not to **prevaricate** and to tell the truth. | Instead of giving a direct answer, the politician chose to **prevaricate**. |
| **Pristine** | Adjective | In its original condition; unspoiled; or clean and fresh as if new. | The beach was **pristine**, with no signs of human interference. | He kept his vintage car in **pristine** condition for over thirty years. | The new snow gave the landscape a **pristine** and magical appearance. |
| **Probity** | Noun | The quality of having strong moral principles; honesty and decency. | The judge was respected for his lifelong **probity** and fairness. | Financial institutions must maintain the highest standards of **probity**. | Her **probity** was never in doubt, even during the most difficult times. |
| **Proclivity** | Noun | A tendency to choose or do something regularly; an inclination or predisposition toward a particular thing. | He has a **proclivity** for getting into trouble whenever he's bored. | Her **proclivity** for hard work earned her a promotion within a year. | Most people have a **proclivity** to avoid conflict whenever possible. |
| **Prodigal** | Adjective | Spending money or resources freely and recklessly; wastefully extravagant. | The **prodigal** heir blew through his entire inheritance in just two years. | We must be careful not to be **prodigal** with our natural resources. | Her **prodigal** lifestyle eventually led to significant financial difficulties. |
