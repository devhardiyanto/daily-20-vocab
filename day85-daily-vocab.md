# Daily Vocabulary - Day 85

| Word | Definition | Example 1 | Example 2 | Example 3 |
| :----------- | :------------------------------------------- | :--------------------------------------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Meticulous** | Showing great attention to detail; very careful and precise. | The artist was **meticulous** in her brushstrokes, ensuring every detail was perfect. | He prepared the report with **meticulous** accuracy. | Her **meticulous** planning ensured the event ran smoothly. |
| **Mitigate** | To make less severe, serious, or painful. | The government implemented policies to **mitigate** the effects of the economic downturn. | Painkillers can help to **mitigate** the discomfort. | They are trying to **mitigate** the risks involved. |
| **Mundane** | Lacking interest or excitement; dull. | He found his daily routine to be quite **mundane** and longed for adventure. | The job involved many **mundane** tasks. | She found beauty in the **mundane** aspects of life. |
| **Nefarious** | (Typically of an action or activity) wicked or criminal. | The supervillain concocted a **nefarious** plan to take over the world. | He was involved in some **nefarious** activities. | The **nefarious** plot was uncovered by the police. |
| **Obfuscate** | To make obscure, unclear, or unintelligible. | The politician tried to **obfuscate** the truth with confusing statements. | His explanations only served to **obfuscate** the issue further. | The legal jargon was designed to **obfuscate** the meaning. |
| **Ostracize** | To exclude (someone) from a society or group. | The community decided to **ostraci**ze him after his controversial remarks. | She felt **ostracized** by her former friends. | He was **ostracized** for his unpopular opinions. |
| **Paradigm** | A typical example or pattern of something; a model. | The discovery of quantum mechanics shifted the scientific **paradigm**. | This new technology represents a complete **paradigm** shift. | His work challenged the prevailing **paradigm** in education. |
| **Pernicious** | Having a harmful effect, especially in a gradual or subtle way. | The **pernicious** effects of social media on mental health are a growing concern. | The **pernicious** rumor spread quickly. | Smoking has a **pernicious** effect on health. |
| **Plausible** | (Of an argument or statement) seeming reasonable or probable. | Her explanation for being late was **plausible**, so her boss accepted it. | The story was **plausible** enough to be believed. | He offered a **plausible** excuse for his absence. |
| **Preclude** | To prevent from happening; make impossible. | The heavy rain will **preclude** us from having the picnic outdoors. | His injury will **preclude** him from playing in the match. | Lack of funds may **preclude** the project from going ahead. |
| **Proclivity** | A tendency to choose or do something regularly; an inclination or predisposition. | He had a natural **proclivity** for artistic pursuits. | Her **proclivity** for late-night snacks was well-known. | There is a **proclivity** among some people to ignore warnings. |
| **Quandary** | A state of perplexity or uncertainty over what to do in a difficult situation. | She found herself in a **quandary** about whether to accept the job offer or not. | The ethical **quandary** presented a difficult choice. | He was in a **quandary** about how to proceed. |
| **Rancor** | Bitterness or resentfulness, especially when long-standing. | The old rivals still harbored deep **rancor** towards each other. | There was no **rancor** in his voice, only sadness. | The debate was marked by intense **rancor**. |
| **Recalcitrant** | Having an obstinately uncooperative attitude toward authority or discipline. | The **recalcitrant** student refused to follow the teacher's instructions. | The **recalcitrant** donkey refused to move. | The **recalcitrant** employees ignored the new rules. |
| **Redundant** | Not or no longer needed or useful; superfluous. | The company laid off several employees whose roles had become **redundant**. | The extra safety features were **redundant**. | The phrase 
was **redundant** and could be removed. |
| **Repudiate** | To refuse to accept or be associated with. | He decided to **repudiate** the accusations made against him. | The government was quick to **repudiate** the claims of corruption. | She chose to **repudiate** her family's wealth and live a simple life. |
| **Resilience** | The capacity to recover quickly from difficulties; toughness. | Her **resilience** in the face of adversity was truly inspiring. | The community showed great **resilience** after the natural disaster. | The material has remarkable **resilience** to wear and tear. |
| **Sanctimonious** | Making a show of being morally superior to other people. | His **sanctimonious** pronouncements about morality often irritated his colleagues. | The preacher delivered a **sanctimonious** sermon. | She disliked his **sanctimonious** attitude. |
| **Scrutinize** | To examine or inspect closely and thoroughly. | The detective began to **scrutinize** the crime scene for clues. | The committee will **scrutinize** the budget proposal. | She **scrutinized** his face for any sign of deception. |
| **Superfluous** | Unnecessary, especially through being more than enough. | The extra details in the report were **superfluous** and only added to its length. | Removing **superfluous** information can improve clarity. | He felt his presence at the meeting was **superfluous**. |
| **Surreptitious** | Kept secret, especially because it would not be approved of. | He made a **surreptitious** attempt to peek at her notes during the exam. | They had a **surreptitious** meeting in the park. | She cast a **surreptitious** glance at her watch. |
| **Tacit** | Understood or implied without being stated. | Their agreement was **tacit**, with no words exchanged but a clear understanding. | He gave his **tacit** approval by nodding. | There was a **tacit** understanding between them. |
| **Ubiquitous** | Present, appearing, or found everywhere. | In today's digital age, smartphones have become **ubiquitous**. | The brand's logo is **ubiquitous** in popular culture. | Coffee shops are **ubiquitous** in this city. |
| **Vehement** | Showing strong feeling; forceful, passionate, or intense. | She expressed her **vehement** opposition to the new policy. | He delivered a **vehement** speech against injustice. | The crowd responded with **vehement** applause. |
| **Venerate** | To regard with great respect; revere. | Many cultures **venerate** their ancestors and elders. | The ancient text was **venerated** by scholars. | They **venerate** the founder of their religion. |
| **Zealous** | Having or showing great energy or enthusiasm in pursuit of a cause or objective. | The **zealous** activist dedicated his life to fighting for environmental protection. | She was a **zealous** supporter of the team. | His **zealous** efforts led to the project's success. |
