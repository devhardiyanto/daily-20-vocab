# Day 126 - Daily Vocabulary

| Word           | Part of Speech   | Definition                                                                                                                       | Example 1                                                          | Example 2                                                                             | Example 3                                                       |
|:---------------|:-----------------|:---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|:-------------------------------------------------------------------|:--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|:----------------------------------------------------------------|
| Alacrity       | Noun             | Brisk and cheerful readiness.                                                                                                    | She accepted the new project with alacrity.                        | The team responded to the challenge with remarkable alacrity.                         | His alacrity in learning new skills impressed his colleagues.   |
| Ameliorate     | Verb             | Make (something bad or unsatisfactory) better.                                                                                   | Steps were taken to ameliorate the living conditions of the poor.  | The new policies aim to ameliorate the economic situation.                            | He tried to ameliorate the pain with medication.                |
| Anathema       | Noun             | Something or someone that one vehemently dislikes.                                                                               | Racial prejudice was anathema to her.                              | The idea of censorship is anathema to a free press.                                   | For the devout, blasphemy is anathema.                          |
| Assiduous      | Adjective        | Showing great care and perseverance.                                                                                             | She was assiduous in her studies, earning top grades.              | The project required assiduous attention to detail.                                   | An assiduous gardener tends to his plants daily.                |
| Cajole         | Verb             | Persuade someone to do something by sustained coaxing or flattery.                                                               | He hoped to cajole her into selling the house.                     | The child tried to cajole his parents into buying him a toy.                          | She used her charm to cajole the information out of him.        |
| Circumlocution | Noun             | The use of many words where fewer would do, especially in a deliberate attempt to be vague or evasive.                           | His speech was full of circumlocutions, avoiding the main issue.   | Legal documents often contain circumlocutions that make them difficult to understand. | She was tired of his circumlocution and wanted a direct answer. |
| Convivial      | Adjective        | (Of an atmosphere or event) friendly, lively, and enjoyable.                                                                     | The convivial atmosphere of the party made everyone feel welcome.  | He was a convivial host, always making sure his guests had a good time.               | The pub had a convivial ambiance.                               |
| Demure         | Adjective        | (Of a woman or her behavior) reserved, modest, and shy.                                                                          | She was a demure young woman who rarely spoke out of turn.         | Her demure smile hid a mischievous personality.                                       | The demure dress was perfect for the formal occasion.           |
| Didactic       | Adjective        | Intended to teach, particularly in having moral instruction as an ulterior motive.                                               | The didactic novel aimed to teach children about honesty.          | His lectures were often didactic, focusing on moral principles.                       | The play was criticized for its overly didactic tone.           |
| Disparage      | Verb             | Regard or represent as being of little worth.                                                                                    | He never missed an opportunity to disparage his competitors.       | It's unwise to disparage someone's efforts, even if they fall short.                  | She felt disparaged by his dismissive comments.                 |
| Ebullient      | Adjective        | Cheerful and full of energy.                                                                                                     | Her ebullient personality made her popular with everyone.          | The ebullient crowd cheered for their team.                                           | He was in an ebullient mood after receiving the good news.      |
| Enervate       | Verb             | Cause (someone) to feel drained of energy or vitality; weaken.                                                                   | The hot weather enervated them.                                    | A long illness can enervate even the strongest person.                                | The constant criticism began to enervate her spirit.            |
| Equivocate     | Verb             | Use ambiguous language so as to conceal the truth or avoid committing oneself.                                                   | When asked about the scandal, the politician equivocated.          | He equivocated, refusing to give a direct answer.                                     | Don't equivocate; just tell me what happened.                   |
| Exacerbate     | Verb             | Make (a problem, bad situation, or negative feeling) worse.                                                                      | The new law will exacerbate the problem of homelessness.           | His rude comments only served to exacerbate the tension.                              | Lack of sleep can exacerbate stress.                            |
| Fastidious     | Adjective        | Very attentive to and concerned about accuracy and detail.                                                                       | He was fastidious about his appearance.                            | The fastidious chef insisted on perfect presentation.                                 | Her fastidious nature made her an excellent proofreader.        |
| Fatuous        | Adjective        | Silly and pointless.                                                                                                             | A fatuous remark that made everyone cringe.                        | He made a fatuous attempt to impress her.                                             | The fatuous arguments of the opposition were easily dismissed.  |
| Furtive        | Adjective        | Attempting to avoid notice or attention, typically because of guilt or a belief that discovery would lead to trouble; secretive. | He cast a furtive glance at his watch.                             | She made a furtive movement to hide the letter.                                       | The cat made a furtive escape through the open window.          |
| Garrulous      | Adjective        | Excessively talkative, especially on trivial matters.                                                                            | The garrulous old man told stories for hours.                      | She became garrulous after a few glasses of wine.                                     | His garrulous nature made him a poor secret-keeper.             |
| Harangue       | Noun             | A lengthy and aggressive speech.                                                                                                 | He delivered a long harangue about the dangers of procrastination. | The coach's harangue motivated the team to play harder.                               | She had to endure a harangue from her boss.                     |
| Ignominious    | Adjective        | Deserving or causing public disgrace or shame.                                                                                   | The team suffered an ignominious defeat.                           | He made an ignominious exit from the stage after forgetting his lines.                | The scandal led to his ignominious resignation.                 |
