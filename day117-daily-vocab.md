# Day 117: Daily English Vocabulary

| No. | Word | Part of Speech | Definition | Example 1 | Example 2 | Example 3 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Ameliorate** | Verb | To make (something bad or unsatisfactory) better. | Efforts were made to ameliorate the living conditions of the poor. | The new policies aim to ameliorate the economic disparity. | A warm cup of tea can often ameliorate a sore throat. |
| 2 | **Capricious** | Adjective | Given to sudden and unaccountable changes of mood or behavior. | Her capricious nature made it difficult to predict her reactions. | The weather in the mountains is often capricious. | He was known for his capricious decisions, often changing his mind without warning. |
| 3 | **Deleterious** | Adjective | Causing harm or damage. | Smoking has a deleterious effect on health. | The deleterious impact of pollution on the environment is undeniable. | Stress can have deleterious consequences for mental well-being. |
| 4 | **Eschew** | Verb | Deliberately avoid using; abstain from. | He chose to eschew the traditional path and forge his own career. | Many religions encourage followers to eschew worldly pleasures. | To maintain a healthy diet, one should eschew processed foods. |
| 5 | **Fervent** | Adjective | Having or displaying a passionate intensity. | She was a fervent supporter of human rights. | His fervent prayers were heard by the congregation. | The speaker delivered a fervent appeal for unity. |
| 6 | **Garrulous** | Adjective | Excessively talkative, especially on trivial matters. | The garrulous old man entertained everyone with his endless stories. | She found his garrulous chatter distracting during the meeting. | A garrulous host can make a party lively. |
| 7 | **Hapless** | Adjective | (Especially of a person) unfortunate. | The hapless victim of the scam lost all his savings. | The hapless traveler missed his flight due to a traffic jam. | Despite his best efforts, he remained a hapless romantic. |
| 8 | **Impecunious** | Adjective | Having little or no money; penniless. | He came from an impecunious background but worked hard to succeed. | The artist lived an impecunious life, often struggling to make ends meet. | Despite their impecunious state, they were rich in spirit. |
| 9 | **Juggernaut** | Noun | A huge, powerful, and overwhelming force or institution. | The company became a marketing juggernaut, dominating the industry. | The political party was an unstoppable juggernaut in the elections. | The economy was a juggernaut, growing at an unprecedented rate. |
| 10 | **Knell** | Noun | The sound of a bell, especially when rung solemnly for a death or funeral. | The church bells tolled a mournful knell for the departed. | The announcement of the factory's closure sounded like a death knell for the town. | The knell of doom echoed through the ancient castle. |
| 11 | **Laconic** | Adjective | (Of a person, speech, or style of writing) using very few words. | His laconic reply left her wondering about his true feelings. | The detective was known for his laconic and observant nature. | She appreciated his laconic wit and sharp observations. |
| 12 | **Malediction** | Noun | A magical word or phrase uttered with the intention of bringing about evil or destruction; a curse. | The witch cast a powerful malediction upon the unsuspecting village. | He muttered a malediction under his breath after tripping. | The ancient tomb was said to be protected by a malediction. |
| 13 | **Nefarious** | Adjective | (Typically of an action or activity) wicked or criminal. | The nefarious plot to overthrow the government was uncovered. | He was involved in nefarious activities that led to his arrest. | The villain's nefarious schemes were always thwarted by the hero. |
| 14 | **Obsequious** | Adjective | Obedient or attentive to an excessive or servile degree. | The obsequious waiter hovered around the wealthy diners. | She disliked his obsequious flattery and insincere compliments. | The assistant's obsequious behavior annoyed his colleagues. |
| 15 | **Palliative** | Adjective | (Of a treatment or medicine) relieving pain without dealing with the cause of the condition. | The doctor prescribed palliative care to ease the patient's suffering. | The government offered palliative measures to address the economic crisis. | He sought palliative solutions to his chronic back pain. |
| 16 | **Quixotic** | Adjective | Extremely idealistic; unrealistic and impractical. | His quixotic quest to save the world often led to humorous failures. | She had a quixotic belief in the inherent goodness of humanity. | The young inventor pursued a quixotic dream of perpetual motion. |
| 17 | **Recalcitrant** | Adjective | Having an obstinately uncooperative attitude toward authority or discipline. | The recalcitrant student refused to follow the teacher's instructions. | The government faced a recalcitrant opposition in parliament. | The wild horse was recalcitrant and difficult to train. |
| 18 | **Sycophant** | Noun | A person who acts obsequiously toward someone important in order to gain advantage. | The politician was surrounded by sycophants who praised his every move. | She despised the sycophant who constantly flattered her boss. | He was accused of being a sycophant, always seeking favor from those in power. |
| 19 | **Truculent** | Adjective | Eager or quick to argue or fight; aggressively defiant. | The truculent teenager often got into trouble at school. | His truculent attitude made him unpopular among his peers. | The truculent dictator threatened his neighbors with military action. |
| 20 | **Ubiquitous** | Adjective | Present, appearing, or found everywhere. | Smartphones have become ubiquitous in modern society. | The company aims to make its product ubiquitous in every household. | His ubiquitous presence at social events made him a well-known figure. |
