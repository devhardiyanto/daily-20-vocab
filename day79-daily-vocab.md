# Day 79: Daily English Vocabulary

| Word | Part of Speech | Definition | Example 1 | Example 2 | Example 3 |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Rectify** | Verb | To put right; to correct. | The company promised to **rectify** the error in the customer's billing statement. | It is important to **rectify** any misunderstandings before they lead to conflict. | The technician was called in to **rectify** the fault in the electrical system. |
| **Redoubtable** | Adjective | Formidable, especially as an opponent. | He was a **redoubtable** debater who rarely lost an argument. | The team faced a **redoubtable** opponent in the final round of the tournament. | Despite her small stature, she was a **redoubtable** force in the courtroom. |
| **Refractory** | Adjective | Stubborn or unmanageable; resistant to a process or stimulus. | The **refractory** horse refused to enter the trailer, despite several attempts. | Some types of cancer are **refractory** to conventional chemotherapy treatments. | The teacher struggled to engage the **refractory** students in the classroom activity. |
| **Refulgent** | Adjective | Shining brightly. | The **refulgent** sun rose over the horizon, casting a golden glow on the water. | She looked stunning in her **refulgent** gown, which shimmered under the lights. | The cathedral's **refulgent** stained-glass windows are a sight to behold. |
| **Refute** | Verb | To prove (a statement or theory) to be wrong or false; disprove. | The scientist published a paper to **refute** the claims made by his colleague. | He attempted to **refute** the allegations of misconduct with evidence and witnesses. | The lawyer worked tirelessly to **refute** the prosecution's case against his client. |
| **Regale** | Verb | To entertain or amuse (someone) with talk. | He would often **regale** his friends with stories of his travels around the world. | The grandmother **regale**d her grandchildren with tales of her own childhood. | The host **regale**d the guests with a series of humorous anecdotes and jokes. |
| **Reiterate** | Verb | To say something again or a number of times, typically for emphasis or clarity. | The manager **reiterate**d the importance of meeting the project deadline. | Let me **reiterate** that safety is our top priority in this workplace. | She **reiterate**d her request for a refund after not receiving a response. |
| **Relegate** | Verb | To consign or dismiss to an inferior rank or position. | The team was **relegate**d to the second division after a poor season. | He felt insulted when he was **relegate**d to a minor role in the project. | The old furniture was **relegate**d to the basement to make room for new pieces. |
| **Remiss** | Adjective | Lacking care or attention to duty; negligent. | It would be **remiss** of me not to mention the contribution of our volunteers. | The company was found to be **remiss** in its safety inspections of the equipment. | He felt **remiss** for not calling his parents on their wedding anniversary. |
| **Remonstrate** | Verb | To make a forcefully reproachful protest. | The players began to **remonstrate** with the referee about the controversial decision. | She went to the manager's office to **remonstrate** against the unfair treatment. | He tried to **remonstrate** with his neighbor about the loud music late at night. |
| **Renegade** | Noun | A person who deserts and betrays an organization, country, or set of principles. | He was labeled a **renegade** after joining the rival political party. | The **renegade** soldiers were captured and brought to justice for their actions. | She felt like a **renegade** for questioning the traditional beliefs of her family. |
| **Renege** | Verb | To go back on a promise, undertaking, or contract. | The government was accused of **renege**ing on its promise to lower taxes. | He decided to **renege** on the deal after finding a better offer elsewhere. | It is dishonest to **renege** on an agreement once it has been signed and sealed. |
| **Repast** | Noun | A meal. | After a long day of hiking, they enjoyed a hearty **repast** by the campfire. | The wedding guests were served a sumptuous **repast** in the grand ballroom. | He prepared a simple **repast** of bread, cheese, and fruit for his lunch. |
| **Replete** | Adjective | Filled or well-supplied with something. | The book is **replete** with interesting facts and anecdotes about history. | After the Thanksgiving feast, everyone felt **replete** and satisfied. | The garden was **replete** with colorful flowers and lush green plants. |
| **Reprehensible** | Adjective | Deserving censure or condemnation. | His behavior toward his colleagues was considered utterly **reprehensible**. | The company's lack of concern for the environment is truly **reprehensible**. | It is **reprehensible** to take advantage of vulnerable people for personal gain. |
| **Reprieve** | Noun | A cancellation or postponement of a punishment. | The prisoner was granted a **reprieve** just hours before his scheduled execution. | The rainy weather provided a brief **reprieve** from the intense summer heat. | The company was given a **reprieve** from bankruptcy after securing a new loan. |
| **Reproof** | Noun | An expression of blame or disapproval. | She received a mild **reproof** from her boss for being late to the meeting. | His father's look of **reproof** was enough to make him feel guilty for his actions. | The teacher's **reproof** of the student's behavior was firm but fair. |
| **Repudiate** | Verb | To refuse to accept or be associated with. | The candidate chose to **repudiate** the extremist views of some of his supporters. | The company issued a statement to **repudiate** the rumors of a merger. | He decided to **repudiate** his inheritance and live a simple, independent life. |
| **Rescind** | Verb | To revoke, cancel, or repeal (a law, order, or agreement). | The government decided to **rescind** the controversial new policy after protests. | The company had to **rescind** the job offer after discovering a discrepancy in the resume. | The court's decision to **rescind** the contract was based on evidence of fraud. |
| **Resilient** | Adjective | (Of a person or animal) able to withstand or recover quickly from difficult conditions. | She is a **resilient** individual who has overcome many challenges in her life. | The local economy proved to be **resilient** despite the global financial crisis. | Children are often more **resilient** than adults when it comes to adapting to change. |
