# Day 112: Daily English Vocabulary

| No. | Word | Part of Speech | Meaning | Example 1 | Example 2 | Example 3 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Ubiquitous** | Adjective | Present, appearing, or found everywhere. | Smartphones have become ubiquitous in modern society. | The company's logo is ubiquitous, appearing on billboards and television commercials alike. | Sugar is ubiquitous in processed foods, making it hard to avoid. |
| 2 | **Ephemeral** | Adjective | Lasting for a very short time. | The beauty of a sunset is ephemeral, fading away as quickly as it appears. | Fame in the age of social media is often ephemeral. | She captured the ephemeral moment with her camera before the butterfly flew away. |
| 3 | **Cacophony** | Noun | A harsh, discordant mixture of sounds. | The construction site produced a cacophony of drills, hammers, and shouting. | As the orchestra tuned their instruments, a cacophony filled the concert hall. | The debate devolved into a cacophony of overlapping voices and angry accusations. |
| 4 | **Mellifluous** | Adjective | (Of a voice or words) sweet or musical; pleasant to hear. | The singer's mellifluous voice captivated the entire audience. | He spoke in a mellifluous tone that immediately put everyone at ease. | The mellifluous sound of the cello echoed through the empty church. |
| 5 | **Serendipity** | Noun | The occurrence and development of events by chance in a happy or beneficial way. | Finding a twenty-dollar bill in an old coat pocket was a moment of pure serendipity. | Their meeting at the coffee shop was a stroke of serendipity that led to a lifelong friendship. | Scientific discoveries are sometimes the result of serendipity rather than planned experiments. |
| 6 | **Sycophant** | Noun | A person who acts obsequiously toward someone important in order to gain advantage. | The CEO was surrounded by sycophants who never dared to disagree with him. | She recognized him as a sycophant who only praised her to get a promotion. | A true leader values honest feedback over the empty flattery of sycophants. |
| 7 | **Obfuscate** | Verb | Render obscure, unclear, or unintelligible. | The politician tried to obfuscate the truth with long, confusing statements. | The legal jargon in the contract seemed designed to obfuscate its true meaning. | Instead of answering the question directly, he chose to obfuscate the issue. |
| 8 | **Alacrity** | Noun | Brisk and cheerful readiness. | She accepted the invitation to the party with alacrity. | The waiter responded to our requests with surprising alacrity. | Having finished his chores, he ran out to play with alacrity. |
| 9 | **Pernicious** | Adjective | Having a harmful effect, especially in a gradual or subtle way. | The pernicious effects of the rumor spread quickly through the small town. | Prolonged exposure to the sun can have a pernicious impact on your skin. | The pernicious weed slowly choked the life out of the other plants in the garden. |
| 10 | **Fastidious** | Adjective | Very attentive to and concerned about accuracy and detail. | He is fastidious about keeping his desk perfectly organized. | The fastidious editor caught every single typo in the manuscript. | She was so fastidious about her appearance that she spent hours getting ready. |
| 11 | **Inexorable** | Adjective | Impossible to stop or prevent. | The inexorable advance of technology has changed the way we live. | They watched the inexorable approach of the storm clouds with growing dread. | The inexorable march of time eventually catches up with everyone. |
| 12 | **Lugubrious** | Adjective | Looking or sounding sad and dismal. | The dog's lugubrious expression made everyone want to pet him. | He told a lugubrious tale of lost love and missed opportunities. | The lugubrious tolling of the church bell signaled the end of the funeral. |
| 13 | **Magnanimous** | Adjective | Generous or forgiving, especially toward a rival or less powerful person. | The winning team was magnanimous in victory, praising their opponents' effort. | It was magnanimous of her to forgive him after such a serious betrayal. | The magnanimous king pardoned the rebels who had plotted against him. |
| 14 | **Nefarious** | Adjective | (Typically of an action or activity) wicked or criminal. | The villain's nefarious plot to take over the city was foiled by the hero. | They discovered a nefarious scheme to embezzle funds from the charity. | The dark alley was known as a gathering place for nefarious characters. |
| 15 | **Ostentatious** | Adjective | Characterized by vulgar or pretentious display; designed to impress or attract notice. | He drove an ostentatious sports car that turned heads wherever he went. | The billionaire's ostentatious mansion featured gold-plated fixtures and marble floors. | Her ostentatious jewelry was entirely inappropriate for the solemn occasion. |
| 16 | **Pedantic** | Adjective | Excessively concerned with minor details and rules or with displaying academic learning. | The professor's pedantic lectures often put his students to sleep. | He was so pedantic about grammar that he corrected people in casual conversation. | The pedantic review focused on trivial errors rather than the book's overall message. |
| 17 | **Quixotic** | Adjective | Exceedingly idealistic; unrealistic and impractical. | His quixotic quest to end world hunger single-handedly was admirable but doomed. | The startup's quixotic business model failed to attract any serious investors. | She had a quixotic belief that everyone was inherently good and honest. |
| 18 | **Recalcitrant** | Adjective | Having an obstinately uncooperative attitude toward authority or discipline. | The recalcitrant teenager refused to follow any of the house rules. | The manager struggled to deal with a recalcitrant employee who ignored instructions. | The recalcitrant horse refused to enter the trailer despite the trainer's efforts. |
| 19 | **Sagacious** | Adjective | Having or showing keen mental discernment and good judgment; shrewd. | The sagacious old man offered advice that proved to be invaluable. | Her sagacious investments allowed her to retire comfortably at a young age. | The committee made a sagacious decision to delay the project until more data was available. |
| 20 | **Trepidation** | Noun | A feeling of fear or agitation about something that may happen. | He opened the letter from the tax office with a sense of trepidation. | She approached the stage with trepidation, her hands shaking slightly. | Despite his trepidation, he decided to take the leap and start his own business. |
